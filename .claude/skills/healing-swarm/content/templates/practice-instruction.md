## Element Guidelines

### Overview

**Purpose:** Help users understand what they're about to do and why

**Keep it:**
- Brief (2-3 sentences)
- Accessible (no jargon)
- Honest about benefits

**Example:**
```markdown
## Overview

This acupressure practice targets a point traditionally used to
support healing in the hand and upper body. By applying gentle
pressure while breathing slowly, you may help reduce tension and
support circulation. Many people find it calming and grounding.
```

### Before You Begin

**Purpose:** Prepare the user and ensure safety

**Sections:**
- **You'll Need:** Materials, environment, time
- **Best Times:** Optimal timing for the practice
- **Cautions:** Who should be careful or skip

**Example:**
```markdown
## Before You Begin

### You'll Need
- A quiet space (5-10 minutes)
- Comfortable seating
- Your hands (both if possible, but can be done with one)

### Best Times to Practice
- Morning upon waking
- Before stressful situations
- As part of a healing routine

### Cautions
- **Pregnancy:** Avoid this point during pregnancy, as it is
  traditionally contraindicated
- **Recent hand surgery:** Wait until your doctor clears you
  for self-massage
- **Open wounds:** Avoid pressing on or near broken skin

*If you're uncertain whether this practice is right for you,
consult your healthcare provider.*
```

### Step Instructions

**Purpose:** Guide the user through each action clearly

**Structure each step:**
1. **What you'll do** - Brief statement of the action
2. **How to do it** - Numbered sub-steps
3. **What you might notice** - Sensory feedback
4. **Duration** - How long to maintain

**Writing guidelines:**
- One action per step
- Use active, present tense
- Be specific about positioning
- Include sensory cues for feedback
- Keep language simple

**Example:**
```markdown
### Step 2: Apply Pressure

**What you'll do:**
Use your thumb to press gently on the acupoint.

**How to do it:**
1. Place the pad of your thumb (not the nail) on the point
2. Apply gentle pressure—enough to feel it, not enough to hurt
3. You can use a small circular motion, or simply hold steady
4. Breathe slowly as you maintain the pressure

**What you might notice:**
- A dull, achy sensation (this is normal and called "de qi")
- Warmth in the area
- Relaxation spreading up your arm
- Your breathing naturally slowing

*Duration: 1-3 minutes per point*
```

### Sensory Cues

**Purpose:** Help users know they're doing it right

**Types of cues:**
- What they might feel (warmth, tingling, pressure)
- What they might see (color changes, subtle movement)
- What they might notice internally (relaxation, calm)

**Language:**
- "You might notice..." (not "you will feel")
- "Some people experience..." (not everyone has same response)
- "It's also normal to not feel much at first"

**Example:**
```markdown
**What you might notice:**

You may feel:
- A subtle ache or fullness at the point (traditional sign of connection)
- Warmth spreading from the point
- A general sense of relaxation

You may also feel very little—this is normal, especially when beginning.
The practice can work even without dramatic sensations. With practice,
awareness often increases.
```

### Variations

**Purpose:** Make the practice accessible to all

**Always include:**
- Limited mobility adaptation
- Lower intensity option
- Advanced variation (optional)
- Situational adaptations

**Example:**
```markdown
## Variations

### If you have limited mobility
If you cannot easily reach the point, you may:
- Use a tennis ball pressed between hands
- Ask a partner to apply the pressure
- Visualize the point being stimulated (research suggests this can help)

### If this feels too intense
- Reduce pressure significantly—even light touch can be effective
- Shorten duration to 30 seconds
- Skip the circular motion; simple holding is fine

### To deepen the practice
- Combine with healing visualization
- Add an invocation or prayer
- Practice for longer duration (up to 5 minutes)
- Coordinate with specific breathing patterns

### For travel or work
- Can be done discreetly under a table
- Eyes open version available
- 30-second "mini practice" for quick reset
```

### Troubleshooting

**Purpose:** Address common issues

**Format:** Problem → Solution table

**Common issues to address:**
- Can't find the point
- Feels too intense / feels like nothing
- Can't maintain position
- Mind wanders
- Uncertain if doing it right

**Example:**
```markdown
## Troubleshooting

| Issue | Suggestion |
|-------|------------|
| Can't find the exact point | The area around the point is also beneficial. Don't worry about precision—explore gently until you find a tender or "active" spot |
| It hurts | Reduce pressure immediately. Pain is not productive. Gentle pressure is sufficient |
| I don't feel anything | This is common, especially at first. The practice can work without dramatic sensation. Continue with gentle, curious attention |
| My mind keeps wandering | This is normal. When you notice, gently return attention to the point. The wandering and returning IS the practice |
| I'm not sure I'm doing it right | If you're applying gentle pressure to roughly the right area and breathing, you're doing it right. Precision comes with practice |
```

### Building a Practice

**Purpose:** Guide sustained engagement

**Include:**
- Recommended frequency
- Realistic timeline for effects
- How to progress

**Example:**
```markdown
## Building a Practice

**Frequency:** Daily practice is ideal, but even 2-3 times per week
can be beneficial. Consistency matters more than duration.

**Duration:** Allow 2-3 weeks of regular practice before evaluating.
Some people notice effects immediately; for others, it's gradual.

**Progression:**
- Week 1-2: Focus on finding the point and basic technique
- Week 3-4: Begin adding breathing coordination
- Month 2+: Explore combining with visualization or prayer
- Ongoing: Deepen awareness and duration as feels right
```

## Language Guidelines

### Action Verbs

| Use These | Avoid These |
|-----------|-------------|
| Press, apply, place | Push, force, dig |
| Rest, allow, notice | Make yourself, force yourself |
| Breathe, release, soften | Relax (too vague) |
| Find, locate, explore | You should, you must |
| May, might, can | Will definitely, always |

### Body Language

| Use These | Avoid These |
|-----------|-------------|
| "The area" | "Your bad hand" |
| "The part that needs attention" | "The injured part" |
| "Your hand" | "Your affected limb" |
| "The point" | "The pressure point" (sounds intense) |

### Certainty Level

| Use These | Avoid These |
|-----------|-------------|
| "May help with" | "Will fix" |
| "Traditionally used for" | "Cures" |
| "Some people find" | "Everyone experiences" |
| "Research suggests" | "Science proves" |

---

## Accessibility Considerations

### Physical Accessibility

- Offer alternatives for each physical requirement
- Don't assume two-handed capability
- Account for limited grip strength
- Consider fatigue and pain

### Cognitive Accessibility

- Short, clear sentences
- One instruction per step
- Bullet points for sub-steps
- Visual diagrams when helpful

### Sensory Accessibility

- Don't rely solely on visual cues
- Describe what things feel like
- Offer audio/tactile alternatives
- Clear text descriptions of positions

## Example: Complete Practice Instruction

```markdown
# Acupressure for Hand Healing: LI-4 (Hegu)

**Tradition:** Traditional Chinese Medicine
**Duration:** 5-10 minutes
**Difficulty:** Beginner
**Best for:** Tension relief, supporting circulation in the hand and arm

---

## Overview

LI-4, also called Hegu ("Joining Valley"), is one of the most
important acupressure points in Traditional Chinese Medicine.
Located in the webbing between thumb and index finger, it has
been used for thousands of years to address conditions of the
hand, arm, head, and face. This practice guides you through
self-acupressure on this point.

---

## Before You Begin

### You'll Need
- 5-10 minutes of uninterrupted time
- Comfortable seating (or can be done anywhere)
- Access to at least one hand

### Best Times to Practice
- Morning, as part of a healing routine
- During breaks when you notice hand tension
- Evening, for relaxation and circulation support

### Cautions
- **Pregnancy:** Traditional sources advise avoiding LI-4 during
  pregnancy, as it is believed to induce labor
- **Blood disorders:** If you have bleeding disorders or are on
  blood thinners, use only very light pressure
- **Local injury:** Avoid pressing directly on wounds, surgical
  sites, or areas of acute inflammation

*If uncertain, consult your healthcare provider before practicing.*

---

## The Practice

### Step 1: Find the Point

**What you'll do:**
Locate LI-4 on your hand.

**How to do it:**
1. Look at the back of your hand (palm facing down)
2. Find the webbing between your thumb and index finger
3. Squeeze your thumb against your index finger—you'll see a small
   mound of muscle
4. The point is at the highest spot of this mound
5. Release the squeeze and place your other thumb on this spot

**What you might notice:**
When you find the right spot, it often feels slightly tender or
"active"—more sensitive than the surrounding area. This is a good
sign you've found it.

*Take your time with this step. The point doesn't have to be exact.*

---

### Step 2: Apply Gentle Pressure

**What you'll do:**
Press on the point with your thumb while breathing slowly.

**How to do it:**
1. Place the pad of your thumb (not the tip or nail) on the point
2. Apply steady, gentle pressure—firm enough to feel, not enough
   to cause sharp pain
3. Hold the pressure steady, or use a small circular motion
4. Breathe slowly and naturally as you maintain pressure

**What you might notice:**
- A dull, achy sensation that may radiate up your arm
- Warmth in the area
- A sense of your breathing naturally slowing
- Relaxation in your hand, arm, or shoulders

*Duration: 1-3 minutes*

---

### Step 3: Breathe with the Point

**What you'll do:**
Coordinate your breathing with the practice.

**How to do it:**
1. As you inhale slowly, imagine breathing into the point
2. As you exhale, imagine tension releasing from your hand
3. Continue this gentle rhythm without forcing
4. Let each exhale be slightly longer than the inhale

**What you might notice:**
- Deepening relaxation
- Sense of energy or warmth moving through your hand
- Mind becoming quieter
- Any pain in the hand may shift or soften

*Continue for 1-3 minutes*

---

### Step 4: Switch Hands (If Applicable)

**What you'll do:**
Repeat on your other hand if possible.

**How to do it:**
1. Gently release the pressure
2. Take a few normal breaths
3. Find LI-4 on your other hand
4. Repeat Steps 2-3

**Note:** If you're working with an injured hand, you may choose to:
- Only work on the uninjured hand (may still help the other)
- Very gently work on the injured side if it's comfortable
- Skip this step entirely

*Duration: 1-3 minutes per hand*

---

### Completing the Practice

**What you'll do:**
Close the practice mindfully.

**How to do it:**
1. Release all pressure
2. Rest your hands gently in your lap
3. Take three slow, easy breaths
4. Notice how your hands feel now
5. Gently move your fingers if you like
6. Open your eyes when ready

*Take a moment before moving on with your day.*

---

## Variations

### If you have limited mobility in one hand
- Practice only on the accessible hand
- Benefit can extend throughout the body
- Consider using a massage tool or asking a partner to help

### If this feels too intense
- Reduce pressure significantly—light touch can be effective
- Shorten duration to 30-60 seconds
- Skip the circular motion; simple holding is gentler

### To deepen the practice
- Add a healing visualization (imagine green healing light)
- Include a prayer or invocation
- Extend duration to 5 minutes per hand
- Practice in a meditative state

### For quick relief (travel, work)
- 30-second version: quick press and hold with 3 deep breaths
- Can be done discreetly under a desk
- Eyes open is fine

---

## Troubleshooting

| Issue | Suggestion |
|-------|------------|
| Can't find the exact point | Explore the general area gently. The zone around the point is also beneficial. With practice, you'll find the most responsive spot |
| It's too sensitive | Reduce pressure immediately. If still too sensitive, try working nearby or on the other hand |
| I don't feel anything | Normal, especially at first. Continue with curious attention. Effects can be subtle or cumulative |
| My hand cramps | Release pressure and rest. Try using a massage tool instead of your thumb, or work for shorter periods |
| Unsure I'm doing it right | If you're pressing gently on the general area and breathing, you're doing it right. Precision develops with practice |

---

## Building a Practice

**Frequency:** Daily practice is ideal. Even 2-3 times per week
offers benefits. Consistency matters more than perfection.

**Timeline:** Allow 2-4 weeks of regular practice to notice cumulative
effects. Some people feel immediate relief; for others, benefits
build gradually.

**Progression:**
- Week 1-2: Focus on locating the point and comfortable pressure
- Week 3-4: Add breathing coordination
- Month 2+: Explore combining with visualization or other practices
- Ongoing: Deepen awareness; some practitioners work this point
  for years with continuing benefit

---

*This practice is adapted from Traditional Chinese Medicine, where
LI-4 has been used for millennia. It is documented in classical texts
including the Huangdi Neijing (Yellow Emperor's Inner Canon) and
Zhenjiu Dacheng (Great Compendium of Acupuncture and Moxibustion).*

*This practice complements but does not replace medical care.
Consult your healthcare provider before beginning any new health
practice, especially if you have a medical condition affecting
your hands.*
```
