# Micro-Practice Design Template

> Template for designing a small daily endo-technology practice that
> reclaims one innate capacity (inquiry phase 4). An endo-technology is an
> inner practice that uses no external device. The practice is short (5 to
> 10 minutes), needs no equipment, and fits wherever the person already is.
> Serves the hyperhumanism-inquiry skill.

---

```markdown

**Phase:** 4 of 4, Practice Design
**Capacity reclaimed:** [The one capacity chosen in phase 3]
**Duration:** [5 to 10 minutes]
**Frequency:** [Daily or near-daily]
**Equipment:** None required
**Where:** [Wherever you already are]

## What This Practice Is For

[2-3 sentences. This is a small, repeatable practice that exercises one
capacity you chose to reconnect with. It is an addition to your day, not a
subtraction from your technology. It asks for a few minutes, no tools, and
no special conditions.]

> **Evidence note:** Hyperhumanism is a theoretical framework from Carl
> Hayden Smith. The idea that a capacity stays available when it is
> exercised regularly is supported by general findings on attention and
> skill, and by capacity-specific research where it exists (noted below if
> relevant). Research suggests that short, consistent practice matters more
> than long, occasional sessions. This design makes no health claim and is
> not a treatment.

---

## The Endo-Technology Principle

This practice uses only your own attention, breath, body, and awareness. No
app, wearable, or external device is part of it. That is what makes it an
endo-technology: the capacity lives in you, and the practice exercises it
directly. No substances of any kind are involved.

- [Duration] of ordinary time, no special setup
- A moment in your day where this can fit (see the anchor below)
- Permission to keep it small; a few minutes counts

## Anchoring It in Your Day

A micro-practice survives when it attaches to something you already do.

- **When:** [An existing daily moment, for example after your first coffee]
- **Where:** [Wherever that moment happens]
- **Cue:** [The everyday trigger that reminds you]
- **If you miss a day:** Begin again the next day. Missing is expected and
  is not failure.

---

## If It Is Not Working

- **Too long:** Cut it to two or three minutes. Short is fine.
- **Keep forgetting:** Attach it to a more reliable daily cue.
- **Feels like a chore:** Make it smaller and gentler, or change the moment.
- **Nothing shifts:** Effects are often subtle and cumulative. Consistency
  matters more than intensity.

_This practice complements but does not replace medical or psychological
care._
```

## Element Guidelines

### What This Practice Is For

**Purpose:** Frame the practice as a small, sustainable addition

**Key principles:**

- Reclamation is addition, never subtraction from technology
- One capacity, chosen in phase 3, is the whole focus
- Attribute Smith and the hyperhumanism framework

### The Endo-Technology Principle

**Purpose:** Keep the practice inner and tool-free

**Required:**

- No app, wearable, or external device is part of the practice
- No substances of any kind (the pharmaco-exclusion)
- The capacity is exercised directly, in the person

### The Practice Steps

**Purpose:** Make the practice concrete and doable today

**Key principles:**

- Five to ten minutes, no equipment, no special conditions
- One instruction per step, body or attention anchored
- Normalized experiences, including little or nothing
- Low-key by design, a daily habit rather than a peak state

### Anchoring and Tracking

**Purpose:** Help the practice survive contact with real life

**Key principles:**

- Attach the practice to an existing daily moment and cue
- Missing a day is expected and framed without shame
- Tracking is light and optional, never a task to perfect

### Evidence Framing

**Purpose:** Keep claims honest

**Required:**

- Hyperhumanism labeled as a theoretical framework
- General and capacity-specific research cited honestly
- "Research suggests" language, no "science proves"
- No health claim and no treatment framing

---

## Accessibility Guidelines

### Physical Accessibility

- Doable seated, standing, lying down, or while moving gently
- No equipment and no special space
- Adaptable to any mobility level

### Sensory Accessibility

- Steps described so they do not depend on a single sense
- A version that leans on the person's strongest sense
- Screen-reader friendly tracking layout

### Cognitive Accessibility

- One instruction per step
- Short, predictable structure
- A single, concrete daily cue rather than a schedule

### Psychological Safety

- Permission to keep it small and to miss days
- No pressure toward a particular experience
- The practice is private to the person
- Not therapy and not a substitute for care
