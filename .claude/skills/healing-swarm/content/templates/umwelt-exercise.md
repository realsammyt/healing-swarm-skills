# Umwelt Exercise Template

> Template for creating sensory augmentation and perceiving-as-other exercises
> based on von Uexkull's umwelt theory (1934) and Carl Hayden Smith's umwelt
> hacking concept. Includes progressive levels, dissociation safety, and
> return-to-default protocol.

---

## Overview

[2-3 sentences describing this exercise, its basis in umwelt
theory, and its purpose. Include evidence level and safety note.]

> **Evidence note:** Umwelt theory (von Uexkull 1934) proposes
> that each organism inhabits a unique perceptual world shaped
> by its sensory capacities. Embodied cognition research suggests
> that directed attention to different senses can shift perceptual
> experience. The practice of "umwelt hacking" — deliberately
> shifting perceptual frames — is a theoretical framework with
> preliminary research support.

## Safety — Read Before Every Session

### For All Levels

- You can stop at any point — there is no obligation to continue
- If you feel uncomfortable, dizzy, or distressed, open your eyes
  and feel your feet on the floor
- This is exploration, not endurance

### For Sensory Foregrounding (Level 2)

- If sustained attention on a single sense becomes uncomfortable,
  return to multi-sensory awareness
- Brief grounding between each sense focus

### For Perceiving-as-Other (Level 3)

**CONTRAINDICATED — DO NOT PRACTICE IF YOU HAVE:**

- Active dissociative disorder (DID)
- Active depersonalization or derealization
- Active psychosis or psychotic features
- Severe PTSD with dissociative episodes
- Recent psychiatric hospitalization (within 3 months)

**Before beginning, answer these screening questions:**

1. Have you ever been diagnosed with a dissociative disorder?
2. Do you sometimes feel disconnected from your body or that
   things aren't real?
3. Have you experienced psychosis or been prescribed
   antipsychotic medication?
4. Do you have PTSD with dissociative episodes?

**If ANY answer is yes → Do NOT proceed with Level 3.**
Choose Level 1 (Sensory Inventory) or Level 2 (Sensory
Foregrounding) instead.

### Important Framing

All perceiving-as-other content is **imagination**, not actual
perceptual access to another organism's experience. We use
"imagine" language throughout. You will remain yourself
throughout the entire practice.

_If you have any mental health conditions, consult your healthcare
provider before beginning this practice._

## Before You Begin

### You'll Need

- [Duration] of uninterrupted time
- A comfortable seated position (can also be done standing or lying)
- A space where you can focus without interruption
- Optional: journal for sensory mapping

### Preparation

1. Sit comfortably — spine supported, body relaxed
2. Close your eyes or soften your gaze
3. Take 3 natural breaths
4. Notice that you are here, in this body, in this place

## The Practice

### [LEVEL 1: SENSORY INVENTORY]

**What you'll do:**
Map your current sensory landscape — what are you sensing right now?

**How to do it:**

1. **Visual field** (1-2 min)
   - Notice your central vision — what's directly ahead?
   - Now notice peripheral vision — what's at the edges?
   - How far does your visual field extend?
   - If eyes are closed: notice what you "see" (patterns, darkness, light)

2. **Auditory landscape** (1-2 min)
   - What's the closest sound you can hear?
   - What's the farthest sound?
   - Are there layers — foreground sounds and background sounds?
   - Notice a sound you weren't aware of until now

3. **Tactile sensations** (1-2 min)
   - Where does clothing touch skin?
   - Where does air touch exposed skin?
   - What surfaces are you in contact with?
   - What's the temperature of the air on your face?

4. **Proprioception** (1-2 min)
   - Without looking: where are your hands?
   - Where are your feet?
   - What angle is your spine at?
   - Can you feel your whole body at once?

5. **Interoception** (1-2 min)
   - Can you feel your heartbeat without touching?
   - Can you feel your breath moving in your chest?
   - What temperature are you? Warm? Cool?
   - Is there hunger? Thirst? Fullness?

6. **Thermoception** (1-2 min)
   - Which part of your body is warmest?
   - Which is coolest?
   - Where is there a gradient (warm to cool)?

7. **Vestibular** (1 min)
   - Which way is gravity pulling you?
   - Are you tilted? Upright? Leaning?
   - How do you know which way is "up"?

**What you might notice:**

- Senses you had completely forgotten about
- How much is happening that you normally filter out
- Some senses may feel vivid, others blank — both fine
- A sense of wonder at your own perceptual equipment

---

### [LEVEL 2: SENSORY FOREGROUNDING]

_Prerequisite: Completed Level 1 at least once_

**What you'll do:**
Take a sense that usually operates in the background and bring
it to the foreground of your attention.

**How to do it:**

**Sense Focus 1: Proprioception (3-5 min)**

1. Close your eyes
2. Bring all your attention to body position
3. Without moving, feel where every part of your body is in space
4. Hold this attention — when it drifts, return to body position
5. After 3-5 minutes: what changed? Did the sense sharpen?

_Grounding (1 min): Open eyes. Feel feet. Normal breaths._

**Sense Focus 2: Interoception (3-5 min)**

1. Close your eyes
2. Bring all your attention inside — heartbeat, breath, temperature
3. Can you feel your pulse? Your digestion? Your breath deep in lungs?
4. Hold attention on internal sensations — when it drifts, return
5. After 3-5 minutes: what did you discover?

_Grounding (1 min): Open eyes. Feel feet. Normal breaths._

**Sense Focus 3: Thermoception (3-5 min)**

1. Close your eyes
2. Bring all attention to temperature
3. Map the thermal landscape of your body — warm and cool zones
4. Notice air temperature on exposed skin vs. covered areas
5. After 3-5 minutes: what surprised you?

_Grounding (1 min): Open eyes. Feel feet. Normal breaths._

**What you might notice:**

- When one sense is foregrounded, others may dim temporarily
- Background senses can be remarkably rich when attended to
- The effort of sustained attention itself is interesting
- Your default sensory priorities become visible

---

### [LEVEL 3: PERCEIVING-AS-OTHER]

_Prerequisite: Completed Levels 1 and 2. Full Tier 2 screening
required. Contraindications reviewed above._

**What you'll do:**
Using imagination, explore what sensing might be like for another
organism. You will remain yourself throughout — this is imaginative
exploration, not identity shifting.

#### Grounding Anchor (2 minutes)

1. Feel your feet on the floor
2. Notice the weight of your body
3. Say your name silently to yourself
4. Take 3 normal breaths
5. You are here. You are yourself.

#### Organism Introduction (2 minutes)

**[Organism: e.g., Bat]**

[Factual description of the organism's sensory world:]

[Example for bat:]

> Bats navigate primarily through echolocation — emitting
> ultrasonic calls and reading the returning echoes to build a
> three-dimensional map of their surroundings. They "see" with
> sound. Each echo carries information about distance, shape,
> texture, and movement. This happens many times per second,
> creating a continuously updated sonic image of the world.

_This is what science tells us. What follows is an imaginative
exercise — we cannot actually experience echolocation._

#### Guided Imagination — Segment 1 (MAX 5 minutes)

**What you'll do:**
Imagine — staying grounded in your own body — what it might
be like if your primary sense were [organism's primary sense].

**How to do it:**

1. Close your eyes
2. **Imagine** that vision fades, and in its place, you sense
   the space around you through [organism's sense]
3. [Specific imagination prompts based on organism sensory data]
4. [Guide attention through different aspects of the organism's
   perceptual world]
5. Remember: you are imagining. You are yourself. This is
   exploration.

**What you might notice:**

- A felt shift in how you relate to space
- New appreciation for senses you take for granted
- The effort of imagining a different perceptual world
- Nothing dramatic — and that's fine

_If you feel uncomfortable, disconnected, or confused about
your own identity, open your eyes immediately and proceed
to the grounding interval._

#### Grounding Interval (2 minutes)

1. Feel your feet on the floor
2. Notice the weight of your body
3. Name one thing you can hear in the room
4. Take two normal breaths
5. Open your eyes — look at your hands
6. You are here. You are yourself.

#### Guided Imagination — Segment 2 (MAX 5 minutes)

_[Optional — only if comfortable after Segment 1]_

[Different aspect of the organism's perceptual world]

#### Return-to-Default Protocol (3 minutes) — MANDATORY

**This protocol is REQUIRED. Do not skip it.**

1. **Name 5 things you can see** — look around the room, name
   them aloud or silently: "I see a chair, a window, a book..."
2. **Say your full name aloud** — "[Your Name]"
3. **Touch a solid surface and describe its texture** — "This
   table is smooth and cool"
4. **State today's date and your location** — "It is [date]
   and I am in [location]"
5. **Take 3 normal breaths before standing**

_You should feel fully yourself and fully present before
ending the practice. If not, repeat the protocol._

---

### [LEVEL 4: INTEGRATION]

**What you'll do:**
Reflect on what the practice revealed about your own perception.

**How to do it:**

1. Compare your sensory map before and after:
   - Which senses feel more vivid now?
   - Which feel the same?
   - Has anything shifted in how you perceive the space?

2. Reflect:
   - What did you discover about your default umwelt?
   - What senses do you normally ignore?
   - What surprised you about the exercise?
   - [If Level 3: What did imagining another perceptual world
     reveal about your own?]

3. Optional journal prompts:
   - "My default perceptual world emphasizes **_ and
     backgrounds _**"
   - "Today I noticed \_\_\_ for the first time"
   - "If I had to describe my umwelt to an alien, I would
     say \_\_\_"

## Evidence Box

> **Umwelt theory:** Jakob von Uexkull (1934) proposed that each
> organism inhabits a unique perceptual world — its umwelt — shaped
> by its sensory and motor capacities. This is established biology.
>
> **Embodied cognition:** Research suggests that bodily states and
> sensory attention influence cognitive processing (Varela et al.
> 1991, Clark 1997). The evidence base is growing but varies by
> specific claim.
>
> **Umwelt hacking:** Carl Hayden Smith's concept of deliberately
> shifting perceptual frames through practice is a theoretical
> framework. The individual sensory exercises draw on attention
> research and interoceptive awareness studies.
>
> **Perceiving-as-other:** This is an imaginative exercise inspired
> by Thomas Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974). We
> cannot actually access another organism's experience. The value
> is in what the imagination exercise reveals about our own
> perceptual defaults.

## Variations

### If perceiving-as-other feels too intense

- Stay with Level 1 or Level 2 — these are profound in themselves
- Background senses are fascinating when foregrounded
- No need to attempt Level 3 until (if) you feel ready

### For different organisms

- **Forest:** Distributed, networked sensing through soil and air
- **Mycelium:** Chemical gradient detection across vast networks
- **Octopus:** Distributed intelligence, taste-by-touch
- **Bat:** Echolocation — building space from sound
- **Whale:** Low-frequency ocean communication across distances

### For different situations

- **Outdoors:** Expanded sensory inventory (wind, sun, ground texture)
- **Urban:** City soundscape as auditory foregrounding
- **Before creative work:** Foregrounding proprioception or interoception

### For accessibility

- **Limited mobility:** Focus on available senses — no movement required
- **Vision impaired:** Skip visual inventory, extend auditory and tactile
- **Hearing impaired:** Skip auditory, extend tactile and proprioceptive
- All exercises can be done seated or lying down

---

## Troubleshooting

| Issue                               | Suggestion                                                                           |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Can't feel a particular sense       | Skip it — not all senses are equally accessible. Move to the next                    |
| Sustained attention is hard         | Normal — return gently when attention drifts. The returning IS practice              |
| Feeling disconnected during Level 3 | Open eyes immediately. Run return-to-default protocol. Choose Level 1 or 2 next time |
| Nothing feels different             | Subtle shifts are more common than dramatic ones. The noticing is the practice       |
| Fell asleep                         | Try seated with eyes open. Shorter duration. Earlier in the day                      |
| Feeling overwhelmed                 | Open eyes, feet on floor, 3 breaths. Return to Level 1                               |

## Building a Practice

**Frequency:** 2-3 times per week for sensory inventory/foregrounding.
Level 3 no more than once per week.

**Timeline:** Allow 2 weeks of Level 1/2 before attempting Level 3.
Sensory awareness deepens gradually with practice.

**Progression:**

- Week 1-2: Sensory Inventory (Level 1) — explore your sensorium
- Week 3-4: Sensory Foregrounding (Level 2) — discover background senses
- Month 2+: Perceiving-as-Other (Level 3) if desired and appropriate
- Ongoing: Deepen sensory awareness, try different organisms

---

_This practice is inspired by Jakob von Uexkull's umwelt theory
(1934), Thomas Nagel's philosophical inquiry into subjective
experience (1974), and Carl Hayden Smith's umwelt hacking concept
(Museum of Consciousness). The sensory exercises draw on attention
research and embodied cognition. Perceiving-as-other is an
imagination exercise, not actual perceptual access._

_This practice complements but does not replace medical or
psychological care. If you have a history of dissociation, psychosis,
or related conditions, consult a mental health professional before
attempting Level 3._

## Element Guidelines

### Safety Section

**Purpose:** Screen for dissociation risk and ensure safe practice

**Requirements:**

- Level-specific safety (escalating with level)
- Contraindication list for Level 3 (explicit, clear)
- Screening questions for Level 3
- "Imagine" framing stated explicitly
- Permission to stop at every level
- Medical disclaimer

**For Level 3, safety is NON-NEGOTIABLE.** Screening, grounding
intervals, and return-to-default protocol must all be present.

### Sensory Inventory (Level 1)

**Purpose:** Map the practitioner's current perceptual landscape

**Key principles:**

- Cover all seven sense categories
- One instruction per step
- Curious, exploratory tone
- No "right" experiences
- Safe for all — no screening required

### Sensory Foregrounding (Level 2)

**Purpose:** Shift background senses to foreground attention

**Key principles:**

- Sustained attention on one sense at a time
- Grounding between each sense focus
- Notice what changes when attention shifts
- Standard mental health screening recommended

### Perceiving-as-Other (Level 3)

**Purpose:** Imaginative exploration of other organisms' perceptual worlds

**CRITICAL principles:**

- ALWAYS "imagine" language — NEVER "become" or "you are"
- Maximum 5-minute segments
- Grounding between every segment
- Return-to-default protocol MANDATORY at end
- Full Tier 2 dissociation screening REQUIRED
- Contraindications listed and enforced

### Return-to-Default Protocol

**Purpose:** Ensure practitioner returns fully to their own
identity and perceptual baseline

**The 5 steps are MANDATORY and COMPLETE:**

1. Name 5 things you can see
2. Say your full name aloud
3. Touch a solid surface and describe its texture
4. State today's date and location
5. Take 3 normal breaths before standing

**This protocol is NEVER abbreviated, softened, or made optional.**

## Accessibility Guidelines

### Physical Accessibility

- All exercises can be done seated or lying down
- No physical movement required
- Adapted for any mobility level

### Sensory Accessibility

- Skip unavailable senses (vision, hearing)
- Extend available senses to compensate
- Written alternative for verbalization steps
- Multiple entry points — start with strongest sense

### Cognitive Accessibility

- Clear level labels
- One instruction per step
- Predictable structure
- Simple language throughout

### Psychological Safety

- Explicit screening for Level 3
- Permission to stop at every level
- Grounding available at every moment
- Identity stability emphasized
- "Imagine" framing throughout
- Not a substitute for therapy
