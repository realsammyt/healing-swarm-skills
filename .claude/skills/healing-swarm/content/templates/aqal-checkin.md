## Structure

### Opening — Grounding (2-3 min)

```
Begin by settling into your seat.
Feel the surface beneath you — its texture, temperature, solidity.
Take three slow breaths, letting each exhale be slightly longer than the inhale.

There is nothing to fix, solve, or achieve in this practice.
You are simply looking at a situation from four different angles.
Whatever you find is useful information.
```

### Upper-Left Quadrant — I (Subjective) (3-5 min)

```
Turn your attention inward.

What are you feeling right now about [SITUATION]?
What emotions are present — even quietly, in the background?
What thoughts keep returning?
What meaning are you making of this?

If nothing particular arises, that is perfectly fine.
Just notice the quality of your inner experience right now.
```

### Upper-Right Quadrant — It (Objective) (3-5 min)

```
Now shift to what is observable from the outside.

What are you actually doing about [SITUATION]?
What behaviors have you shown? What actions have you taken or avoided?
What would a neutral observer see?
How is your body responding — tension, energy, fatigue?

Stay with what is measurable and visible.
```

### Lower-Left Quadrant — We (Intersubjective) (3-5 min)

```
Now consider the relational and cultural dimensions.

What shared meaning exists around [SITUATION]?
How do the people involved understand what is happening?
What cultural values or expectations are at play?
What is the quality of the relational space — trust, tension, distance, warmth?

You do not need to agree with others' perspectives to notice them.
```

### Lower-Right Quadrant — Its (Interobjective) (3-5 min)

```
Finally, consider the systems and structures.

What institutions, organizations, or environments are involved?
What material conditions shape [SITUATION]?
What rules, policies, or economic factors are at play?
What would change if the system changed?

Systems are often invisible until we look for them.
```

### Synthesis (2-3 min)

```
You have now looked at [SITUATION] from four irreducible perspectives.

Which quadrant felt most familiar? Which felt most uncomfortable or unfamiliar?
Did anything surprise you?
What pattern emerges when you hold all four views at once?

There is no right answer. The value is in the looking.
```

### Closing — Grounding (1-2 min)

```
Take a moment to return to where you are.
Feel your feet. Notice the room.
Whatever you discovered, it is yours to use or set aside.

This practice can be revisited whenever a situation feels stuck or one-dimensional.
```

## Variations

### Brief Check-In (5-7 min)

- One prompt per quadrant, 60 seconds each
- Quick synthesis
- Good for daily orientation

### Deep Inquiry (30-40 min)

- Extended prompts with journaling
- Multiple rounds through each quadrant
- Cross-quadrant dialogue
- Best for significant decisions or persistent patterns

### Focused Check-In

- Emphasize one or two quadrants while briefly touching the others
- Useful when a specific dimension is habitually overlooked

---

## Evidence and Attribution

> This practice structure is based on Ken Wilber's AQAL framework (2000),
> which maps experience across four irreducible quadrants. The AQAL model
> is a theoretical synthesis that has not been validated as an integrated
> assessment instrument. Individual components — mindfulness-based
> self-inquiry, structured perspective-taking — have stronger empirical
> support (Kabat-Zinn, 2003; Galinsky et al., 2005).
