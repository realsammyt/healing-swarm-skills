## Safety — Read Before Every Session

### Volume and Hearing

- Practice at a **comfortable volume** — toning should never cause
  ear discomfort
- If you have **tinnitus**, use only very soft humming; stop if
  ringing increases
- If you wear **hearing aids**, remove or adjust them before toning
- Keep volume at conversational level — never exceed 85 dB

### Vocal Safety

- Always complete the warm-up before toning
- Maximum **5 minutes** of continuous toning per frequency
- Hydrate between frequencies — water nearby is recommended
- If your throat hurts, STOP. Pain means stop.
- If your voice feels strained, switch to listening-only

### Contraindications — Do Not Practice Toning If You Have:

- Active ear infection or recent ear surgery
- Vocal cord conditions (nodules, polyps, laryngitis)
- Severe tinnitus or hyperacusis
- Recent jaw or dental surgery
- Active respiratory infection with sore throat
- Epilepsy with sound sensitivity (consult your neurologist)
- Any condition your healthcare provider has advised against
  vocal exercise

### Stop Immediately If You Experience:

- Ear pain or sudden hearing changes
- Persistent dizziness or vertigo
- Voice pain or loss during practice
- New ringing in ears not present before
- Severe headache

_If you have any medical conditions, consult your healthcare provider
before beginning this practice._

## The Practice

### Warm-Up (2-3 minutes)

**What you'll do:**
Gently prepare your voice and throat with soft humming.

**How to do it:**

1. Close your lips gently and begin humming at any comfortable pitch
2. There is no correct pitch — let your body find what resonates
3. Place one hand on your chest to feel the vibration
4. Hum through 3-5 slow exhales
5. Gently open and close your jaw a few times while humming
6. Notice where you feel vibration — chest, throat, face, sinuses

**What you might notice:**

- Warmth or tingling in chest, throat, or face
- Jaw and throat relaxing
- A natural settling of your breathing rhythm

_If your voice cracks or wavers, that's completely normal. This is
exploration, not performance._

#### Phase 1: Listen (60 seconds)

**What you'll do:**

**How to do it:**

1. Close your eyes if comfortable
2. Listen to the space around you
3. [If using reference tone: listen to the [X] Hz tone]

5. No need to do anything — just receive

#### Phase 2: Hum (90 seconds)

**What you'll do:**
Begin humming — finding a pitch that feels comfortable in your body.

**How to do it:**

1. Lips closed, jaw relaxed
2. Begin humming at whatever pitch feels natural
3. [If matching frequency: let your pitch drift toward the reference]
4. Hand on chest — feel the vibration
5. Let the hum fill your exhale naturally
6. Rest on the inhale, resume on the exhale

**What you might notice:**

- Vibration spreading through your chest and throat
- A sense of the sound "finding its place" in your body
- Your pitch may naturally drift — that's fine

#### Phase 3: Vocal Tone (90 seconds)

**What you'll do:**
Open the hum into a vowel sound — AH, OH, or OO.

**How to do it:**

1. While humming, gently open your lips to an "AH" or "OH"
2. Keep the same pitch — just open the sound
3. The volume is moderate — no need to project
4. Feel where the vibration shifts when you open the sound
5. Experiment: try AH, then OH, then OO — notice the difference
6. Let the sound ride your natural exhale

**What you might notice:**

- Vibration moving from chest to face as vowels change
- A sense of spaciousness as the sound opens
- Emotional release (tears, sighing, laughter — all normal)
- Different vowels resonate in different parts of the body

_Every few cycles, pause for a breath or two of silence. Listen
to the space after the sound._

#### Phase 4: Harmonics Exploration (60 seconds)

_[Standard and Deep depth only — skip for Foundation]_

**What you'll do:**
Explore the overtones within your vocal tone.

**How to do it:**

1. While sustaining a vowel tone, very slowly change mouth shape
2. Move from "AH" through "OH" to "OO" and back — slowly
3. Listen for higher whistling tones that appear above the base note
4. These are harmonics — natural overtones of your fundamental pitch
5. No need to force them — just notice if they emerge

**What you might notice:**

- Faint whistling or ringing tones above your voice
- The sound becoming more complex
- This is subtle — don't worry if you don't notice harmonics

#### Phase 5: Silence (30 seconds)

**What you'll do:**
Rest in silence after the sound.

**How to do it:**

1. Let the last tone fade naturally
2. Sit in the silence
3. Notice the quality of silence after sound
4. Feel your body — what sensations remain?

_The silence after sound is part of the practice._

## Variations

### If you can't vocalize

- Listening-only: use reference tones (app or recording)
- Focus on body sensations while listening
- All benefits of sound awareness without vocal production

### If this feels too intense

- Stay with humming only — skip vocal toning
- Reduce to 3-minute frequency segments
- Take longer silence intervals
- Stop at any point — partial practice counts

### To deepen the practice

- Extend frequency segments to 7-8 minutes
- Explore multiple frequencies in sequence
- Add resonance body mapping after each frequency
- Practice at the same time daily for consistency

### For different times of day

- **Morning:** Energizing — brighter tones, slightly more volume
- **Midday:** Reset — 5 minutes of humming only
- **Evening:** Calming — lower pitch, softer volume, longer silence

## Troubleshooting

| Issue                          | Suggestion                                                                                  |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Can't find a comfortable pitch | Start by sighing with sound ("Ahhh") — where your voice naturally lands is your pitch today |
| Throat feels scratchy          | Drink water, reduce volume, switch to humming or listening-only                             |
| Feeling self-conscious         | Practice alone first. There is no audience. There is no wrong sound                         |
| Lightheaded after toning       | Pause, breathe normally for a minute. Extended sound naturally extends exhale               |
| No sensations at all           | This is fine. Effects can be subtle or build over sessions                                  |
| Ears ringing after practice    | Reduce volume significantly next time. If new ringing persists, consult healthcare provider |

## Building a Practice

**Frequency:** 3-5 times per week. Daily is ideal if sustainable.

**Timeline:** Allow 1-2 weeks to feel comfortable with vocal
toning. Most people notice changes in body awareness within
2-3 weeks.

**Progression:**

- Week 1-2: Foundation depth — single frequency, humming focus
- Week 3-4: Standard depth — two frequencies, full toning
- Month 2+: Deep depth — multiple frequencies, harmonics, assessment
- Ongoing: Explore different frequencies, deepen body awareness

## Background

### About Archaeoacoustics

Archaeoacoustics is the study of sound in archaeological contexts —
how ancient structures were designed with acoustic properties and
how sound may have been used in ancient ritual contexts. Research
by Jahn & Dunne (1992) documented resonant frequencies in sites
including the Great Pyramid of Giza, Newgrange, and Maltese temples.

Carl Hayden Smith (Museum of Consciousness, Balliol College, Oxford)
has extended this research to explore the relationship between

139.01 Hz at the Saqqara step pyramid (EVA London 2023).

## Element Guidelines

### Safety Section

**Purpose:** Ensure hearing and vocal safety before every session

**Requirements:**

- Volume guidance (comfortable, never exceeding 85 dB)
- Hearing condition screening (tinnitus, hearing aids, ear conditions)
- Vocal condition screening (throat conditions, vocal cord issues)
- Maximum toning duration per frequency (5 minutes)
- Hydration reminders
- Red flag symptoms for adverse effects
- Medical disclaimer

**This section must NEVER be shortened, softened, or made optional.**

### Warm-Up Phase

**Purpose:** Prepare the voice and throat safely

**Key principles:**

- Start with humming (lowest barrier)
- Self-selected pitch (never prescribed)
- Jaw and throat relaxation
- Vibrotactile awareness (hand on chest)
- Permission for voice imperfection

### Frequency Segments

**Purpose:** Guide exploration of specific archaeoacoustic frequencies

**Key principles:**

- Progressive engagement: listen → hum → tone → harmonics → silence
- Each phase builds on the previous
- Self-selected pitch in comfortable range near target frequency
- NEVER require exact frequency matching
- Body awareness throughout
- Silence as integration, not absence

### Evidence Notes

**Purpose:** Honest framing of archaeoacoustic research

**Required in every practice:**

- Specific site and frequency named with source
- Study type, sample size, and findings
- Limitations clearly noted
- "Suggests" not "proves"
- Distinguish acoustic measurement from consciousness claims

## Accessibility Guidelines

### Physical Accessibility

- Can be done seated in any position
- No physical requirements beyond breathing
- Listening-only variation for non-vocalizers
- Whispered/subvocal variation for limited vocal capacity
- No equipment required (reference tones optional)

### Cognitive Accessibility

- Clear phase markers (Listen, Hum, Tone, Silence)
- One instruction per step
- Simple progression — same structure every frequency
- Counting optional, never required

### Sensory Accessibility

- Vibrotactile cues (hand on chest) for deaf/hard-of-hearing
- Written instructions for all phases
- Multiple modalities: sound, vibration, body awareness
- No reliance on visual cues during practice

### Psychological Safety

- Permission to stop at any phase
- No requirement for specific experiences
- Emotional release framed as normal, not required
- Not a performance
- Volume always self-controlled
