# Hyperhumanism Onboarding: Four-Phase Inquiry

> A full hyperhumanism-inquiry output for one composite, fictional participant.
> It walks the four phases in order: inventory, audit, reclamation, and
> practice design. Each phase fills the matching template by name and carries
> one thread forward: the participant's own sense of place. Carl Hayden Smith's
> hyperhumanism is the framework throughout, offered as a lens, not a treatment.

---

## Onboarding at a Glance

- **Participant:** a composite, fictional adult, heavy screen use, wants more
  presence and a stronger sense of place. Nothing here identifies a real person.
- **Baseline:** from `research/capacity-baseline.md`, using the four clusters in
  `shared/consciousness-literacy-levels.md`: somatic Developing, perceptual
  Nascent, relational Nascent, attentional Developing.
- **Thread carried through all four phases:** wayfinding and the participant's
  own sense of place.

> **A note on boundaries:** This inquiry is a private reflection, not a clinical
> or diagnostic process. It measures nothing about the participant's health or
> worth, and it does not replace medical or psychological care. It includes no
> substances of any kind.

## Phase 1: Capacity Inventory

Filled from `content/templates/capacity-inventory.md`.

**Framework:** Hyperhumanism (Carl Hayden Smith)
**Phase:** 1 of 4, Inventory
**Focus area:** presence and sense of place

The inventory is a snapshot, not a scorecard. Delegating a capacity to a device
is a normal, often useful choice. The point is to see clearly.

| Capacity          | Direct | Shared | Delegated | What stands in for it |
| ----------------- | ------ | ------ | --------- | --------------------- |
| Navigation        | [ ]    | [ ]    | [x]       | Satellite-nav app     |
| Memory            | [ ]    | [x]    | [ ]       | Contacts and notes    |
| Attention         | [ ]    | [x]    | [ ]       | Feed or timeline      |
| Sensing           | [ ]    | [x]    | [ ]       | Camera                |
| Social connection | [ ]    | [x]    | [ ]       | Messaging             |
| Wonder            | [x]    | [ ]    | [ ]       | Crowded out, not delegated |
| Presence          | [ ]    | [x]    | [ ]       | Messaging, camera     |
| Movement          | [x]    | [ ]    | [ ]       | None                  |

**Reflection prompts, in the participant's words:**

- Direct capacities: movement still feels like their own, and wonder is intact
  when there is room for it.
- The surprise: navigation read as fully Delegated. They could not recall the
  last familiar route they held from memory.
- A capacity they miss exercising, with no judgment attached: their own sense
  of place.

**Carry forward to the audit:** navigation and sense of place. Curiosity is the
only requirement; the participant does not need to want to change anything yet.

## Phase 2: Technology Landscape

Filled from `content/templates/technology-landscape.md`, supported by
`research/technology-displacement.md`.

**Framework:** Hyperhumanism (Carl Hayden Smith)
**Phase:** 2 of 4, Audit
**Builds on:** the phase 1 inventory

Every tool sits somewhere on a balance. It can **displace** a capacity, doing
the work so the person exercises it less, and it can **augment** a capacity,
extending reach. Most tools do both. Naming both sides is the point; neither
side is a judgment.

| Device or App     | Capacity displaced | Capacity augmented   | Reflection |
| ----------------- | ------------------ | -------------------- | ---------- |
| Satellite-nav app | Wayfinding, spatial memory | Reach into unfamiliar places | Used even on familiar routes |
| Messaging         | Presence           | Reach, speed         | Convenient, a little flattening |
| Feed or timeline  | Attention          | Awareness of many    | Attention leaves in small bites |
| Camera            | Present sensing    | Recall, sharing      | Sometimes watches through the screen |

**Patterns across the landscape:**

- The capacity displaced most often is wayfinding.
- The satellite-nav app gives genuine augmentation, reach into unfamiliar
  places, in exchange for the sense of place the participant would like back
  for routes they already know.
- Most exchanges arrived gradually and unnoticed, not by a conscious choice.

**Honest evidence note:** the association between habitual satellite-navigation
use and less spatial-memory engagement is correlational (for example Dahmani
and Bohbot, 2020), not causal. Research suggests leaning on the app tends to
mean exercising one's own sense of place less often. Hyperhumanism itself is a
theoretical framework, not a validated instrument.

**Carry forward to reclamation:** the participant chooses to reconnect with
wayfinding on familiar routes. This is addition, not deletion; the app stays.

## Phase 3: Reclamation, a Scaffold→Remove Progression

Filled from `content/templates/scaffold-progression.md`.

**Target capacity:** wayfinding and sense of place on familiar routes
**Scaffolding tool:** the satellite-nav app, used deliberately and then set down
**Total arc:** 6 weeks
**Based on:** Carl Hayden Smith's principle that technology is a catalyst for
innate capacity, not a permanent crutch

The plan is stated up front so the app is never mistaken for the practice: use
it as a scaffold for a few weeks, then set it down for familiar routes.

> **Evidence note:** memory-of-place work relates to the memory-palace and
> sensory-foregrounding family in `shared/endo-technology-taxonomy.md`.
> Method-of-loci memory is Well-established as a mnemonic; its use for everyday
> sense of place is Preliminary. The scaffold-to-remove arc is a practice
> framework from Smith's hyperhumanism, not a clinically validated protocol.

**Safety notes:** wayfinding practice for familiar, safe routes only, in
daylight where the participant already feels secure. Keep a phone available for
genuine need; setting the app down is for practice, not for getting stranded.
Returning to an earlier phase is always allowed.

**Phase 1, Technology-Supported (weeks 1 to 2):** on two familiar routes, run
the app but look up from it. Before each turn, predict which way, then let the
app confirm. Track: how often the prediction matches.

**Phase 2, Bridging (weeks 3 to 4):** on the same routes, keep the app in the
pocket. Find the way from memory, then check the app only at the end to confirm.

**Phase 3, Unaided (weeks 5 to 6 and onward):** find the way with no app on the
two familiar routes. Outcome markers, concrete and checkable:

- [ ] Reaches both familiar destinations without opening the app
- [ ] Can describe each route from memory before setting out
- [ ] Notices one or two landmarks they had never registered before
- [ ] Chooses freely, route by route, whether the app helps or not

**Consciousness-literacy tie-in:** wayfinding draws on the perceptual cluster,
where the participant starts Nascent. Nascent means full structure helps, so
phase 1 keeps the app present and the routes familiar. Graduation toward
Developing looks like naming the route from memory while walking it, not only
afterward.

**If removal stalls:** return to phase 2, shorten to one route, or extend the
timeline. Reducing the app more slowly is a valid outcome. The goal is a
portable sense of place, not a fast finish.

## Phase 4: Micro-Practice Design

Filled from `content/templates/micro-practice-design.md`.

**Practice name:** Landmark Noticing
**Capacity reclaimed:** wayfinding and sense of place
**Duration:** 5 minutes
**Frequency:** daily or near-daily
**Equipment:** none required
**Where:** on a short walk the participant already takes

This is a small, repeatable practice that exercises the capacity chosen in
phase 3. It is an addition to the day, not a subtraction from technology. It
asks for a few minutes, no tools, and no special conditions.

> **Evidence note:** the idea that a capacity stays available when it is
> exercised regularly draws on general findings on attention and skill, and on
> the correlational spatial-memory research noted above. Research suggests short,
> consistent practice matters more than long, occasional sessions. This design
> makes no health claim and is not a treatment.

**The endo-technology principle:** this practice uses only the participant's own
attention, senses, and memory. No app, wearable, or device is part of it, and no
substances are involved.

**Step by step:**

1. On a short familiar walk, put the phone away.
2. Pick one landmark ahead: a tree, a door, a corner.
3. Name to yourself where it sits relative to the last one you noticed.
4. At the end, recall the sequence of landmarks in order.

**What you might notice:** perhaps a route feeling more like a place and less
like a blur, perhaps nothing at first. A flat result is still a valid result.

**Anchoring it:** attach the practice to an existing daily walk (to the shop,
the station, or around the block). Cue: stepping out the door. If a day is
missed, begin again the next day. Missing is expected and is not failure.

**Optional light tracking:** a checkbox and one word for how the walk felt, no
more. After a week or two, notice whether the participant's sense of place feels
a little more available.

## Where This Can Go Next

The four phases are a starting arc, not a finish line. If the participant wants
to keep building, two paths in `shared/practice-pathways.md` fit:

- **The Hyperhuman Cultivation Arc:** the participant is at the capacity
  inventory and technology audit stages; the arc continues into an
  endo-technology foundation and, later, an ecology of practices.
- **An ecology of practices:** once the wayfinding practice is steady, the
  participant could design a small ecology with `content/templates/ecology-design.md`,
  adding a somatic and a consent-first relational practice so no single practice
  carries the whole system.

Each next step stays optional, self-paced, and honest about evidence. Smith's
frame holds throughout: the tools are already in the body, and technology is a
scaffold to borrow and then set down.
