# Technology Displacement Audit

> Research support for the technology-landscape phase of the onboarding. It
> reads which innate capacities the composite, fictional participant has
> handed to devices, using the displace-and-augment lens of the
> technology-landscape template and the endo-vs-cyberdelic boundary in
> `shared/endo-technology-taxonomy.md`. The frame is curious and even-handed:
> technology is a set of tradeoffs, not an enemy.

---

## What This Audit Is For

Every tool sits somewhere on a balance. It can **displace** a capacity, doing
the work so the person exercises it less, and it can **augment** a capacity,
extending reach beyond what the person could do alone. Most tools do both, to
different capacities. This audit names both sides for the participant's daily
tools, without labelling any tool good or bad.

> **Evidence note:** Hyperhumanism is a theoretical framework from Carl Hayden
> Smith. It draws attention to how tools change our relationship with innate
> capacities. Where research exists on a specific effect it is noted and kept
> honest; where it does not, the observation is offered as reflection only.
> Research suggests both displacement and augmentation can happen at once. This
> audit makes no claim that any device causes harm.

| Device or App     | Capacity displaced | Capacity augmented   | Reflection |

| Satellite-nav app | Wayfinding, spatial memory | Reach into unfamiliar places | Used for almost every trip, even familiar ones |

## Where Research Speaks, and Where It Does Not

### Wayfinding and spatial memory

Some studies report an association between habitual satellite-navigation use
and less engagement of spatial memory and hippocampus-dependent wayfinding
(for example Dahmani and Bohbot, 2020, in *Scientific Reports*). This work is
correlational: it does not show that navigation apps cause a lasting change,
and people who already find their way by other means may simply use the app
less. The honest reading is that leaning on the app tends to mean exercising
one's own sense of place less often, not that the app damages anything.

Wayfinding here relates to the memory-palace and sensory-foregrounding family
of endo-technologies. Method-of-loci memory work is Well-established as a
mnemonic (for example Dresler et al., 2017); its use for everyday sense of
place is Preliminary.

### Presence and attention

The participant reports that messaging and feeds take a share of presence and
attention. There is a broad research conversation about attention and digital
media, but findings are mixed and often correlational, so this audit treats the
participant's own report as the primary data and avoids strong causal claims.
Silence and coherent breathing, both endo-technologies in the taxonomy, are the
practices most relevant to reclaiming attention directly.

### Wonder and awe

The participant mentions that days can pass without a moment of wonder. Research
on awe suggests associations with prosocial and self-transcendent states (for
example Piff et al., 2015), with individual variation and no promised effect.
Wonder is not delegated to a single device so much as crowded out by a full
screen; naming it is enough for the inventory to carry it forward.

---

## Patterns Across the Landscape

- The capacity displaced most often across the participant's tools is
  wayfinding, followed by attention and presence.
- The satellite-nav app gives real augmentation, reach into unfamiliar places,
  in exchange for a sense of place the participant would like back for familiar
  routes.
- Most exchanges arrived gradually and unnoticed, rather than by a conscious
  choice.

---

## Carry Forward

From this map, the participant is curious to reconnect with **wayfinding and
their own sense of place**, starting with familiar routes. This is not deleting
the app or blaming it; it is choosing one capacity to exercise a little more
directly. The reclamation phase builds on this, using the scaffold-to-remove
progression so the app becomes a temporary scaffold rather than a permanent
stand-in.

---
