# Contemporary Grief Research and Continuing Bonds Theory

> Evidence review for the grief healing example.

---

## Overview

Grief research has undergone significant evolution over the past three decades,
moving from prescriptive stage models toward more nuanced, flexible frameworks
that respect individual variation and cultural context. This review covers the
key findings relevant to the "Acknowledging Loss" grief practice.

---

## Foundational Grief Theories

### Stage Models: Historical Context and Limitations

**Kubler-Ross (1969)** — *On Death and Dying*
- Proposed five stages: denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance
- Originally described reactions of terminally ill patients, not bereaved
- Widely popularized but frequently misapplied as prescriptive timeline
- Kubler-Ross herself later clarified the stages were never meant to be linear
  or universal
- **Current status:** Historically significant but no longer considered an
  accurate model for bereavement. Can cause harm when used prescriptively
  ("You should be in the acceptance stage by now").

**Evidence against stage models:**
- Bonanno (2009): Large-scale studies show grief trajectories are diverse;
  no single pattern predominates
- Maciejewski et al. (2007, JAMA): While some sequential patterns exist,
  individual variation is enormous (n=233)
- Holland & Neimeyer (2010): Stage models do not predict grief outcomes

### Dual Process Model (Stroebe & Schut, 1999)

**The framework:** Bereaved individuals oscillate between two orientations:
- **Loss-oriented coping**: Confronting and processing the loss itself
  (crying, yearning, reviewing memories)
- **Restoration-oriented coping**: Attending to life changes resulting from
  the loss (new roles, new identity, practical tasks)

**Key insight:** Healthy grieving involves dynamic oscillation between both.
Neither permanent immersion in grief nor avoidance of it is adaptive.

**Evidence:**
- Widely adopted in bereavement research and clinical practice
- Supported by longitudinal studies showing oscillation patterns
- Cross-cultural applicability demonstrated (though balance differs by culture)
- Guides the "Acknowledging Loss" practice: we create space for loss-oriented
  engagement while respecting that restoration is also necessary

*Source: Stroebe, M. & Schut, H. (1999). The dual process model of coping
with bereavement: rationale and description. Death Studies, 23(3), 197-224.
DOI: 10.1080/074811899201046*

### Continuing Bonds Theory (Klass, Silverman & Nickman, 1996)

**The paradigm shift:** For most of the 20th century, Western psychology held
that healthy grief required "letting go" and severing attachment to the deceased
(influenced by Freud's "Mourning and Melancholia," 1917). Continuing bonds
theory challenged this directly.

**Core claims:**
- Maintaining an ongoing relationship with the deceased is normal and healthy
- The bond transforms rather than dissolves
- Most cultures worldwide practice continuing bonds naturally (ancestor
  veneration, Day of the Dead, yahrzeit)
- The "letting go" model was culturally specific to mid-20th century Western
  psychology and not evidence-based

**Evidence:**
- Field (2006): Continuing bonds associated with better adjustment when
  the relationship is comforting rather than distressing
- Root & Exline (2014): Both secular and religious continuing bonds predict
  better grief outcomes in most cases
- Klass (2006): Cross-cultural evidence strongly supports maintaining
  connection across cultures

**Nuance:** Not all continuing bonds are helpful. Bonds characterized by
dependency, guilt, or unresolved conflict may require therapeutic processing.
Healthy continuing bonds involve comfort, meaning, and ongoing connection.

**Application to practice:** The "Acknowledging Loss" practice does not ask
anyone to "let go." It creates space for whatever relationship exists with
what has been lost — including ongoing connection.

*Source: Klass, D., Silverman, P.R. & Nickman, S.L. (1996). Continuing
Bonds: New Understandings of Grief. Taylor & Francis.*

## Evidence for Specific Grief Interventions

### Grief Counseling Effectiveness

**Currier, Neimeyer & Berman (2008)** — Meta-analysis
- Grief counseling is most effective for complicated/prolonged grief
- For "normal" grief, universal interventions show smaller effect sizes
- Targeted interventions (for those with identified complications) show
  moderate to large effects (d = 0.53)
- Non-targeted interventions (offered to all bereaved) show small effects
  (d = 0.16)
- **Implication:** Grief practices should be offered as supportive, not
  as treatment for a disorder. Those struggling should be referred.

*Source: Currier, J.M., Neimeyer, R.A. & Berman, J.S. (2008). The
effectiveness of psychotherapeutic interventions for bereaved persons:
A comprehensive quantitative review. Psychological Bulletin, 134(5),
648-661. PMID: 18729566*

### Mindfulness-Based Grief Interventions

**Thieleman & Cacciatore (2014)** — ATTEND model
- Mindfulness-based intervention for bereaved individuals
- Attunement, Trust, Therapeutic Touch, Egalitarianism, Nuance, Death Education
- Moderate evidence for reducing grief symptoms and improving wellbeing
- Mindfulness supports "being with" grief rather than avoiding or being
  overwhelmed by it

**O'Connor et al. (2014)** — Neuroimaging
- Functional MRI studies of bereaved individuals show that mindfulness
  practice modulates activity in brain regions associated with emotional
  regulation and rumination
- Suggests a neurobiological basis for mindfulness-based grief support

**Kumar (2005)** — *Grieving Mindfully*
- Practical framework for applying mindfulness to bereavement
- Emphasis on present-moment awareness of grief without judgment
- Aligns with the "Acknowledging Loss" practice approach

*Sources:*
- *Thieleman, K. & Cacciatore, J. (2014). Witness to suffering: Mindfulness
  and compassion fatigue among traumatic bereavement volunteers. Social Work,
  59(1), 34-41. PMID: 24640229*
- *Kumar, S.M. (2005). Grieving Mindfully: A Compassionate and Spiritual
  Guide to Coping with Loss. New Harbinger Publications.*

### Narrative and Meaning-Making Approaches

**Neimeyer (2001)** — Meaning Reconstruction
- Loss challenges the "narrative" of one's life
- Meaning-making involves integrating loss into an ongoing life story
- Two forms: sense-making ("Why did this happen?") and benefit-finding
  ("What has changed in me?")
- Those who find meaning show better adjustment (Davis et al., 1998)
- **But:** meaning-making should never be forced or rushed. Premature
  meaning-making can feel dismissive of the loss itself

*Source: Neimeyer, R.A. (2001). Meaning Reconstruction and the Experience
of Loss. American Psychological Association.*

### Expressive Writing for Grief

**Pennebaker & Beall (1986)** — Original expressive writing studies
- Writing about emotional experiences improves health outcomes
- Applied to bereavement: moderate evidence for benefit
- 15-20 minutes of writing, 3-4 consecutive days
- Mechanism: translating emotional experience into language supports
  cognitive processing

**Lichtenthal & Cruess (2010)** — Bereavement-specific writing
- Expressive writing adapted for bereaved individuals
- Benefits most pronounced for those with complicated grief features
- Important: writing about loss can temporarily increase distress before
  improving outcomes — practices should include this warning

*Source: Pennebaker, J.W. & Beall, S.K. (1986). Confronting a traumatic
event: Toward an understanding of inhibition and disease. Journal of
Abnormal Psychology, 95(3), 274-281. PMID: 3745650*

### Social Support and Grief

**Stroebe et al. (2005)** — Review of social support in bereavement
- Social support is consistently the strongest predictor of grief adjustment
- Both emotional support (listening, validating) and practical support
  (meals, childcare, administrative help) matter
- Perceived support (feeling supported) matters more than received support
  (actual help provided)
- Social isolation significantly worsens grief outcomes

**Implications for practice:**
- Individual grief practices cannot substitute for social support
- Practices should encourage connection, not isolation
- Community grief practices (see community-facilitator) have additional value

*Source: Stroebe, W., Zech, E., Stroebe, M.S. & Abakoumkin, G. (2005).
Does social support help in bereavement? Journal of Social and Clinical
Psychology, 24(7), 1030-1050. DOI: 10.1521/jscp.2005.24.7.1030*

## Complicated Grief / Prolonged Grief Disorder

### Diagnostic Criteria

The DSM-5-TR (2022) includes Prolonged Grief Disorder as a diagnostic category:
- Intense grief persisting beyond 12 months (adults) or 6 months (children)
- Marked by intense yearning/longing and preoccupation with the deceased
- At least 3 of 8 additional symptoms (identity disruption, disbelief,
  avoidance, emotional numbness, meaninglessness, loneliness, etc.)
- Significant functional impairment

### Treatment Evidence

**Shear et al. (2005, 2014)** — Complicated Grief Treatment (CGT)
- Randomized controlled trial comparing CGT to interpersonal psychotherapy
- CGT showed significantly better outcomes (response rate: 51% vs. 28%)
- CGT includes: dual process model, continuing bonds, exposure to grief cues
- **Strong evidence** for targeted treatment of complicated grief

*Source: Shear, M.K. et al. (2014). Optimizing treatment of complicated
grief: A randomized clinical trial. JAMA Psychiatry, 71(4), 363-370.
PMID: 24477837*

### Implications for Grief Practices

**Critical distinction:** The grief practices in this skill are NOT treatment
for prolonged grief disorder. They are contemplative companions for normal
grief. When criteria for professional referral are met, the practice should
direct the person to a qualified grief therapist.

**Overall evidence for this practice type:** Moderate. The individual
components are well-supported, and the approach aligns with contemporary
grief theory. The specific combination as a self-guided practice has less
direct evidence, though the underlying principles are well-established.

## Limitations and Honest Assessment

1. **Self-guided grief practices have limited direct research.** Most grief
   intervention research involves therapist-guided treatment.
2. **Individual variation is enormous.** What helps one person may not help
   another. Practices should always be offered as invitations, not prescriptions.
3. **Timing matters.** Very recent loss may not be appropriate for structured
   practice — sometimes simply surviving the day is enough.
4. **Cultural context matters.** Evidence is predominantly from Western,
   English-speaking populations. Applicability across cultures should not
   be assumed.
5. **Grief practices are not therapy.** They complement but do not replace
   professional support, especially for complicated grief.
