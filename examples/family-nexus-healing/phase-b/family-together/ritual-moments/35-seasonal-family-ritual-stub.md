# Seasonal Family Ritual — If Today Feels Right

**Configuration:** family-together
**Duration:** 5–20 minutes (variable; the practice ends when it ends)
**Energy cost:** low

## Why this practice

Across traditions and centuries, families facing impossible situations have found that one small, repeated, set-apart gesture — done together when conditions allow — may help hold the shape of the family across time. Ritual theory (van Gennep, 1909; Turner, 1969) identifies three stages in rites of passage: separation from ordinary time, a liminal threshold, and return. This practice is a miniature version of that structure: a moment set apart, a small shared act, a return to ordinary life. The act does not require explanation, narrative memory, or a specific cognitive level. It requires only that the people present are willing to be present together for a few minutes. If the father can be there, he is there. If he cannot, the mother and children hold the moment and hold him in mind. Both versions are complete.

## You'll need

Choose ONE. The same one can be used every time, or the family can rotate.

- **A candle and matches.** Any candle. A birthday candle in a holder works. A jar candle works. Nothing ceremonial is required.
- **A seed, a bulb, or a small plant.** Plus a pot with soil, or a patch of earth the family can reach.
- **Walking shoes.** For a walk to a place that matters to the family — a park, a bench, a tree, a corner of a neighborhood.
- **A small stone or shell** to add to a growing collection (a bowl, a jar, a windowsill).

No single material is essential. The practice works with nothing at all.

## Before you start

- **Safety check.** Is today a day when the family can be together within the terms of the court arrangement? If supervised visitation is in effect, this practice must occur within the structure of an approved visit or an approved family-together occasion. If there is any doubt, check with the visit supervisor or the family's legal representative first.
- **Weather check on every person.** The mother checks in — with herself, with each child, with the father if he is present. Does today feel right? Not "should we do this" but "does this feel okay right now?" If anyone is unsettled, today is not the day. Another day will come.
- **Exit permission.** Anyone can stop at any step. Anyone can sit quietly and watch instead of participating. Watching is participating. "You can stop at any step. Stopping is a win, not a failure."

## When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** any configuration that violates the court-ordered arrangement or safety boundaries. If the parents are not currently permitted to be together unsupervised, this practice must happen within an approved supervised setting, or the father-absent version is used instead.
- **Relative:** extended all-day family time during acute phases of the father's condition. This practice is short on purpose. It is not a prelude to spending the whole day together. If the family is in an acute phase, the practice should be at its shortest (5 minutes or less) or deferred entirely.
- **Caution:** any framing that pressures the father to "be like before" — to remember past rituals in detail, to perform a role he held when his cognition was different, to demonstrate capacity. The practice meets him where he is today.
- **If any signal crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

**The invitation.** This is not a scheduled obligation. It is an offered moment. The mother holds the invitation: "If today feels right, today is the day. If not, another day." The practice might happen quarterly, seasonally, or once a year. It might happen three times in one season and then not again for six months. The rhythm belongs to the family.

When the day arrives:

1. **Gather.** Everyone who is present comes to the same place — a table, a patch of ground, a trailhead, a bench. No rush. No announcement beyond: "Let's do our thing."

2. **One small shared act.** Choose the version that fits today:

   - **Candle version.** Light the candle together. (The 5-year-old may need help; helping is part of the ritual.) Sit with it for one minute. Each person, if they want to, names one thing they are grateful for. One sentence. "I'm grateful for this park." "I'm grateful for my sister." "I'm grateful we're here." Silence is also allowed. Blow the candle out together or let the youngest blow it out.

   - **Planting version.** Put the seed, bulb, or plant into the soil together. Each person touches the soil. One person waters it. If the family wants to, they name the plant — not ceremonially, just: "This one is the spring one." The plant does not have to survive for the ritual to have worked. The act of planting is the practice.

   - **Walking version.** Walk together to the place. Stand there for a minute. Notice one thing — the light, the sound, the temperature, a bird, a color. One person says what they notice. Others can add theirs or stay quiet. Walk back.

   - **Stone version.** Each person chooses or holds a small stone (or shell, or acorn, or button). Place it in the family's collection — a bowl, a jar, a windowsill shelf. One sentence if it comes: "This one is from April." "This one is the blue one." The collection grows over time without anyone needing to explain it.

3. **One breath together.** At the end of the small act, everyone pauses. One slow breath. Not a breathing exercise. Just a shared moment of noticing that everyone is in the same place.

4. **Return to ordinary time.** The ritual is done. No debrief. No "how did that feel?" The family moves to whatever comes next — lunch, a walk, going home, continuing the visit. The transition out is gentle and unmarked.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** confusion about what is happening that does not settle with gentle guidance; agitation; withdrawal; tearfulness that is building rather than settling. If any appear: the mother gently completes the ritual on behalf of everyone ("Let's take our breath and we're done") and moves on.
- **In either child:** refusal to participate (honored without comment — they can watch), distress, complaints that feel somatic ("my tummy hurts"), sudden energy shift toward agitation. If any appear: shorten to one breath and stop.
- **In the mother:** overwhelming grief surfacing, dissociation, sudden anger. Take a breath. Name it to yourself. The ritual can end here and still be complete. If you cannot settle, the ritual is over for today. That is fine.

## Modifications

### If the father cannot be present

The mother and children do this practice together, holding space for the father. The structure is the same with one addition:

- After the small shared act and before the closing breath, the mother says one simple sentence that includes the father: "Daddy is part of our family today too, even though he's not here right now." Or: "We're thinking of Daddy." Or: "This one is for all four of us." The children may add something or stay quiet. The father does not need to be named elaborately. One sentence is enough.

- If the family is using the stone version, they may place a stone for the father as well. The 8-year-old may want to choose it. The 5-year-old may want to place it. Either is fine.

- If the children ask why Daddy isn't here, the mother uses the language from protocol 13 (*"Why can't Daddy live with us?"*): honest, simple, age-appropriate, not evasive. This is not the moment for a long conversation. "Daddy is at his home today. We're doing our thing and he's part of it."

### Shorter version (5 minutes)

Light the candle or hold the stone. One breath. Done.

### For the first time

The mother may want to introduce the practice simply: "I thought we could start doing a small thing together sometimes — just lighting a candle and saying one thing we're grateful for. We don't have to do it every time. Just when it feels right." No buildup. No explanation of why rituals matter. The doing is the teaching.

## After

Optional. The mother may, later and privately, notice:

1. Did the moment feel held, or did it feel forced?
2. What did each person's face look like during the shared act?
3. Is there anything to adjust for next time?

No scoring. No journaling requirement. Just quiet noticing.

## Traditions honored

This practice draws on structural insights from multiple traditions without requiring the family to adopt any one:

- **Ritual theory** — van Gennep's three-stage rite of passage (separation / liminal / incorporation) provides the structure: a moment set apart from ordinary time, a threshold act, a return. Turner's *communitas* names the quality of undifferentiated closeness that sometimes appears in threshold moments. Catherine Bell's practice-theoretic framing: the ritualizing is what makes it ritual, not any external authority. (References: van Gennep, *Rites of Passage*, 1909; Turner, *The Ritual Process*, 1969; Bell, *Ritual Theory, Ritual Practice*, 1992.)
- **Jewish tradition** — the *shehecheyanu* blessing ("who has kept us, sustained us, and brought us to this moment") as a frame for gratitude at the seasonal turn. The candle-lighting draws on Shabbat and Hanukkah practice without replicating either. (Reference: Lamm, *The Jewish Way in Death and Mourning*, 1969/2000.)
- **Christian tradition** — the Ignatian Examen's "consolations" practice (noticing where life was given today) as a frame for the one-sentence gratitude. (Reference: Fleming, *What Is Ignatian Spirituality?*, 2008.)
- **Japanese tea ceremony** — *ichi-go ichi-e* ("one time, one meeting"), the principle that every encounter is unrepeatable and deserving of complete attention. The "if today feels right, today is the day" framing draws directly on this principle. (Reference: Okakura, *The Book of Tea*, 1906.)
- **Secular and humanist approaches** — Sasha Sagan's work on secular ritual design within family life; Viktor Frankl's logotherapy as meaning-making without theism. (References: Sagan, *For Small Creatures Such as We*, 2019; Frankl, *Man's Search for Meaning*, 1946/2006.)
- **Indigenous philosophical orientation** — Robin Wall Kimmerer's writing on reciprocity with the living world informs the planting version. The practice does not enact any closed ceremony; it draws on the openly published philosophical orientation of kinship with all beings. (Reference: Kimmerer, *Braiding Sweetgrass*, 2013.)

None of these traditions is privileged. The family may draw on whichever resonates, or none at all. The ritual is offered as a universal human form: people who love each other, pausing together, noticing they are here.
