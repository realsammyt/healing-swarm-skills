# Milestone Marker — This Shape, This Day

**Configuration:** family-together
**Duration:** 2–10 minutes (the marker is a moment, not an event)
**Energy cost:** low

## Why this practice

Milestones come whether or not the family is ready for them. Birthdays, first days of school, holidays, the turn of a new year. In families facing progressive cognitive change, milestones can carry a specific kind of pain: the distance between how this day was marked before and how it must be marked now. The impulse is either to perform the old version (exhausting, often impossible) or to skip the milestone entirely (a different kind of loss).

This practice offers a third path: mark the milestone in whatever shape the family takes today. The Japanese principle of *ichi-go ichi-e* ("one time, one meeting") holds that every encounter is unique, unrepeatable, and complete in itself. Continuing-bonds theory (Klass, Silverman & Nickman, 1996) holds that relationship persists across change and absence — not as denial of loss, but as honest recognition that love does not require the loved one to be unchanged. Together, these frames may help a family mark a milestone without measuring it against what used to be. This birthday, this first day of school, this holiday — in the shape of the family as it is right now — is whole.

## You'll need

Choose ONE. The same one can be used every milestone, or different milestones can have different markers.

- **A camera** (phone camera is fine) for one photograph.
- **The shared book** (see protocol 27, *Shared Book That Lives in Both Houses*, if available) and a pen or marker.
- **Ingredients for one special food** — a pancake, a cupcake, a specific fruit, a family recipe scaled to its simplest version.
- **A song** the family knows — or any song at all.
- **Nothing.** The marker can be a sentence spoken aloud.

## Before you start

- **Safety check.** Is this milestone day already carrying more weight than the family can hold? If the mother is overwhelmed, if the children are dysregulated, if the father is having a particularly hard day — the milestone can be marked tomorrow, or next week, or in a quiet private moment later. A milestone marked on a different day is still marked.
- **Expectation check.** The mother asks herself, privately: "Am I trying to make this feel like it used to, or am I letting it be what it is today?" If the answer is the former, pause. The practice works only when it is allowed to be small.
- **Exit permission.** "We're going to do one small thing for [the birthday / the holiday / the first day]. If it doesn't feel right, we stop. That's fine."

## When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** any configuration that violates the court-ordered arrangement or safety boundaries. If supervised visitation is in effect, this practice must occur within the structure of an approved visit or an approved family-together occasion. If there is any doubt, check with the visit supervisor or the family's legal representative first. If the milestone falls on a day when the family cannot legally or safely be together, the father-absent version is used.
- **Relative:** high-stakes milestone events where performance pressure is inescapable — large family gatherings, formal celebrations with extended family present, events where multiple people have expectations about how the father should appear or behave. This practice is for the private family moment, not the public performance.
- **Caution:** anything that frames the milestone as a comparison ("last year, Daddy could..."). The marker honors today. If a child spontaneously mentions a comparison, that is their processing and should be received gently (see protocol 14, *How Was Your Heart Today?*), but the adults do not introduce the comparison.
- **If any signal crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

The milestone arrives. The family (however many members are present) does ONE small thing together.

1. **Name the day.** The mother says it simply: "Today is [name]'s birthday." "Today is the first day of school." "Today is [holiday]." One sentence. Not a speech. The naming is itself the separation from ordinary time.

2. **One marker.** Choose the version that fits today:

   - **Photograph version.** Everyone present stands or sits together. One photograph. No posing requirements. No "everyone smile." The photograph is of the family in whatever shape they take today. If someone is crying, that is in the photograph. If someone is laughing, that is in the photograph. The photograph is honest.

   - **Shared-book version.** Open the shared book (protocol 27) to the next blank page. Write the date and one sentence. The sentence can come from anyone: "Mia turned 9 today." "First day of second grade." "We had pancakes." The 5-year-old can draw something instead of writing. The father can write his name, or make a mark, or the mother can write for him. One page. Close the book.

   - **Special food version.** Make or eat one special thing together. A pancake with a candle. A cupcake split four ways (or three ways, or two ways). A bowl of the fruit someone loves. The food does not have to be elaborate. Eating together is the marker.

   - **Song version.** Sing one song. "Happy Birthday" counts. A lullaby counts. A song the family used to sing in the car counts. Humming counts. The father humming one note while the children sing the words counts. If nobody can sing today, someone can play the song on a phone and everyone listens for one minute.

   - **Spoken-sentence version.** The mother says one sentence that marks the day: "We are here on [name]'s birthday, and we love [name]." Or: "First day of school. We're proud of you." Or simply: "Today matters." The children can add a sentence or not.

3. **One breath.** The same shared breath from other practices. A pause. Present.

4. **Continue the day.** The marker is done. The day continues. The milestone has been honored. No debrief.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** distress at the milestone itself (awareness of what has changed, grief at the passage of time, confusion about the occasion). If he is distressed: the mother gently completes the marker without requiring his participation. He can simply be in the room. His presence is his contribution.
- **In either child:** sadness (expected and appropriate — do not fix it), frustration that the day is "not like before" (receive the feeling; do not argue with it), acting out. If distress escalates: the marker is complete as-is. Move on gently.
- **In the mother:** grief that ambushes. Milestones are grief-surge points (Meuser & Marwit, 2001). If grief rises: let it be present. The children can see that milestones can be sad and still marked. If you cannot continue, the marker is done. You can return to it privately later if you want to.

## Specific variations

### If the father is having a hard day and cannot participate

The marker happens with whoever is present and settled. The father can be in the room without participating — his body in the space is enough. The mother does not narrate his difficulty to the children in the moment. She simply carries the marker: names the day, does the one thing, takes the breath. After, if the children ask, she can say: "Daddy is having a tired day. He was with us, and that's what matters."

### If the father is not present at all

The mother and children mark the milestone together. The structure is the same. After the one marker, the mother adds one sentence that includes the father: "Daddy loves you on your birthday too, even though he's not here today." Or: "We'll tell Daddy about the first day of school next time we see him." Or, for the shared book: the mother writes "from all four of us" on the page.

If the children want to call or send a photo to the father (and the court arrangement permits), that can be the marker itself.

### If this is the first milestone since the diagnosis

The first birthday, the first holiday, the first school year after the family's world changed. This one may carry more weight. The mother gives herself permission for that weight. She does not have to make the day feel normal. She does not have to protect the children from every feeling. She does need to keep the marker small — because small is holdable, and holdable is what matters on the hardest days.

If the mother wants to, she can add one private sentence to herself before or after: "This is the first one. We did it. It was enough." No one else needs to hear it.

### If a previous version of this milestone is painfully vivid

The mother may remember last year's birthday party, or the holiday when the father was fully present, or the school morning routine they used to share. The comparison is real. The grief is real. The practice does not ask her to suppress it. It asks her to let today be today. The milestone is marked in today's shape, not measured against yesterday's. Both days are real. Both are honored by the family continuing to mark what matters.

## After

Optional. The mother may, later and privately, notice:

1. Was the marker the right size for today? Too much? Too little?
2. What did the children's faces look like?
3. Is there anything I want to remember for the next milestone?

If using the shared book, the book itself is the record. No additional documentation is needed.

## Traditions honored

This practice draws on structural insights from multiple traditions:

- **Japanese *ichi-go ichi-e*** — "one time, one meeting." The principle that every encounter is unique, irreplaceable, and deserving of full presence. Applied here: this milestone, in this shape, with these people present, is itself complete. It is not a diminished version of a previous milestone. It is the only version of this one. (Reference: attributed to Sen no Rikyu, 16th c., developed by Ii Naosuke; Okakura, *The Book of Tea*, 1906.)
- **Continuing-bonds theory** — Klass, Silverman, and Nickman's research (1996) demonstrating that maintaining connection with the changed or absent person is healthy grief, not denial. The father-absent versions of this practice draw directly on this finding: including the father in the milestone by naming him is continuing the bond, not pretending he is unchanged. (Reference: Klass, Silverman & Nickman, *Continuing Bonds: New Understandings of Grief*, 1996.)
- **Mexican *Dia de los Muertos* and continuing relationship** — the cultural tradition of marking milestones and occasions with those who are absent, through ofrenda, food, and naming. The structural insight: absence does not end relationship; it changes the form relationship takes.
- **Jewish *yahrzeit* and annual marking** — the tradition of returning to specific dates to remember and honor. Milestone-marking across the calendar year echoes this rhythm of return. (Reference: Lamm, *The Jewish Way in Death and Mourning*, 1969/2000.)
- **Anticipatory mourning research** — Rando's (2000) and Boss's (1999) empirical finding that grief surges at transitions and milestones, and that structured containers at those points are protective. The smallness of this practice is deliberate: it provides the container without adding burden. (References: Rando, *Clinical Dimensions of Anticipatory Mourning*, 2000; Boss, *Ambiguous Loss*, 1999.)
- **Secular and humanist approaches** — Viktor Frankl's orientation toward meaning in suffering; Sasha Sagan's secular family rituals. A milestone does not require religious framing to be sacred. It is sacred because the family cares about it. (References: Frankl, *Man's Search for Meaning*, 1946/2006; Sagan, *For Small Creatures Such as We*, 2019.)

None of these traditions is privileged. The family draws on whichever resonates, or none. The milestone is marked because it matters to someone in the family. That is enough.
