# Closing Ritual — Three Glads

**Configuration:** father-with-kids-supervised
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 2–5 minutes (at the end of every visit)
**Energy cost:** low
**Age range:** 5 and 8

## Why this practice

A visit with a clear ending leaves everyone with a shape to hold afterward. The research on visit-bracketing (Phase A synthesis §3.5) converges with both attachment research and ritual theory: children need to know a visit is ending before it ends, and a small closing gesture helps the transition be graceful rather than abrupt. Without a closing, the end feels like a rupture.

"Three glads" is a small shared moment in which each person (including the father, if he can, and including the children) names one thing they are glad about from today. Not "what was your favorite part" — that is a graded question. Just one gladness. Small is fine. Quiet is fine. "I am glad we walked" is enough. Silence is also allowed.

## You'll need

- Nothing. No materials.
- The last 3–5 minutes of the visit, protected from rushing.

## Before you start

- The visit must have a predictable end-point that the children know about 5 minutes before. "We have a little bit more time, and then we're going to do our closing circle and say goodbye."
- The mother checks in with the supervisor about the remaining time.

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Situational:** if the visit ended early due to distress, skip the full ritual and do a 30-second version (one shared breath, a hug, "see you next time").
- **Relational:** if either child is in a state where saying "glad" feels false, do not force it. The ritual can be: "Three things we noticed today" or "Three things we did" instead.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **Announce the ending.** 5 minutes before end: "We have a little bit more time, then we'll do our glads, then it's time to go."
2. **Gather.** Everyone sits close together, the same way they did for the beginning ritual.
3. **One gladness each.** Go in order: mother first, then 5yo, then 8yo, then father. (The mother goes first to model tone — matter-of-fact, small, specific. "I'm glad we walked in the garden today." Not performative. Not poetic.) Each person gets the same small space. If someone cannot name a gladness, they can say "pass" or "I don't have one today," and that is honored.
4. **The father last.** If he can name one, he does. If he says "pass," the mother gently says, "Daddy's glad you were here," on his behalf. A gladness said for someone is still real.
5. **One hug, one goodbye, one small phrase.** The mother's choice: "See you next time, Daddy." The father's response, whatever he can offer. The children's goodbyes.
6. **Move toward leaving without rushing.** Gathering coats, bag, water bottles. The transition is part of the ritual.

## Holding-parent voice (mother)

The mother goes first to set the tone: small, specific, matter-of-fact. She does not build up the moment or ask the children to produce a deep feeling. "I'm glad the light was nice today" is a legitimate gladness. The tone protects the children and the father from feeling they have to perform.

In traditions the mother may hold quietly: the Ignatian Examen's "consolations" — noticing where life was given today — is a Christian/Jesuit practice that names specifically this kind of gratitude. (Reference: Fleming, *What Is Ignatian Spirituality?*, 2008.) The Examen is not being taught here; it is being named as philosophical framing for why noticing small gladnesses matters.

## Changing-parent voice (father)

The father's part is to receive the closing. He does not have to produce a clever gladness. He can say "I'm glad I saw you," or "I'm glad we read," or just smile and point at the children. Anything he offers is enough. Anything the mother says for him is also enough. The practice is the gathering, not the words.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** difficulty finding words at all, confusion about what is being asked, tearfulness at the ending. If any appear: shorten to one hand squeeze and "see you next time." The ritual is honored by being done at all, not by being complete.
- **In either child:** sadness as the end approaches (expected and appropriate; do not try to fix it), clinging ("can we stay longer?"), tantrum about leaving. If any: honor the feeling without extending the visit. "I know it's hard. We'll see Daddy again on [day]." Hold the leaving.
- **In yourself:** overwhelming sadness at the goodbye. Feel it; do not perform around it. The children will see that goodbyes can be sad and survived.

## Modifications

- **Shorter (60 seconds):** No glads. Just: everyone stands, one shared look, one hug each, "See you next time." Done.
- **If a child cannot find a gladness:** "Three things we did" instead. The children name one concrete thing each. The father tries one. The mother covers any gaps.
- **If the father is very confused at closing:** the mother handles the whole ritual verbally on everyone's behalf. The father just receives it. This is not a failure; this is the ritual adapting to what is possible today.

## After

Walk to the car or the exit. No processing in the visit space. The post-visit decompression happens later, at home, with the `12-after-school-decompression.md` or whatever mother-with-kids protocol fits. The closing ritual is complete when the goodbye is said.

## Traditions honored

- **Ignatian Examen** — Christian (Jesuit) practice of daily review noticing *consolations* (moments of given-life). Cited philosophically as the frame for noticing small gladnesses. (Reference: Fleming 2008.)
- **Universal human practice** — across cultures, endings of gatherings are marked with a small shared moment. The Tibetan dedication of merit at the end of practice, the Christian "peace be with you" at the end of a Mass, the Jewish "shalom" at parting — all are examples of marking that a gathering is ending and something has been held.

The ritual is offered as a universal form, shaped to this family's specific context.
