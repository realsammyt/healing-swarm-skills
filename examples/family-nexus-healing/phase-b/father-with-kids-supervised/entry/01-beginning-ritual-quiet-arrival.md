# Beginning Ritual — Quiet Arrival

**Configuration:** father-with-kids-supervised
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 2–4 minutes (at the start of every visit)
**Energy cost:** low
**Age range:** 5 and 8 (both children)

## Why this practice

Every supervised visit benefits from a small, consistent opening that gives the visit a shape. The research on ritual as container (Fiese et al. 2002) and on limited-time presence across traditions (Turner on pilgrimage; Japanese *ichi-go ichi-e*) converges on a single insight: when a visit has a beginning gesture, the nervous systems of everyone in the room settle into "this is the visit now." Without a beginning, the first ten minutes are spent improvising.

This ritual is also the moment where the supervisor is *welcomed* rather than ignored. A brief greeting reframes the supervisor from invisible authority to acknowledged presence, which changes the weather of the room.

The ritual is small on purpose. It should not require the father to remember lines, plan, or perform. It gives him a known way to enter the visit *settled* rather than *braced*.

## You'll need

- Nothing. No materials.
- A quiet moment at the start of the visit, before activities begin.
- Permission from everyone in the room that this will happen every time (established on the first visit and then repeated).

## Before you start

- Safety check: everyone in the room is physically settled. If the father arrived agitated or the children arrived distressed, take 60 seconds for everyone to breathe before starting the ritual.
- Exit permission: anyone can skip the ritual on any given day. It is an offering, not an obligation. "You can stop at any step. Stopping is a win, not a failure."

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Situational:** if the father arrives in acute distress, if either child is crying on arrival, if something has just happened that makes a settled opening feel false — skip the ritual and go directly to a sensory anchor (held hands, a slow breath together, sitting quietly).
- **Relational:** if the supervisor has indicated this is not their first visit with the family and the opening needs to adjust, follow their lead.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **Acknowledge the supervisor.** The mother (or whoever is bringing the children in) looks at the supervisor and says, simply: "Thank you for being with us today." The supervisor may or may not reply; that is their choice. This takes five seconds.
2. **The father settles into his seat or place in the room.** If he is standing, the mother may invite him to sit: "Come sit with us." No pressure. If he prefers to stand, that is fine.
3. **One small body gesture for each child.** The father puts one hand gently on the head, shoulder, or back of each child — whatever feels natural to him and welcome to the child. If touch is not welcome today (children sometimes shy away, and that is normal), the gesture becomes an eye-to-eye moment and a soft "hi."
4. **One phrase, said by the father if he can.** Simple. It can be the same phrase every time. Examples the father may choose from: "I'm happy to see you." "Hi, my beautiful ones." "I've been waiting for today." If the father cannot find a phrase, the mother can say it for him: "Daddy's glad you're here." A phrase said for someone is still a real phrase.
5. **One slow breath together.** Everyone in the room takes one slow breath. Not a "do a breathing exercise" breath. Just a quiet moment where everyone notices they are in the same room. The father, the children, the mother, the supervisor. Present.
6. **Transition to the planned activity.** The mother (or whoever is leading) says: "So today we're going to [walk in the garden / read two books / play with the stones]." One sentence. Then begin.

## Holding-parent voice (mother)

The mother's part is the stable, unhurried framing. She is the one who welcomes the supervisor, who invites the father into the moment if he hesitates, who speaks for him only if he cannot speak for himself. Her tone is matter-of-fact, warm, not performed. "Daddy's glad you're here" is a statement of fact, not a script.

In traditions the mother may draw on: the Jewish *shehecheyanu* blessing for moments of presence ("Blessed are You who has kept us, sustained us, and brought us to this moment"); the Christian simple grace ("Thank you for this time together"); the secular humanist acknowledgment ("We are here together, right now"). The mother may say one of these silently or out loud if it fits her own tradition. None is required.

## Changing-parent voice (father)

The father's part is simply to be present and let the greeting happen. If he has a phrase that comes easily, he says it. If he does not, the mother or he or the children carry the moment. He is not performing a ritual. He is being greeted and greeting back, in whatever form his body and voice are offering today.

In Japanese tea-ceremony practice, the tea master simply *arrives*. There is no speech required. The arrival is itself the ritual's first act. The father's simply being in the room, receiving his children, is the same kind of act.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** visible agitation on arrival, confusion about where he is or who the children are (different from a playful "who are these munchkins?"), tearfulness he cannot settle, pulling back from touch, flat affect replacing warmth. If any appears, the mother gently shortens the ritual to its smallest form — eye contact and one breath — and moves to a sensory anchor (water, a known song, sitting quietly).
- **In either child:** crying on arrival, clinging to the mother, hiding behind the mother, refusing to look at the father, somatic complaints (tummy ache). A child who is not ready to greet the father is not being difficult; they are being accurate to their nervous system. Allow them their distance. The father can greet them from across the room or simply smile at them. The ritual is still honored if it is done quietly.
- **In yourself (the mother):** suddenly tight throat, dissociation, anger rising. Take a breath. Name it to yourself. The ritual will hold while you settle. If you cannot settle, it is appropriate to ask the supervisor to give you 60 seconds to step to the side.

**What to do when you see these:** Shorten the ritual. Move to whatever the next activity is, slowly. Do not try to "rescue" the beginning. The visit can still be whole even if the opening was not. See `00-crisis-response-card.md` if the signals are acute.

## Modifications

- **Shorter version (60 seconds total):** Greet the supervisor. The father sits. One hand on each child's head. One slow breath together. Begin the activity.
- **"Hard day" version (the father is unsettled):** Skip the phrase entirely. The mother carries the whole opening. The father just settles into his place. The body gesture is optional. The ritual becomes: "Thank you for being with us today. Let's sit." One breath. Begin.
- **Wheelchair or seated version:** Exactly the same; the father's gesture is from a seated position. Some fathers may kiss the children's foreheads instead of touching heads. Any gesture that comes naturally.

## After

Optional three-line reflection, spoken silently by the mother or in a private note later:

1. Did the opening settle us, or did we have to work at it?
2. What did the father's face look like as the ritual happened?
3. What do I want to keep the same, or change, next visit?

No scoring. No judgment.

## Traditions honored

This ritual draws on the universal human practice of greeting at the threshold, with specific philosophical frame from:

- **Japanese tea ceremony** — the principle of *ichi-go ichi-e* ("one time, one meeting"), which holds that every encounter is irreplaceable and deserves complete attention. Attributed to Sen no Rikyū (16th c.) and developed by Ii Naosuke. The beginning of a tea gathering is itself a ritual gesture. (Reference: Okakura, *The Book of Tea*, 1906.)
- **Jewish tradition** — the optional *shehecheyanu* blessing as a felt-sense acknowledgment of "we are here now." (The mother may or may not draw on this; it belongs to her own relationship with the tradition.)
- **Quaker silent meeting** — the principle that gathered silence is itself a meeting, not a failure of meeting. The "one slow breath together" draws on this. (Reference: Pink Dandelion, *The Quakers: A Very Short Introduction*, 2008.)

None of these traditions is privileged. The ritual is offered as a universal human form, and the mother and father may draw on whichever tradition they find meaningful, or none at all.
