# Shared Sensory Walk

**Configuration:** father-with-kids-supervised
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 15–30 minutes
**Energy cost:** low–medium
**Age range:** 5 and 8 (both children)

## Why this practice

A slow walk outside with shared attention to small sensory things (leaves, stones, birds, water) is one of the most research-supported practices at the intersection of "works for a cognitively-changing parent," "works for young children," and "works with supervision present." Forest-bathing research (Li 2010; Park et al. 2010) shows lower cortisol and settled nervous systems after time in natural settings. Nature-based interventions for cognitive change (Clark et al.; Rappe 2005) show reduced agitation. Children's nature research (Chawla 2015; Louv 2005) shows emotional regulation and attentional recovery.

The walk is shared-attention, not shared-conversation. No one has to plan, remember, or perform. The father walks alongside his children and notices the world with them. That is the whole practice.

## You'll need

- A quiet outdoor space (a park, a garden, a quiet path, or a tree-lined sidewalk — something with enough visual and tactile interest to support attention)
- Comfortable walking shoes
- Water, in case anyone gets thirsty
- Permission from the supervisor for the outdoor location (verify before the visit)
- Optional: a small basket, cloth bag, or paper envelope for gathering small findings (leaves, pebbles, flower petals) — **nothing that could hurt anyone if swallowed by a child**

## Before you start

- Safety check: weather is safe (not too cold, too hot, too wet, or with any pollen or wildlife concerns the children are sensitive to). The father is steady on his feet today. The children are willing to walk.
- Exit permission: anyone can turn back at any time. A 5-minute walk is as successful as a 30-minute walk if it was settling.
- Hydration check before leaving.

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** if the father is unsteady on his feet today (fall risk) — **operationalized fall-risk check before each walk:** observe his gait as he stands from the chair (shuffling, toe-drag, wide-based instability, needing a hand to stand). Has he fallen in the last 2 weeks? Has any new medication been started that may cause orthostasis (dizziness on standing)? If any of these apply, do the seated version (see Modifications) instead. **Check footwear:** non-slip shoes, no untied laces, no flip-flops. **Avoid uneven terrain:** tree-root heave, loose gravel, stairs, curbs, wet or icy surfaces. A paved, flat path is always safer. **In hot weather:** shorten the walk, stay in shade, offer water before starting and at the midpoint; if the father shows confusion or fatigue in heat, stop and move indoors (dehydration and heat can trigger confusion rapidly in cognitive change). If the children are in acute distress that outdoor walking would not help; if the weather makes it unsafe.
- **Relative:** crowded parks where the ambient noise and distraction would be overstimulating; unfamiliar locations where the father may become disoriented (prefer the same place every time for the first several visits).
- **Caution:** if either child is preoccupied with something emotional from the trip over (a hard conversation, a separation from the mother if she is not coming on the walk), address that before starting the walk, not during it.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **Begin with the beginning ritual** (`01-beginning-ritual-quiet-arrival.md`) — the walk is the "today we're going to walk" part of the transition.
2. **Walk slowly.** Not "children at play" pace, and not "adults exercising" pace. The pace of a 5-year-old noticing a beetle. This pace is often the natural pace of a father whose cognition is shifting; it may be the first time in a while that the family's natural pace matches his.
3. **Set a simple shared task.** One of:
   - *Leaf-finding*: "Let's see how many different kinds of leaves we can find." The children gather. The father points out ones he sees. Keep them in the bag or basket.
   - *Stone-sorting*: "Let's find five smooth stones." Same principle. The children can give the best one to the father, who holds it.
   - *Bird-listening*: "Let's see how many different bird sounds we can hear." Everyone stops and listens when someone hears one. No scorekeeping.
   - *Color-finding*: "Let's find something blue. Now something yellow." The children run and find; the father nods and smiles at what they bring back.
4. **The father's role is to be present, not to lead.** He can comment, point, nod, smile, hold what the children bring him. He does not have to know the names of things, remember names from last time, or produce content. The children lead the finding; he witnesses and appreciates.
5. **Pause for water if anyone gets thirsty.** The pause is itself part of the walk.
6. **End when the children's attention starts to scatter, or when you reach a natural stopping point** (a bench, the far side of the path, the end of a set time). Do not push through the "bored" phase. Short and settled is better than long and stretched.
7. **Walk back to the visit space** with what you gathered. The gathering becomes a small artifact the children can hold or show.
8. **Transition to the closing ritual** (`04-closing-ritual-three-glads.md`) when you return, or to another activity if the visit has more time.

## Holding-parent voice (mother)

The mother's role on the walk, if she is present, is to be the gentle narrator and safety net. She keeps an eye on the path ahead, holds water, reads when a child needs a cue. She does not direct the father. She does not correct his comments if he names a leaf wrong. She lets him be a father on a walk with his children.

If the mother draws on a tradition, she may hold it quietly: the Indigenous philosophical orientation of *Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ* ("all my relations") as a frame for the walk — the trees, the stones, the father, the children, all part of the same web. (Reference: Kimmerer, *Braiding Sweetgrass*, 2013, as an openly published Potawatomi source for this orientation. The phrase itself is Lakota and is used by many Lakota authors.) This is philosophical framing only; no ceremonial practice is being drawn on.

## Changing-parent voice (father)

The father's role is to walk, see, and be with. His body remembers how to walk. His eyes know how to notice. His hands know how to hold a small stone or leaf a child brings him. None of this requires narrative memory. The walk is exactly the kind of practice the research in Phase A §3.3 describes — presence-over-narrative, sensory anchoring, shared attention without shared plan.

If the father wishes to say something, he says it. If he has no words, the walk speaks for him. A father walking with his children is a father with his children.

The Japanese Zen practice of *kinhin* (walking meditation) is mentioned here philosophically: it is the practice of walking slowly with awareness, one step at a time, without a destination in mind. The father's walk with his children can draw on the same spirit — not as a Zen practice to be learned, but as a reminder that walking itself can be practice.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** unsteadiness, fatigue (slowing beyond the walk's natural pace, asking to sit), confusion about where he is, repetitive questions, growing agitation or frustration. **If any appear:** slow down, offer his water, sit on the nearest bench, let the children play nearby for a moment. If the father is not settling, turn back toward the visit space.
- **In either child:** clinginess to the mother, boredom that becomes whining, concerns about the father ("Is Daddy tired?" "Why is Daddy walking slow?"), physical complaints (tummy ache, tired legs). **If any appear:** the walk has reached its natural end. Turn back. A 10-minute walk that ended with everyone present is a successful walk. A 30-minute walk that ended with one child whining is a walk that went too long.
- **In yourself (the mother):** sudden emotional surge (the sight of your family walking together may trigger grief), dissociation from the moment, impatience. **If any appear:** take one slow breath. You do not have to manage your feelings perfectly to lead the walk. Acknowledge the surge silently and keep walking. If the surge is too large to hold, ask the supervisor if they can stand with the father and children for a moment while you step aside.

**What to do when you see these signals:** See `00-crisis-response-card.md` if any crosses into acute territory. Otherwise: slow down, shorten, return.

## Modifications

- **Shorter version (10 minutes):** A walk around a single small loop in the nearest green space. The father and children pick up one or two small things. Return.
- **Lower-energy version (seated):** If the father cannot walk today, sit together on a bench somewhere outdoors and *notice* instead of walking. "What do you hear? What color is that flower? Where is the bird?" The practice is still shared attention, just without locomotion.
- **"Hard day" version:** If the outdoor space is too overwhelming today, do the practice indoors near a window. Look at what's outside. Notice the weather, the sky, the one tree you can see. The sensory orientation is the practice; the outdoors is the easier venue, not a requirement.
- **Wheelchair-accessible version:** Choose paths that are paved and not too hilly; the father's pace is now your pace. Many parks have accessible loops specifically designed for this.

## After

Three-line optional reflection for the mother later that day or the next day:

1. What did the father notice on the walk that I didn't?
2. What did the children do with what he brought them?
3. Did the walk settle us, or did it stretch us?

The findings (leaves, stones) can go into a small keepsake bag for each child if they want to keep them. This becomes an incidental connection-capsule — not a project, just a reminder.

## Traditions honored

- **Japanese Shinrin-yoku** (forest bathing) — the research tradition originating in Japan that established the physiological benefits of time in forested environments. (Reference: Li 2010; Park et al. 2010.)
- **Indigenous philosophical orientation** — *Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ* ("all my relations") as a frame for kinship with the more-than-human world. Reference: Kimmerer, *Braiding Sweetgrass* (2013), as an openly published Potawatomi source. Note: specific Lakota ceremonial practices around land and relations are not drawn on here; this is philosophical orientation only.
- **Zen walking meditation (*kinhin*)** — the practice of walking with awareness, one step at a time, without a destination. Referenced philosophically; not taught as a practice to be learned.

No single tradition is privileged. The walk is a universal human activity. The traditions are named because they each offer a window onto why walking together with attention matters.
