# Shared Drawing — Each Adds

**Configuration:** father-with-kids-supervised
**Level:** deepening
**Duration:** 15–30 minutes
**Energy cost:** low
**Age range:** 5 and 8

## Why this practice

This is a deepening-level practice because it produces an artifact the children can keep, and that low-pressure artifact-creation requires the family to have enough settled rhythm in earlier practices to hold this together. A single large sheet of paper on which everyone adds something — a line, a color, a shape, a small drawing — is collaborative without requiring any single person to produce something meaningful on their own. The father does not have to remember how to draw a specific thing. He can add a squiggle. He can color in a shape a child has started. He can press his palm to the paper and trace his own hand. All of these are contributions.

Phase A synthesis §3.5 on creative engagement in dementia care (Basting, 2009, *Forget Memory*) supports this approach: the measure is presence, not memory; the output is the shared attention, not the aesthetic result.

## You'll need

- One large sheet of paper — big enough that three or four people can work on it from different sides without crowding. A long roll of craft paper is ideal; a large poster board works; even several pieces of standard paper taped together works.
- Crayons, colored pencils, markers, or oil pastels — choose the most mess-tolerant option for the visit location. Crayons are safest for most locations.
- A flat surface — a low table, the floor with a mat, a broad bench
- Optional: a frame or folder at home to keep the finished drawing in
- Tape to hold the paper in place if there's wind (outdoor) or slippage (indoor)

## Before you start

- Pre-set the paper and art materials before the father and children arrive, so the practice can start without planning
- Safety check: washable markers if any surface could be affected; age-appropriate crayons (chunky for 5yo comfort); nothing toxic
- Exit permission: anyone can stop adding whenever they want; a blank corner of the paper is also fine

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Relative:** if the father has a history of being a skilled artist and now struggles to draw at his former level — this practice may surface frustration. Pre-discuss with the care team or family therapist whether it serves him.
- **Caution:** if either child has started using drawing as a high-stakes performance space (art class anxiety, perfectionism), this may import that stress. If so, use it only if the child enters voluntarily.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **Lay the paper flat.** Put the art materials in small bowls or containers so everyone can reach them easily.
2. **Introduce it simply.** "We're going to draw on this paper together. Everyone can add whatever they want. There's no right way to do this."
3. **Start the father with a simple entry.** Hand him a crayon. Suggest (gently, without pressure) "Daddy, you can make the first mark. Or we can make it together." If he hesitates, the mother traces a small line and hands him the crayon.
4. **Everyone adds in parallel.** The 5yo scribbles, colors, makes shapes. The 8yo may draw recognizable things (flowers, a sun, a stick figure). The father adds — whatever he can, however he can. A line. A color fill. A shape. A handprint (he presses his palm flat, the mother traces around it).
5. **Let the drawing evolve.** If a child starts drawing a tree, the father can add leaves. If he adds a circle, a child can add a smile inside it. The drawing is a dialogue in marks.
6. **Pauses are fine.** Quiet work is fine. Conversation is fine ("I like your blue"). Do not require narration.
7. **End at a natural stopping point** — when the paper feels full, or when the children's attention shifts, or when the practice time is up. Do not over-fill the paper or push past the natural ending.
8. **The drawing becomes an artifact.** Decide together what happens to it: stays at the visit location for next time, or the mother takes it home, or one child takes it home. Let the children help decide.

## Holding-parent voice (mother)

The mother's role is to set up, hand the first crayon to the father with gentle framing, and protect him from being asked to produce. She models low-stakes drawing: "I'm going to make a squiggle." She is not making art; she is demonstrating that making marks is allowed.

She may quietly hold a tradition: the Japanese practice of *haiga* (simple brush drawings accompanying poetry) as a philosophical frame — brief, simple, shared. This is not being taught; it is offered as framing for why small marks count.

## Changing-parent voice (father)

The father's drawing does not have to look like anything. A line is a drawing. A splash of color is a drawing. His handprint is a drawing. If he starts to draw something specific and cannot finish, a child can finish it (if the father welcomes that). If he says "I can't draw," the mother says gently, "Just add a line. Any line." Any mark is a contribution.

On a good day, he might draw something recognizable — a sun, a tree, a heart. On a harder day, his contribution may be coloring in a shape a child started. Both are practices of being with his children through their hands on the same paper.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** visible frustration ("I used to be able to draw this"), embarrassment, hesitation to add anything. **If any:** simplify to handprint tracing or color-filling. "Daddy, just color this corner blue for me." A direct concrete task he can succeed at.
- **In either child:** perfectionism surfacing ("I messed up!"), wanting to erase the father's marks, hogging the paper. **If any:** gently remind that the drawing is collaborative. "There's no mess up on this paper. Let's all keep adding."
- **In yourself:** grief at watching what your family was, compared with what it is. Allow it quietly. The drawing is still being made.

## Modifications

- **Shorter (10 minutes):** a smaller sheet, fewer materials, one contribution each.
- **"Hard day":** the father presses his palm flat and the mother traces around it — that's his contribution. The children draw around his handprint. Minimum viable practice.
- **If the location cannot accommodate a large paper:** individual small drawings that the family trades (each person draws one thing, hands it to the next, who adds to it). Round-robin version.
- **For the 8yo:** if she wants more structure, she can lead a theme ("let's all draw things that live in a garden") — the father contributes what he can within the theme.

## After

The drawing is an artifact. Date it on the back, and ideally note who contributed what (if memorable). Over time, a family can accumulate several of these collaborative drawings — a visible record of being together.

This is one of the protocols most directly oriented toward **connection-capsules** (see father-alone directory). The drawings become things the children can look at years from now, and the marks they made alongside their father are real no matter where his cognition is then.

## Traditions honored

- **Universal human practice of collaborative art-making** — from cave paintings to contemporary family art projects
- **Japanese *haiga*** — brief brush drawings with poetry — referenced philosophically as "small marks matter." (Reference: see the tradition of Matsuo Bashō's haiga as brief shared works.)
- **Art therapy research in dementia care** — Anne Basting's work, including the *TimeSlips* creative engagement program, specifically establishes that creative practice without a "right answer" is both protective and generative for people with cognitive change. (Reference: Basting, *Forget Memory*, 2009.)

The practice is offered as a universal form. Nothing about it requires drawing skill or art-world knowledge. The marks are real because they were made together.
