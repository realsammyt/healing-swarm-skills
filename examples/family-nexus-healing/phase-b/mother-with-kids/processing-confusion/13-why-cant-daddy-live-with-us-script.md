# "Why Can't Daddy Live With Us?" — Scripts

**Configuration:** mother-with-kids
**Level:** processing-confusion
**Duration:** 2–5 minutes per conversation (not a long session — short, honest, done)
**Energy cost:** high (emotionally; practically brief)
**Age range:** 5 and 8 (SEPARATE scripts below — the children need different words)

## Why this practice

The question will come. It may come as "why can't Daddy live with us?" or "when is Daddy coming home?" or "why does Daddy live somewhere else?" or "is Daddy going to die?" or "did I make Daddy sick?" Each of these is a variant of the same underlying question: *what is happening to my family, and am I safe?*

The research on children facing parental illness (Rauch & Muriel 2006; Christ 2000) consistently finds that what damages children is not the truth but the *silence, evasion, and disorganization* around the truth. Children who are told age-appropriate truths, said once, calmly, do better across every measured domain than children who are protected from knowing. The silence is worse than the answer.

This protocol provides scripts — not word-for-word prescriptions, but shaped responses the mother can adapt to her own voice. The scripts are said once, calmly, not repeated anxiously. If the child asks again later, the mother repeats the same answer in the same tone. Consistency, not elaboration, is what settles a child.

## You'll need

- A quiet moment (not at bedtime — bedtime is for sleep, not processing; not in the car on the way to a visit — too close to the visit itself; ideally during a calm daytime moment when there is space afterward)
- The mother's own regulated state (if she is dysregulated, she uses `33-fast-reset-sixty-seconds.md` first, then comes back to this conversation)

## Before you start

- The mother does not initiate this conversation unless the child has asked. The scripts are responses, not preemptive talks. Children ask when they are ready. Preemptive explanations often overwhelm.
- Exception: the first time the father's living situation changes, or the first supervised visit, the mother may need to offer a brief frame *before* the child asks. That frame is below under "First-time framing."

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** never at bedtime (the child needs to sleep, not process; if the question comes at bedtime, receive it briefly — "I hear you, and we'll talk about it tomorrow after breakfast" — then return to the bedtime ritual); never when the mother is actively crying or unable to regulate (the child reads the mother's state before the words; regulated first, then conversation)
- **Caution:** never in front of the father during a visit (this is a mother-with-kids conversation, not a family-together one; the father should not witness the children processing his condition in real time — it is not his burden to absorb during his limited visit time); never when the other child is in the middle of their own distress (give each child their own space for this)
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

### First-time framing (when the situation is new or has changed)

If the mother needs to introduce the situation proactively:

**For both children together (or separately, depending on family preference):**

"I want to tell you something about Daddy. Daddy's brain works in a different way now. It's called [progressive cognitive change / a brain illness — the mother chooses her own words]. It means he forgets things sometimes, and he gets confused sometimes, and it's harder for him to take care of you by himself right now. That's why he lives in a different place, and that's why we visit him with [the supervisor's name] there.

It's not because of anything you did. It's not something you can catch, like a cold. It's not something you can fix. And it doesn't mean Daddy doesn't love you — he loves you very much.

We're going to take care of each other. And there are other grown-ups helping too — [name the therapist, the supervisor, the grandmother, whoever is in the circle].

Do you have any questions right now?"

Then STOP. Do not elaborate. Receive whatever comes. If they have no questions, that is fine — they will ask later, when they are ready.

### Script for the 5-year-old boy

**When he asks "Why can't Daddy live with us?"**

"Daddy's brain works in a different way now. It's hard for him to take care of everything by himself. So he lives in his own place where people can help him, and we visit him so you can be together. He still loves you."

**When he asks "Did I make Daddy sick?"**

"No. You did not make Daddy sick. Nothing you did caused this. Nothing you can do will fix it. That's not your job. Your job is to be [his name] and to be five."

**When he asks "Is Daddy going to die?"**

"Everybody's body stops working someday. Right now, Daddy is alive and we get to see him. Nobody knows exactly what will happen. What I know is that we're going to take care of each other."

**When he says something that is factually wrong but emotionally true** ("Daddy was normal yesterday" when the father was confused; "Daddy doesn't love me" when the father didn't recognize him for a moment):

"I hear you. That must have felt really hard. [Name what the child is actually feeling, not what is factually correct.] Daddy loves you even when his brain is working differently."

Do not correct the child's perception. Do not say "no, Daddy was having a hard day." The child is reporting from inside a 5-year-old body. Receive the report.

### Script for the 8-year-old girl

**When she asks "Why can't Daddy live with us?"**

"Daddy's brain is changing in a way that makes it hard for him to take care of himself and of you and your brother the way he used to. So he lives somewhere people can help him, and we visit him with [the supervisor] there to make sure everyone is safe and comfortable. It's not because he did anything wrong, and it's not because we don't love him."

**When she asks "Is it going to get worse?"**

"It might. Nobody knows for sure how it will go. Some days will be better than others. What I can tell you is that we will always have a way to be together, and there are a lot of people helping — [names]. You don't have to figure this out by yourself."

**When she asks "Are you okay, Mommy?"**

This question is a parentification signal. The 8-year-old is monitoring the mother. The answer must be honest AND boundaried:

"I have hard days. But I have help, and I am taking care of myself. You don't need to watch me. Your job is to be eight. My job is to take care of you. I promise I will tell you if I need your help with something specific."

**When she says something that reveals she is carrying too much** ("I need to take care of [brother]" or "I don't want to bother you" or she stops asking for things she needs):

"I hear you. And I want you to know — your brother is my job, not yours. You can love him and play with him, but taking care of him is what I do. You're allowed to need things. You're allowed to ask me. I'm here."

## Holding-parent voice (mother)

The mother's tone is the message as much as the words. The tone should be:
- Matter-of-fact (not dramatic, not weepy, not performing bravery)
- Short (one paragraph, not a lecture)
- Steady (even if she doesn't feel steady — she can be wobbly inside and steady outside; if she cannot be steady outside, she fast-resets first)
- Repeatable (the same words, the same tone, every time the question comes; consistency is what settles the child)

The mother may find herself grieving during or after this conversation. That is expected. The conversation is a grief event for her too. She should not grieve in front of the child during the script, but she may need her own grief window afterward (see `mother-alone/container-practice/29-weekly-grief-window.md`).

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the 5yo:** crying (hold him; the answer was heard and is being processed through the body; do not add more words), asking the same question repeatedly in the same conversation (answer once more with the same words; then: "we can talk about it again tomorrow"), magical-thinking elaboration ("I'll make Daddy better by being good") — gently correct once: "nothing you do will make this different. You are already good enough."
- **In the 8yo:** going very quiet after the conversation (processing; give her space; check in later), becoming angry at the mother ("why did you LET this happen?") — receive the anger without defending: "I hear you. You're angry. That makes sense." Asking for more detail than the mother has ("what exactly is wrong with his brain?") — be honest about what you know and don't know: "I don't have all the answers. Here's what I do know: [repeat the core frame]."
- **In yourself (the mother):** *Tears* while speaking are normal and the children can see that you are human — pause, breathe, resume. **But genuine dysregulation is different from tears.** If you find yourself unable to speak, unable to stop crying, or losing the thread of what you are saying — this is your body telling you to pause. Say: "I need a minute. I love you. We will come back to this." Leave the room briefly if you can. Return within 5 minutes to close the conversation with one sentence: "I'm okay. We can talk more tomorrow." If you cannot return within 5 minutes, send a trusted adult to the child with the same message. **Do not abandon the conversation entirely** without closing it — a child left mid-conversation about a parent's illness will fill the silence with their own worst fears. Even a brief closing ("I love you, we're okay, we'll talk more") is enough.
- **If any child signal sustains beyond this session, see pediatric crisis signals in `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## Modifications

- **If the child asks at a bad time (car, bedtime, in public):** "That's a really important question. Let's talk about it when we get home / tomorrow after breakfast." Then follow through. Do not break the promise.
- **If both children ask at the same time:** answer the question for both, using the simpler version (the 5yo script). The 8yo can be given a private follow-up later with her own version. Do not make the 8yo wait too long.
- **If the mother cannot say the words yet:** a family therapist or trusted adult can do the first conversation with the mother present. The mother's presence matters; her voice can come on the second round.
- **If the father has been told not to discuss the diagnosis with the children (court or care-team guidance):** the mother carries this alone, which is heavy. She should have her own support (therapist, trusted friend) for debriefing after the conversation.

## After

Let the conversation end. Do not follow up immediately with "are you okay?" Let the children return to their activities. Check in later — the `14-how-was-your-heart-today.md` protocol is designed for these quieter follow-up moments.

The mother may want to write one line in a private note about how the conversation went, for her own record and to share with the family therapist at the next session.

## Traditions honored

The practice of telling children the truth about difficult things, with love and without evasion, is universal. No specific tradition is drawn on here because the scripts are written in the family's own voice, not in any tradition's voice. The underpinning is developmental psychology (Rauch & Muriel 2006; Christ 2000) and attachment research (Main, Kaplan & Cassidy 1985 on coherent narrative as protective factor).

The one tradition worth naming: the Jewish concept of *emet* (truth) as a fundamental value — the Talmud teaches that the seal of God is truth (Shabbat 55a), and that truth, even when painful, creates the conditions for trust. The scripts above are an exercise in *emet* adapted for a 5-year-old and an 8-year-old.
