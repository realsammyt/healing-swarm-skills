# "How Was Your Heart Today?"

**Configuration:** mother-with-kids
**Level:** processing-confusion
**Duration:** 1–5 minutes
**Energy cost:** low
**Age range:** 5 and 8

## Why this practice

"How was your day?" asks a child to organize narrative. "How was your heart?" asks a child to check in with themselves. The difference matters. For children carrying the weight of a parent's progressive cognitive change, the narrative-organization question ("what happened?") is often too much. The felt-sense question ("how are you feeling?") is sometimes too abstract. "How was your heart?" sits between: it is concrete enough (the heart is a body part the child can point to) and open enough (the answer can be a word, a color, a gesture, or silence).

This is not an interrogation. It is a daily or weekly touchpoint that the mother offers, the children can accept or decline, and the response is received without judgment or correction. The child who says "my heart was gray today" is not asked to explain why. The answer is the whole answer.

## You'll need

- A quiet moment (after-school decompression is a natural home for this; or after dinner, or during a walk together)
- The mother's capacity to receive whatever comes without trying to fix it

## Before you start

- This is an offering, not a demand. The mother asks; the child is free to shrug, say "fine," say nothing, or say something the mother did not expect.
- The mother does not correct, interpret, or diagnose the child's answer. "Gray" is not a wrong answer. "I don't know" is not a wrong answer. "My heart was punchy" is not a wrong answer.

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** not at bedtime (bedtime is for sleep); not during or immediately after a supervised visit (too raw — use `15-emergency-co-regulation-90-seconds.md` if needed, or let the after-school decompression do its work first)
- **Caution:** not when the mother herself is in a state where she cannot receive hard answers without crumbling — fast-reset first, then ask
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **Ask.** "How was your heart today?" Or a variant that fits the family's language: "How's your heart?" or "What color is your heart right now?" or "If your heart was weather, what would it be?"
2. **Wait.** Do not fill the silence. The child may need 10–30 seconds to check in with themselves. That waiting is the practice.
3. **Receive.** Whatever the child says, receive it with one short acknowledgment:
   - "Thank you for telling me."
   - "I hear you."
   - "That makes sense."
   - A nod. A hand squeeze. A quiet "mm."
4. **Do not follow up unless the child invites it.** If the child says "my heart was sad today" and then goes quiet, the practice is complete. The child has been heard. If the child says "my heart was sad today because of Daddy" and pauses — they may want to say more. Let them. If they do not, say "I'm glad you told me" and let it rest.
5. **The mother may share her own — briefly, to model, not to process.** After the child has answered (or chosen not to), the mother can say: "My heart was [color/weather/word] today." This models that adults have hearts too, and that naming inner states is a normal human activity. Keep it honest but not heavy. "My heart was tired today" is enough. "My heart was drowning" is too much for a child to hold. **If the mother finds herself wanting to say more than one sentence about her own heart, that is information that she needs her own grief window** (see `mother-alone/container-practice/29-weekly-grief-window.md`), **not the children's ears.** The Absolute contraindication from Phase A §5.5 applies here: the children must never become the mother's emotional caregivers.

## Holding-parent voice (mother)

The mother's role is to ask, wait, receive, and not fix. The temptation to fix ("but it was a good day! you had fun at school!") is strong. Resist it. The child's heart may have been gray even though the day was objectively fine. Both can be true. The child learns that inner states are allowed when the mother receives them without correction.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the 5yo:** confusion about the question (reframe: "Is your body feeling happy or sad or something else right now?" — simpler, more concrete); answering "good" every time without variation (may be performing — that's okay; the practice plants the seed; the honest answers come later, on their own timeline).
- **In the 8yo:** using the question to launch a long processing session (gently hold the boundary: "I hear you. Do you want to talk more, or is that enough for now?" — let her choose; if she chooses more, see `13-why-cant-daddy-live-with-us-script.md` for the longer conversation frame); or deflecting with humor every time (also fine — humor is a legitimate coping strategy at 8; the practice plants the seed).
- **In yourself:** if the child's answer surfaces your own grief (especially "my heart misses Daddy"), receive it, acknowledge it, and process your own response later — in your grief window, with a trusted friend, or in a private moment. Do not process your grief through the child's answer. "I miss him too" is honest. Breaking down in tears in response to the child's report is too much to put on the child.
- **If any child signal sustains beyond this session, see pediatric crisis signals in `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## Modifications

- **For the 5yo who cannot use words:** "Show me with your hands — is your heart big or small today?" (Arms wide = big, arms close = small.) Or: "Pick a color for your heart right now." Or: "Draw your heart." (See `each-child-alone/age-5-boy/16-drawing-folder-he-owns.md`.)
- **For the 8yo who wants more structure:** she may want to keep a small "heart weather" journal — one word or one weather symbol per day. See `each-child-alone/age-8-girl/19-simple-prompted-journal.md`.
- **If the mother cannot ask today:** skip it. The practice works cumulatively over weeks. Missing a day does not break it.
- **Weekly version instead of daily:** if daily feels like too much, this can be a weekly check-in — Sunday evening, or after a visit, or whenever the family naturally pauses.

## After

Nothing to process. The check-in is complete when the answer is received. If the child's answer revealed something that needs follow-up (a specific worry, a persistent sadness, a new fear), the mother may want to return to it at a different time using the scripts in `13-why-cant-daddy-live-with-us-script.md`.

## Traditions honored

- **The universal human practice of asking "how are you?" and meaning it** — the heart-check is a refinement of what every caring adult does. It shifts the question from the head (narrative) to the heart (felt-sense), which is a small and powerful move.
- **Contemplative inquiry traditions** — the question "how is your heart?" appears across traditions: the Ignatian Examen's attention to consolation and desolation (Fleming 2008), the Sufi practice of checking the state of the *qalb* (heart), and the Buddhist practice of checking in with the mind-heart (*citta*). Referenced philosophically only. The question itself is universal.
- **Jewish tradition of *emet*** (truth) — the same value that grounds protocol 13 (the scripts). The heart-check honors truth by inviting the child to report honestly from their own interior.

The practice is offered as a universal form. The question is the mother's own — she may find her own words for it. The principle is: ask, wait, receive, do not fix.
