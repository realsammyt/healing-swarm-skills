# After-School Decompression

**Configuration:** mother-with-kids
**Level:** daily-regulation
**Duration:** 15–20 minutes (the first stretch after arriving home from school)
**Energy cost:** low
**Age range:** 5 and 8

## Why this practice

The transition from school to home is one of the highest-demand moments of a child's day. At school, children regulate their behavior for hours. When they walk through the door, they often decompress — loudly, messily, or by going quiet and flat. For children carrying the additional load of a parent with progressive cognitive change, this transition can be especially charged, particularly on visit days or the day after a visit.

The after-school decompression is not a therapy session. It is a deliberate 15-minute window in which the children are allowed to arrive home without being asked questions, given tasks, or processed. The mother provides a sensory anchor (snack, water, a quiet space) and waits. The children's nervous systems do the work.

## You'll need

- A simple snack and water ready when the children arrive (prepared before pickup if possible)
- A quiet-ish space — not necessarily silent, but lower-stimulation than the school day
- 15 minutes where the mother does not ask questions. This is the hardest part.

## Before you start

- The mother prepares the snack before pickup. Having something ready when the children walk in eliminates the "what do you want?" negotiation that burns transition energy.
- No questions for the first 15 minutes after arrival. Not "how was school?" Not "what did you learn?" Not "did anything happen today?" The children will tell you what matters to them when their nervous systems settle. Asking before they've settled forces them to organize an answer before they have the capacity.

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** if the child arrives home in acute distress (crying, inconsolable, reporting something harmful that happened at school) — attend to the distress first. The decompression protocol yields to the child's need for immediate co-regulation.
- **Caution:** on visit days or the day after a visit, the children may arrive home carrying more than usual. The decompression still applies — the 15-minute no-questions window is even more important when the child is carrying visit-related feelings. Do not rush to process the visit. Let the child decompress first.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **Arrive home.** Coats off, shoes off, school bags put down.
2. **Snack and water on the table.** "Here's your snack." No follow-up question.
3. **The 15-minute window.** The mother is present but not asking. She can be in the kitchen, nearby, available. She does not initiate conversation. She does not ask about school. She does not bring up the visit. She does not ask "are you okay?" She is simply there.
4. **What the children do during this window is up to them:**
   - The 5yo may eat silently, then start playing on the floor. This is regulation.
   - The 5yo may start talking unprompted. Receive whatever comes without steering.
   - The 8yo may go to her room and close the door. This is regulation. It is not rejection.
   - The 8yo may want to talk immediately. Listen. Do not redirect to the visit or the father unless she raises it herself.
   - Either child may have a meltdown. This is also regulation — the home is the safe place where the held-together energy of the school day can finally come apart. Hold the meltdown without trying to fix it. Say "I'm here" and wait.
5. **After 15 minutes, one gentle opening.** "How are you doing?" or "How was your heart today?" (borrowing from protocol `14-how-was-your-heart-today.md`). If the child answers, receive it. If the child says "fine" or shrugs, accept it. The decompression has done its work whether or not it produces conversation.
6. **Transition to homework, play, or the next part of the afternoon.** The decompression is over. The day's structure resumes.

## Holding-parent voice (mother)

The mother's role in this practice is to be present without being active. This is harder than it sounds. Her instinct may be to check in, to ask, to process, to make sure the children are okay. The practice asks her to wait. The waiting is itself the gift — it communicates "you don't have to perform for me; you can just be home."

The tradition the mother may draw on: the Quaker practice of "expectant waiting" (Pink Dandelion 2008) — sitting in silence without an agenda, trusting that what needs to arise will arise. Or the Zen concept of *mu* (not-doing, not-forcing) — being present without acting. Or simply: the secular humanist recognition that rest is not laziness; it is restoration.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the 5yo:** meltdown that escalates beyond what a held "I'm here" can settle (tantrum lasting more than 15 minutes without any settling, physical aggression, self-harm). If this happens, the decompression yields to co-regulation: hold the child, slow breath together, and if the distress does not settle, see `15-emergency-co-regulation-90-seconds.md`.
- **In the 8yo:** completely silent withdrawal sustained across the afternoon (not just 15 minutes of quiet, but hours of not speaking, not eating, not engaging). A single quiet afternoon is normal; if it sustains across days, see `00-crisis-response-card.md` pediatric crisis signals.
- **In yourself (the mother):** anxiety about the silence (wanting to fill it, wanting to check, wanting to ask). The anxiety is information about your own state, not about the children's. Take a breath. The silence is the practice. If the anxiety sustains and you cannot be present without asking, you may need your own fast-reset first (see `mother-alone/re-centering/33-fast-reset-sixty-seconds.md`).
- **If any child signal sustains beyond this session, see pediatric crisis signals in `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## Modifications

- **Shorter (5 minutes):** Snack on the table. No questions for 5 minutes. Then gently open. Even 5 minutes of no-questions is better than immediate interrogation.
- **Visit-day variant:** On visit days, the decompression happens both before the visit (prep — see `father-with-kids-supervised/entry/01-beginning-ritual-quiet-arrival.md`) and after the visit (post-visit). The post-visit decompression may need to be longer (20–30 minutes) and may include physical activity (a walk, jumping on a trampoline, running in the yard) before any verbal processing. The body needs to discharge before the mind can organize.
- **If the mother picks up at school and the children talk in the car:** let them talk. The car ride is a natural decompression space (no eye contact required; motion is regulating). When they arrive home, the 15-minute window still applies — the transition from car to home is its own moment.

## After

The decompression is over. The afternoon's structure begins (homework, play, activities). If either child raised something about the father or about feelings during the decompression, the mother may want to return to it later in a more structured way — see `13-why-cant-daddy-live-with-us-script.md` or `14-how-was-your-heart-today.md` for those conversations.

## Traditions honored

- **Quaker expectant waiting** — sitting in silence without an agenda, trusting what arises. (Reference: Pink Dandelion, *The Quakers: A Very Short Introduction*, 2008.)
- **Zen concept of *mu*** — not-doing, not-forcing — referenced philosophically as permission to be present without acting. (Reference: philosophical concept in Zen Buddhism, not a specific practice being taught.)
- **Universal parenting wisdom** — the practice of not asking a child "how was your day?" the moment they walk through the door is recommended by developmental psychologists across frameworks. The 15-minute buffer is grounded in practical observation of child regulation patterns.

The practice is offered as a universal form. Every parent who has ever watched a child decompress after school already knows this rhythm. The kit's contribution is: name it, protect it, and don't let the family's larger stress compress it out of existence.
