# Emergency Co-Regulation — 90 Seconds

**Configuration:** mother-with-kids
**Level:** grief-windows (emergency use)
**Duration:** 90 seconds
**Energy cost:** low (body-based; no thinking required)
**Age range:** 5 and 8 (and the mother herself)

## Why this practice

This is the fast-acting co-regulation practice for the whole family in hot moments — when the children are dysregulated after a visit, when a meltdown is escalating, when the mother is at the edge and needs to settle herself AND the children simultaneously, when something has just happened and everyone's nervous systems are activated.

The polyvagal research (Porges 2011; Dana 2018, 2020) describes how nervous systems regulate each other through face, voice, and breath. When one person in the room is settled, it creates the conditions for others to settle. The mother does not have to be fully regulated to lead this practice — she only has to be regulated enough to start. The practice itself does the rest.

90 seconds is chosen deliberately. Research on cortisol response curves suggests that a physiological stress response, once activated, begins to de-escalate within 60–90 seconds if the activation source stops and a calming signal is introduced. 90 seconds is long enough to shift the nervous system and short enough that a 5-year-old can tolerate it.

## You'll need

- Nothing. No materials. No preparation. This practice works standing in the kitchen, sitting on the floor, in the car, at the visit location, anywhere.
- The mother's body. The children's bodies. Proximity (touching or nearly touching).

## Before you start

- No setup. This is an emergency practice. It starts when it is needed.
- The mother does not explain what is about to happen. She does it. The explanation, if any, can come after.

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **This practice has almost no contraindications.** It is the floor of the kit — the practice that holds when nothing else does.
- **The one exception:** if a child is in a state of physical aggression (hitting, kicking, throwing things AT someone), the mother may need to physically hold or redirect the child before co-regulation can begin. Co-regulation works only when the body is no longer fighting.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.** If the mother or a child is in acute crisis (self-harm, inability to function, thoughts of harm), the 90-second practice may help as a bridge to the escalation path, but it is not a substitute for crisis support.

## The practice

1. **Get close.** The mother moves physically close to both children. If they are in different rooms, she brings them together or goes to the one most activated. She sits on the floor if they are on the floor. She kneels if they are standing. She matches their level.
2. **Touch.** One hand on one child, one hand on the other. On the back, the shoulder, the arm, the knee — wherever the child will accept touch. If a child rejects touch right now, the mother puts her hand on her own knee near the child. Proximity without contact is still co-regulation.
3. **Hum.** The mother starts humming. Not a specific song — a low, steady hum at a comfortable pitch. The vibration in her chest and throat sends a vagal signal to her own nervous system AND to any child whose body is touching hers. The children do not have to hum back. Many will start automatically.
4. **Breathe.** While humming, the mother's exhale is long (the hum IS a long exhale). After 3–4 hums, she may shift to audible breathing — a slow exhale through the mouth, like a sigh. The children's breathing will start to entrain (match) hers within about 30 seconds. This is automatic and does not require their cooperation.
5. **Count silently to 90.** Or just stay in the hum/breathe cycle until the room settles. The mother does not talk. She does not explain. She does not ask what happened. She hums, breathes, touches.
6. **When the 90 seconds are up** (or when the room has visibly settled — whichever comes first), the mother says one quiet sentence: "I'm here. We're okay." Then she waits another 10 seconds. Then normal life can resume — a glass of water, a quiet activity, a return to whatever was happening before the activation.

## Holding-parent voice (mother)

The mother is the ventral vagal anchor in this practice. She does not have to feel calm. She has to ACT calm — specifically, she has to hum and breathe slowly, which will generate the physiological signals of calm even if her mind is racing. The body leads the mind here, not the other way around. Within about 30 seconds, her own nervous system will begin to settle in response to her own humming and slow exhale. This is the co-regulation paradox: the act of offering regulation to the children IS the act of generating regulation in herself.

If the mother has a tradition she draws on: the Jewish practice of *niggun* (wordless melody) IS humming. The Buddhist practice of *om* chanting IS a sustained hum. The Christian monastic practice of drone-singing IS a held tone. The secular practice of "humming to calm down" IS the same physiology without a frame. All are available. None is required.

## Changing-parent voice (father)

The father is not present for this practice. However, if a hot moment happens during a supervised visit, a simplified version can be used: the mother sits near the children and the father, hums for 30 seconds, and offers one phrase: "We're okay. Let's be quiet for a minute." The supervisor can assist by being still. If the father himself is activated, the supervisor may need to gently redirect while the mother holds the children.

## Signals to slow down or stop

This practice does not have "stop" signals in the usual sense — it IS the stop signal for everything else. However:

- **If a child pushes away from the mother's touch:** do not force it. Hum without touch. Stay close. The child may return to contact after 20–30 seconds.
- **If the hum makes a child more distressed:** (rare but possible — some children find humming overstimulating when very activated) switch to silent slow breathing only. Or whisper "shhh, shhh, shhh" rhythmically — same vagal pathway, different sound.
- **If the mother cannot hum because she is crying:** *quiet, steady tears with a long exhale* are compatible with the practice — crying IS a long exhale with vocalization, and the children will feel the rhythm and begin to entrain. This is not failure; this is the practice working through tears. **However, heaving sobs or hyperventilation are NOT compatible** — sobs replace the long exhale with short gasps, defeating the vagal mechanism and potentially dysregulating the children further. If the mother's crying escalates from quiet tears to sobs: pause the hum, take one slow exhale through the mouth, attempt to restart the hum at a lower volume. If she cannot restart, use proximity and touch only (no vocalization) — hold the children close, breathe slowly, let the body do the settling. After the moment passes, she should seek her own co-regulation support (see `mother-alone/re-centering/33-fast-reset-sixty-seconds.md` or `34-im-at-the-edge-escalation.md`). If the sobbing does not settle and the children's distress is escalating, activate the crisis response path (`00-crisis-response-card.md`) — call the trusted contact to come NOW.
- **If 90 seconds is not enough:** do another 90 seconds. Or 3 minutes. Or as long as it takes. There is no upper limit on co-regulation.
- **If the activation does not settle after 5 minutes of continuous humming/breathing:** the situation may require more than co-regulation. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.
- **If any child signal sustains beyond this session, see pediatric crisis signals in `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## Modifications

- **30-second version (absolute minimum):** Sit. Touch. Three long exhales. "I'm here." Done.
- **In the car:** no touch needed (children are in car seats). The mother hums or breathes audibly. The enclosed space amplifies the sound. The children's nervous systems will respond.
- **At the visit location:** the mother, the children, and optionally the supervisor. The mother sits, gathers the children, hums. The supervisor stays still. The father may or may not be present; if he is, his body's presence (sitting quietly nearby) is itself part of the co-regulation field.
- **Mother alone (no children present):** the same practice works for the mother by herself. Sit. Hum. 90 seconds. This is a fast-reset she can use in any moment — see `mother-alone/re-centering/33-fast-reset-sixty-seconds.md` for the solo version.
- **For the 8yo who finds humming weird:** she does not have to hum. She can sit close, breathe, be still. Her nervous system will entrain to the mother's even without active participation. Tell her once: "You don't have to do anything. Just sit close for a minute."

## After

One glass of water for everyone. Then whatever was supposed to happen next — or a quiet 10 minutes before resuming. The practice does not require debriefing. The body has done the work. If the child wants to talk about what happened, the `14-how-was-your-heart-today.md` check-in can happen later, not immediately.

## Traditions honored

- **Polyvagal theory** — Stephen Porges (2011) and Deb Dana (2018, 2020) — the scientific framework grounding why humming, long exhales, and touch produce nervous-system settling. Referenced as research, not as tradition.
- **Jewish *niggun*** — wordless melody hummed in community, particularly Hasidic. Referenced philosophically: the mother's hum is structurally a niggun. (Reference: Hasidic musical tradition; widely shared.)
- **Buddhist *om*** — sustained vocalization as meditation anchor. Referenced philosophically. (Reference: mainstream Buddhist and Hindu practice.)
- **Christian drone-singing** — monastic chant traditions (Gregorian, Taizé). Referenced philosophically. (Reference: Taizé community music; Gregorian chant tradition.)
- **Universal human humming** — every culture hums to soothe. Lullabies across all traditions are essentially structured humming with words. This practice strips the words and returns to the hum.

The practice is offered as a universal form. No tradition is required. The hum is the tradition — it is older than any lineage and belongs to every body that has a voice.
