# Crisis Response Card

*Read this once before using any practice in the kit. Read it again when needed.*

**Crisis response takes precedence over every other practice in this kit.** If anyone in the family is in acute distress, the practices here yield. The family's existing care team, local crisis services, and the escalation paths below come first. Always.

---

## The framework — RECOGNIZE · STOP · GROUND · ASSESS · RESPOND · FOLLOW-UP

**RECOGNIZE** — notice the signals (see below for adult and pediatric signals).
**STOP** — pause whatever practice is happening. Stopping is always a win, never a failure.
**GROUND** — return attention to body, breath, safe sensory anchors (water, touch, a known song).
**ASSESS** — ask: is this within the range of ordinary distress that will settle, or is professional help needed now?
**RESPOND** — match the response to the assessment. Continue gently if it will settle. Pause the practice if it will not. Escalate if the signals are acute.
**FOLLOW-UP** — later, check in. Notice what the signals were. Adjust future practice.

---

## Immediate escalation paths — know these before you need them

**988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** — call or text **988** (US). Active 24/7. For anyone in mental health crisis, including caregivers, people with a diagnosis, children, and teens. The counselors are trained in complicated grief, caregiver collapse, and younger-onset dementia situations.

**Local crisis / mobile crisis line** — identify and save your city's local mobile crisis or emergency mental health contact BEFORE it is needed. Put it in this card and on the refrigerator.

**Your written trusted contact** — one named person (family, friend, neighbor, faith community member) you have agreed on in advance will come when called. Put their name and number here:

## Pediatric crisis signals (ages 5–8)

These are signals that a child needs additional adult support beyond what home practices can provide. Any one of them, sustained, is a reason to escalate — not an emergency by itself unless accompanied by acute statements of self-harm.

- **Persistent nightmares** more than 2 weeks without easing
- **New school refusal** or significant shift in school functioning
- **Any statement about wanting to disappear, go away, or not exist** — escalate immediately to pediatrician and school counselor
- **Self-harm behavior** — hitting self, picking at skin to injury, pulling hair — escalate immediately
- **Dissociation or freeze responses** during or after visits, sustained beyond the visit itself
- **Significant regression** sustained beyond a few days — earlier toileting patterns, baby-voice, sleep severely disrupted
- **Withdrawal from previously loved activities** sustained beyond a week

These are NOT ordinary stress responses in the acute-and-then-settle pattern. They are signals that the child's container needs reinforcement beyond what the mother and the kit can provide alone.

---

## Adult crisis signals (mother or father)

Any of these is a reason to activate the escalation path immediately:

- **Any thoughts of self-harm or harm to others** — call 988 or local crisis line now
- **Inability to care for the children** — call your trusted contact and/or family therapist now
- **Dissociation sustained across hours** — call your trusted contact
- **Acute hopelessness that does not respond to ordinary supports** — escalate
- **In the father specifically: any crisis post-diagnosis or at a capability-loss transition** — younger-onset dementia carries elevated suicide risk in the post-diagnosis window and at major capability-loss transitions. Named here because it is load-bearing: the father's care team should be informed immediately if any acute distress appears, and the family should not attempt to absorb it privately.

---

## What to do if a child shows acute distress DURING or AFTER a supervised visit

1. **Stop the visit** or end it early. A visit that ends well five minutes early is a successful visit. The supervisor can assist with closure.
2. **Co-regulate with the child** — held body, slow breath together, a known song, a quiet room.
3. **Do not interrogate.** Do not ask "what happened?" until they have settled. Receive what they offer in their own time.
4. **If the distress is acute** (self-harm, dissociation, freeze, statements of wanting to disappear), call the pediatrician and, if needed, 988 or your local pediatric crisis line.
5. **Tell the visit supervisor** what happened if they do not already know. The supervisor is part of the care team, not an obstacle.
6. **Call the family therapist** within 24 hours to talk about what happened.
7. **Write it down briefly** — what signals appeared, what you did, what helped — so next time, the pattern is known.

---

## Permission, explicit

You are allowed to stop any practice at any time. You are allowed to call for help. You are allowed to ask someone else to hold the children while you regulate yourself. You are allowed to end a supervised visit early. You are allowed to put this kit down and come back to it another day.

Stopping is never a failure. Reaching out is never a weakness. The edge is not the end — it is information.
