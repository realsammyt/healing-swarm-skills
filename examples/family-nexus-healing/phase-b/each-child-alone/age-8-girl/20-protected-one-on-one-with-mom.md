# Protected One-on-One with Mom

**Configuration:** each-child-alone / age-8-girl
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 30–60 minutes per week
**Energy cost:** medium
**Age range:** 8 (specifically)

## Why this practice

The 8-year-old girl in this family carries a specific risk: parentification. Under stress, she may become the watcher — monitoring her mother's emotional state, caring for her 5-year-old brother, suppressing her own needs to avoid burdening the adults (Chase 1999; Earley & Cushway 2002). One of the most direct protections against parentification is protected time with her mother that is *not about Daddy, not about her brother, not about the family situation*. Just her and her mom, doing something she chose, with her mother's undivided attention.

This practice exists to communicate one message: "You matter. Not as a helper. Not as a second parent. As an 8-year-old girl whose mother wants to be with her."

## You'll need

- 30–60 minutes per week (scheduled, predictable, not cancelled except for genuine emergency — the predictability is the point)
- An activity the 8-year-old chooses (not the mother's choice, not an educational activity, not something "good for her" — HER choice: baking cookies, going to the library, painting nails, a walk to the ice cream shop, a board game, lying on the couch reading side by side)
- The 5-year-old is NOT PRESENT. He is with another trusted adult — grandmother, babysitter, neighbor, or at a playdate. The mother's attention during this hour is not divided.
- The mother's phone is away. Not on silent. Away.

## Before you start

- The mother introduces this practice clearly: "Every [Saturday morning / Wednesday after school / whenever fits], you and I are going to have our time. Just us. You pick what we do. Your brother will be with [trusted adult]. This is your time."
- The child picks the activity. If she cannot decide, the mother offers three choices and she picks one. The mother does not steer toward anything therapeutic, educational, or family-related.
- **The mother does not bring up Daddy during this time unless the child does.** If the child brings up Daddy, the mother receives it (per the scripts in `13-why-cant-daddy-live-with-us-script.md`) but does not redirect the whole hour toward processing. The hour is *hers*, not the family's.

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** the practice is NOT cancelled for convenience. It is only cancelled for genuine emergency (illness, safety issue). If cancelled, it is rescheduled within the same week and the child is told when. Cancelling this practice repeatedly teaches the child that her needs are the first to be set aside — which is exactly the parentification message this practice exists to prevent.
- **Caution:** if the child consistently chooses activities where she "takes care of" the mother (baking FOR the family, cleaning the house together, helping with errands), the mother gently redirects: "this is YOUR time. What do you want to do that's just for you?" Caretaking during protected one-on-one is a parentification signal, not a preference.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **At the scheduled time, the 5yo goes to the trusted adult.** The mother and the 8yo are alone.
2. **"What do you want to do today?"** The child decides. The mother follows.
3. **Be together.** No agenda. No outcome. No teaching. Just be with her. If she wants to talk, listen. If she wants to be quiet, be quiet with her. If she wants to be silly, be silly. If she cries, hold her. The hour is hers.
4. **Do not bring up Daddy, the visits, her brother, or the family situation** unless she does. If she does, receive it and then gently check: "Do you want to keep talking about this, or do you want to go back to [what we were doing]?" Let her choose.
5. **End at the agreed time.** "That was our time. I loved being with you. Same time next [day]." The closing is warm, brief, and carries the promise of next time.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **The child declines the practice** ("I don't want to do our time this week"): accept it once. If it repeats, gently explore: "Is everything okay? You don't have to tell me, but I want you to know I love our time together." If she is declining because she feels she should be watching her brother or helping the mother, that is a parentification signal to address directly: "Your brother is safe with [trusted adult]. This time is yours."
- **The child uses the practice to process heavy content** (disclosure of something at school, sudden grief about the father, a hard question): receive it. The one-on-one is a safe space for this. But do not let the processing consume every week — if the child consistently uses the hour for grief work, she may need a separate processing channel (family therapist, the heart-check in `14-how-was-your-heart-today.md`), and the one-on-one should be protected as a joy-and-connection space.
- **The mother finds the practice emotionally difficult** (grief at the comparison between this protected hour and the intact-family life she imagined): this is expected. The mother processes her own grief in her own channels (grief window, therapist, peer), not during the child's protected time.
- **If any signal sustains beyond this session, see pediatric crisis signals in `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## Modifications

- **Shorter (20 minutes):** if 60 minutes is genuinely impossible to schedule weekly, 20 focused minutes is better than no protected time. The minimum viable practice is: the 5yo is elsewhere, the phone is away, and the mother's attention is undivided for 20 minutes.
- **If no trusted adult is available for the 5yo:** the practice can happen after the 5yo's bedtime — the mother and the 8yo stay up 30 minutes later together. Not ideal (the 8yo is tired too) but functional.
- **If the child wants to do something the mother does not enjoy:** do it anyway. This is her hour. The mother's job is to be present, not to be entertained.
- **If the child wants to do nothing:** sit together. Read side by side. Lie on the couch. Doing nothing together is a complete practice.

## After

No debrief. No journal. No report. The mother privately notes how the practice went (for herself and the family therapist), but the child does not generate output. The practice's value is in the experience of being chosen, being seen, and being allowed to be eight.

## Traditions honored

- **The universal parenting practice of dedicated attention** — every culture recognizes that children need to be seen individually, not only as part of a group. The practice of "special time" or "one-on-one time" is recommended across developmental-psychology frameworks (Ginott, Gordon, Siegel & Bryson).
- **Ubuntu philosophy** — "I am because we are" — the child's personhood is relational, and the one-on-one with her mother is where her own personhood (not her role as helper or watcher) is confirmed.
- **Winnicott's holding environment** — the practice IS the holding environment for this child. The mother's undivided attention, predictably given, is the structure that allows the child's interior life to develop. (Reference: Winnicott, *Playing and Reality*, 1971.)

The practice is offered as a universal form. Every mother and every daughter already know this practice in some version. The kit's contribution is: protect it, schedule it, and do not let the family's crisis compress it out of existence.
