# Externalizing the Forgetting

**Configuration:** each-child-alone / age-8-girl
**Level:** middle (requires the child to have some stability first — not a first-week practice)
**Duration:** 5–15 minutes (a single conversation, repeated as needed)
**Energy cost:** medium (conceptually; practically brief)
**Age range:** 8 (specifically; adaptable to younger children only with therapist guidance)

## Why this practice

Narrative therapy's central move — **externalizing the problem** — reframes a difficulty as something separate from the person, something the person is in relationship with rather than identical to (White & Epston 1990). For this family, the move is explicit: *the Forgetting* is not Daddy. The Forgetting is something that is happening to Daddy, that the family is living alongside.

This grammatical shift gives the child a relationship-with rather than a fusion-with. She can love Daddy AND dislike the Forgetting in the same sentence. She can be angry at the Forgetting without being angry at Daddy. She can notice when the Forgetting is stronger and when it is weaker without feeling that Daddy himself is appearing and disappearing. The externalization creates space between the person and the condition — space the child can breathe in.

The child-focused narrative therapy literature (Freeman, Epston & Lobovits 1997) provides many examples of children working with externalized "problems" through naming, drawing, and storytelling. At 8, the child is developmentally ready for this — she can hold metaphor, understand cause and effect, and use language to reshape her experience.

## You'll need

- A quiet moment between the mother and the child — not at bedtime, not during a visit, not in crisis. A calm daytime moment.
- The mother's own clarity about the concept (she may want to practice the framing with a friend or therapist before offering it to the child)
- Optional: paper and crayons if the child wants to draw the Forgetting

## Before you start

- This is offered once, gently, and then the child decides whether to use it. If the concept does not land, the mother does not push. Some children find it helpful immediately; some return to it months later; some never use it. All are fine.
- The mother should have processed her own relationship with the concept first. If she finds the externalization painful or confusing, she should explore it with the family therapist before offering it to the child.
- This practice is NOT recommended as a first-week activity. The child needs some stability (stable bedtime, stable one-on-one time, at least a few settled visits) before being offered a conceptual reframe. The reframe is not urgent; it is a middle-level tool.

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** not with the 5-year-old without therapist guidance — the 5yo's preoperational thinking may confuse the externalization with magical thinking ("the Forgetting is a monster I can fight"), which could increase anxiety rather than reduce it. The externalization is designed for the 8yo's emerging concrete-operational capacity. If the 5yo overhears and asks, a simple version is possible: "Daddy's brain has something called the Forgetting. It's not Daddy's fault and it's not your fault." But do not elaborate without professional guidance.
- **Caution:** if the child becomes anxious about the Forgetting as a "thing" (imagining it as a real entity, having nightmares about it), the externalization is not serving her and should be retired. Return to simpler framing: "Daddy's brain works differently now."
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **The mother introduces the concept.** At a calm moment, when the child is receptive:

   "I want to tell you something that might help us talk about what's happening with Daddy. You know how Daddy's brain works differently now — how he forgets things and gets confused sometimes? Some people call that 'the Forgetting.' The Forgetting is something that's happening TO Daddy. It's not WHO Daddy is. Daddy is still Daddy — he still loves you, he still thinks you're amazing, he's still your dad. The Forgetting is like... [choose a metaphor that fits the child's world]:
   - ...like fog that comes and goes. Sometimes the fog is thick and Daddy can't see through it. Sometimes the fog lifts and Daddy is right there, clear as day.
   - ...like a bully that picks on Daddy's brain. The bully makes it hard for Daddy to remember things. But the bully is not Daddy.
   - ...like a glitch in a video game. The game is still the game, but sometimes it glitches."

2. **Wait.** Let the child respond however she responds. She may:
   - Ask questions ("Can we get rid of the Forgetting?" — "The doctors are trying to help. We can't get rid of it, but we can learn to live with it.")
   - Be silent (processing)
   - Cry (hold her)
   - Reject it ("That's dumb") — accept the rejection without defending the concept. It may land later.
   - Add to it ("I think the Forgetting is like a cloud" — "Tell me more about that.")

3. **If the concept lands,** the mother can use it going forward:
   - Before a visit: "The Forgetting might be strong today, or it might be quiet. We'll see."
   - After a visit: "How was the Forgetting today?" (This is different from "how was Daddy?" — it separates the condition from the person.)
   - In general conversation: "The Forgetting was making it hard for Daddy to find words today. But did you see how he smiled when you sang?"

4. **If the child wants to draw the Forgetting:** let her. The drawing externalizes the externalization — it gives the concept a visual form the child controls. She can draw it as a fog, a cloud, a monster, a glitch, a gray blob. The drawing goes in her journal or her drawing folder if she wants.

5. **The concept is hers to use or not.** The mother does not force the language. If the child prefers "Daddy's brain thing" or "the stuff that makes Daddy confused" or her own name for it, that is her externalization and it works just as well.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **The child becomes anxious about the Forgetting as a real entity** (scared of it, nightmares about it, believing she can "catch" it): the metaphor is not serving her. Retire it gently: "You know what, we don't have to call it that. Daddy's brain just works differently, and that's okay to say."
- **The child uses the Forgetting to express anger at the father** ("I hate the Forgetting AND I hate Daddy"): this is okay in the short term. The child is allowed to be angry. The mother can gently separate: "It makes sense to be angry at the Forgetting. And Daddy is still Daddy underneath it." If the anger at the father specifically sustains and intensifies, loop in the family therapist.
- **The 5yo hears the concept and becomes confused or frightened:** redirect to simpler language for the younger child. The externalization belongs to the 8yo's cognitive world.
- **If any signal sustains beyond this session, see pediatric crisis signals in `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## Modifications

- **If the child prefers a different name:** the concept does not have to be called "the Forgetting." It can be "the Fog," "the Glitch," "the Thing," "the Brain Bully," or whatever the child names it. The naming is itself part of the externalization — she chooses what to call it, which gives her agency over the concept.
- **If the mother prefers not to use externalization:** the scripts in `13-why-cant-daddy-live-with-us-script.md` work without it. The externalization is an additional tool, not a required frame.
- **For use with the family therapist:** the therapist may want to develop the externalization further in sessions — drawing the Forgetting, writing letters to the Forgetting, having the child interview the Forgetting. These are narrative-therapy techniques that work best with professional guidance (Freeman, Epston & Lobovits 1997). The mother's role at home is the simpler version: use the language if the child uses it, and do not push.

## After

The concept enters the family's language organically if it resonates. "How was the Forgetting today?" becomes a normal question. The mother does not debrief the conversation; she lets the language do its work over time.

## Traditions honored

- **Narrative therapy** (White & Epston 1990; Freeman, Epston & Lobovits 1997) — the externalization technique is drawn directly from this therapeutic tradition, which is open, published, and widely taught to clinicians and educators.
- **Universal storytelling** — the practice of naming a difficulty as a character (the dragon, the wolf, the storm) is among the oldest narrative devices in human storytelling. Every tradition has monsters. The child who names her father's condition as "the Forgetting" joins a very old human practice of making the unspeakable speakable by giving it a name.

The practice is offered as a universal form, grounded in specific clinical technique (narrative therapy) and the universal human practice of naming the unnamed.
