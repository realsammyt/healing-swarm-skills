# Sensory Bin — Solo

**Configuration:** each-child-alone / age-5-boy
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 5–20 minutes (child-led)
**Energy cost:** low
**Age range:** 5 (specifically)

## Why this practice

The sensory bin appears in the father-with-kids-supervised configuration as a shared activity (`05-sensory-bin-play.md`). This solo version serves a different purpose: it is a self-regulation tool the 5-year-old can reach for on his own, without needing an adult to initiate. When his body is activated — after a visit, after a hard moment, after waking from a nightmare, or just because 5-year-old nervous systems run hot — the bin gives his hands something to do and his attention something to settle on.

The sensory engagement is the regulation mechanism. He does not have to name what he feels. He pours, stirs, scoops, buries, finds. His nervous system does the rest.

## You'll need

- A small bin (shoebox-sized for solo use) with filler: dry rice, dry lentils, smooth small stones, kinetic sand
- 2–3 small scoops, cups, or spoons
- Optional: 2–3 small figurines or smooth objects to bury and find
- A mat or towel underneath for easy cleanup
- A dedicated shelf or basket where the bin lives — accessible to the child without asking permission
- **Safety: nothing smaller than 3.2 cm (toilet-paper tube / small-parts cylinder standard). NO water beads (Absolute — recall/ingestion hazard). Adult within earshot when bin is in use; adult within arm's reach if the child has any history of putting small items in his mouth.**

## Before you start

- The mother sets up the bin once and shows the child where it lives: "This is your sensory bin. You can use it whenever you want. When you're done, put the lid on." That is the entire instruction.
- The child does not need permission to use the bin. If the mother notices him reaching for it at a particular moment (after a visit, after a nightmare), she notes it silently. The child is self-regulating.

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** the child puts filler in his mouth — stop, redirect, and assess whether this bin is appropriate for him right now. If hand-to-mouth behavior persists, replace the bin with a water-play version (a bowl of warm water with cups — no choking hazard).
- **Caution:** if the child uses the bin to throw filler at people or furniture, the practice has shifted from regulation to dysregulation — the bin is paused for the day and a co-regulation practice (`15-emergency-co-regulation-90-seconds.md`) may be needed first.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **The child goes to the bin when he wants to.** No adult initiates.
2. **He plays.** Pours, stirs, scoops, buries, finds. No goal. No right way.
3. **The mother is nearby but not directing.** She does not ask "what are you making?" She does not join unless invited. She is available.
4. **He stops when he stops.** Puts the lid on. The practice is done.
5. **Cleanup** is putting the lid on and sweeping any spilled filler with a small brush he keeps next to the bin. The cleanup is part of the practice — it is a small concrete action that closes the cycle.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **Escalating energy** (throwing filler, slamming cups, yelling at the bin): the regulation has tipped into dysregulation. The mother gently says "let's close the bin for now" and offers an alternative (held body, a walk outside, the 90-second co-regulation hum).
- **Hand-to-mouth behavior:** immediate redirect. If persistent, swap to water play.
- **Complete disinterest over weeks:** fine. Not every child regulates through tactile play. The bin waits. Other practices carry the work.
- **If any signal sustains beyond this session, see pediatric crisis signals in `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## Modifications

- **Water version (no choking concern):** a small bowl of warm water with 2 cups and a sponge. Same regulation principle, safer for persistent hand-to-mouth children.
- **Outdoor version:** a patch of dirt, sand, or gravel in the yard. The earth IS a sensory bin. A stick and a small bucket are the tools.
- **Bedtime version:** a very small bag of smooth stones (5–6 stones, each larger than 4 cm) that the child holds and sorts in bed before sleep. Calming, tactile, no filler to spill.

## After

Nothing to process. The bin does its work through the hands. If the mother wants to note privately when the child reaches for the bin and what preceded it (visit day, nightmare, school stress), that pattern information may be useful for the family therapist over time.

## Traditions honored

- **Early childhood education** — Montessori and Reggio Emilia sensory-exploration traditions
- **Snoezelen principle** — low-demand sensory engagement as regulation (Hulsegge & Verheul 1987)

The practice is offered as a universal form. Every child who has ever dug in sand or stirred in a bowl already knows this practice. The kit's contribution is: name it as a regulation tool, make it accessible, and protect it.
