# Drawing Folder He Owns

**Configuration:** each-child-alone / age-5-boy
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 5–15 minutes (whenever he picks it up)
**Energy cost:** low
**Age range:** 5 (specifically)

## Why this practice

At five, a child's primary expression modes are felt-sense, body, play, and drawing — not words. He does not yet have the narrative scaffolding to tell the story of what he feels; he will draw it, play it, or enact it through his body (Christ 2000; Cait 2005). Drawing-as-writing adapts Pennebaker's expressive writing paradigm (Pennebaker 1997; Fivush et al. 2007) to the developmental stage where pencil-on-paper IS the language.

The critical design principle: **the folder is HIS**. He controls what goes in, what comes out, what is shown, and what is kept private. The mother does not interpret the drawings back to him. She does not correct them, grade them, or display them without his permission. The folder is his interior life in visible form, and its privacy is his to manage.

## You'll need

- A folder, binder, or large envelope that is clearly his (his name on it, his chosen color, his sticker — whatever makes it his)
- Paper (blank, not lined — lines restrict drawing)
- Crayons, colored pencils, or markers (non-toxic, ASTM D-4236; chunky crayons for small hands)
- A place in the house where the folder lives that he can reach himself

## Before you start

- The mother introduces the folder once: "This is your drawing folder. You can draw in it whenever you want. Whatever you draw is yours. You can show me if you want, or keep it private." Then she steps back.
- The mother does NOT say "draw your feelings" or "draw about Daddy." The folder is unprompted. If the child draws about Daddy, he is choosing to. If he draws trucks, he is choosing to. Both are the practice working.

## ⚠ When NOT to use this

- **Absolute:** do not use the drawings as diagnostic material without a child therapist's guidance — a 5-year-old's drawing of a dark sky is not necessarily a distress signal; it may be a dark sky. The mother watches patterns over weeks, not individual drawings.
- **Caution:** do not ask the child to draw "what happened at the visit today" — that turns the folder into an assignment. If he draws it unprompted, receive it without interpretation. If he does not, the folder is not failing.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **The folder is available.** It lives where the child can reach it — on a low shelf, in a basket by his bed, on the kitchen table during drawing time.
2. **The child draws when he wants to.** Not scheduled. Not prompted. Not assigned.
3. **If the mother is present when he draws:** she sits nearby, available, not directing. She may draw her own picture alongside him (parallel drawing, like parallel play — the grown-up version of "I'm doing this too"). She does not ask "what are you drawing?" unless he invites her to look.
4. **If he shows her a drawing:** she receives it. "Tell me about this" is better than "what is this?" (the first invites; the second demands). If he explains, she listens. If he says "it's a drawing," that is a complete answer.
5. **If a drawing is clearly about the father or the visits** (a figure labeled Daddy, a building that looks like the visit location, a picture of the family with someone missing or changed): receive it without interpretation. Do not say "you miss Daddy, don't you?" Say "thank you for showing me." The drawing speaks for itself. If the content concerns the mother (drawings of harm, drawings of the child alone, repeated images of destruction), she may want to bring the folder to the family therapist for guidance — but she does not tell the child this. She simply notes it.
6. **The drawings stay in the folder** unless the child takes them out. Over time, the folder accumulates — a visible record of his interior life across weeks and months. This is itself a connection-capsule: the drawings made at age 5, about this time in his life, will be meaningful to him at 15 and at 25 in ways neither he nor the mother can predict now.

## Simple prompts (OPTIONAL — only if the child wants structure)

Some 5-year-olds want a prompt. If he asks "what should I draw?", the mother may offer ONE of these and then step back:

- "Draw where you are right now."
- "Draw the weather inside you."
- "Draw something you saw today."
- "Draw something you want to remember."
- "Draw your favorite color doing something."

The prompts are open-ended. They do not mention Daddy, the visits, or the family situation unless the child takes them there himself.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **Distress during drawing:** if the child becomes upset while drawing (crying, tearing the paper, throwing crayons), the drawing has surfaced something. Do not take the drawing away. Say "I see you're having a hard time. Do you want to keep drawing, or do you want to stop?" Honor his answer. If he stops, the 90-second co-regulation practice (`15-emergency-co-regulation-90-seconds.md`) is available.
- **Repeated imagery of harm, destruction, or self-directed violence** across multiple drawings over weeks: this is a pattern to bring to the family therapist, not a single-drawing alarm. One drawing of a monster eating a house is normal 5-year-old imagination. Ten drawings of monsters eating houses over three weeks is a message.
- **Refusal to draw:** completely fine. Some children do not express through drawing. The folder waits. Other practices (sensory bin play, body-based co-regulation, bedtime story) carry the expressive work.
- **Magical-thinking self-blame:** if a drawing reveals the child believes he caused the father's condition ("I drew Daddy sick because I was bad"), this is a 5-year-old's preoperational magical thinking, not a clinical emergency — but it needs a response. See `mother-with-kids/processing-confusion/13-why-cant-daddy-live-with-us-script.md` for the age-appropriate script: "It's not because of you. It's not something you can catch. It's not something you can fix." Said once, calmly.
- **If any signal sustains beyond this session, see pediatric crisis signals in `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## Modifications

- **If the child prefers modeling clay or playdough to drawing:** the "folder" becomes a "box" and the medium shifts. The principle is the same: his creative expression, his ownership, no interpretation.
- **If the child wants to draw WITH Daddy during a supervised visit:** this is a beautiful crossover with `08-shared-drawing-each-adds.md`. The drawing from the visit can go into the folder. A drawing made with Daddy is a connection-capsule.
- **If the child cannot hold a crayon comfortably:** finger paints (washable, non-toxic) on large paper on the floor. Or stickers on paper. Or tearing colored paper and gluing it. The medium adapts; the ownership principle stays.

## After

Nothing. The folder is the practice. It does not generate homework, reports, or conversations. The mother may privately note (for her own record and the family therapist's) any patterns she notices over weeks — but this is her observation, not the child's assignment.

## Traditions honored

- **Universal childhood drawing** — every culture's children draw. The practice of giving a child paper and colors and stepping back is as old as pigment on cave walls.
- **Art therapy research** — the principle that drawing externalizes interior states is grounded in clinical art therapy (Malchiodi 2011 [verification needed for specific citation]) and narrative therapy's externalization principle (White & Epston 1990; Freeman, Epston & Lobovits 1997).
- **Pennebaker's expressive writing paradigm** — adapted for pre-literate children. (Reference: Pennebaker 1997; Fivush et al. 2007.)

The practice is offered as a universal form. The folder is the child's own.
