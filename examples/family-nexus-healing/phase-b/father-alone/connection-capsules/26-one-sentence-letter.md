# One-Sentence Letter

**Configuration:** father-alone
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 5 minutes or less
**Energy cost:** low

## Why this practice

The Phase A synthesis (§3.2) identifies connection-capsules as low-stakes, consent-dependent practices that create durable artifacts of the father-child relationship. A one-sentence letter is the smallest possible capsule: one sentence per child, written or dictated on a good day, placed in an envelope, and held in trust.

This is not a legacy letter. It is not a farewell. It is not a summary of a life. It is one sentence — "I love you," or "You made me laugh today," or "I am proud of you" — written when the words come easily and the day is clear. Harvey Max Chochinov's Dignity Therapy (2005, 2011) demonstrated that even brief acts of generativity — leaving something behind for someone you love — may help preserve a sense of purpose and meaning. The adaptation here is radical simplicity: one sentence, because one sentence is achievable on a day when a paragraph is not.

The continuing-bonds framework (Klass, Silverman, & Nickman, 1996) holds that ongoing connection with a person who is changing or gone is healthy. A sentence written today may reach a child years from now — not as a relic of loss but as evidence of love that was active and present.

## You'll need

- Paper and a pen. Or a person who can write what the father dictates.
- An envelope for each child.
- A label or marker.
- Nothing else.

## Before you start

- **Consent is the foundation.** The father chooses to do this. No one prompts him by saying "you should leave something for the kids." If the idea appeals to him, he does it. If it does not, he does not.
- This is done on a GOOD DAY. If today is hard, this practice waits.
- You can stop at any step. A blank envelope is not a failure.
- The father is the author, not the subject. He writes (or dictates) what he wants to say. No one edits his sentence.
- If a caregiver is present, they may prepare the paper and envelope and offer to write if the father prefers to dictate. They do not suggest content.

## When NOT to use this

- **DRIVING FLAG (Absolute):** This practice does not involve driving. However, per §5.1, driving status must be verified with the care team at each stage of the condition. No protocol in this kit assumes driving capacity.
- **SUICIDALITY FLAG (Caution):** The younger-onset dementia literature (Draper et al. 2010; Erlangsen et al. 2008) documents elevated suicide risk in the immediate post-diagnosis window and at major capability-loss transitions. If the father is experiencing thoughts of self-harm, or if a caregiver notices acute distress, withdrawal, or hopelessness, this practice yields to crisis response. Call 988 (US) or your local crisis line. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.
- **EXISTENTIAL-CONTENT FLAG (Caution):** Creating artifacts for a future in which the father may not be cognitively present inherently carries existential weight. If the practice surfaces awareness of decline, loss of identity, or despair about the future — if the father says anything suggesting the letter feels like a farewell or a final act ("this is the last thing I'll write") — the practice has shifted from generative to anguished. Stop, offer presence, and consult the care team. This is the existential-content contraindication from §5.1.
- **CONSENT AND CAPACITY-LOSS (Critical):** The father must give clear consent before each letter. Consent is renewable and withdrawable at any time, including retroactively — if he later asks that a letter not be kept or delivered, it is removed without argument. **If the father's condition progresses to the point where he can no longer meaningfully consent,** the decision about whether to continue delivering previously-written letters rests with the person holding his healthcare power of attorney (POA), or — if no POA is designated — with the family therapist in consultation with the care team. Letters already sealed remain in the mother's trust. No new letters are created without clear consent. This should be discussed with the care team early, ideally before the first letter is written.
- **Situational:** If writing or dictating a sentence causes visible distress — if the father becomes tearful, agitated, or withdrawn — stop. The sentence is not urgent. It can happen another day or not at all.
- **Relational:** If the father says "I don't know what to say," that is a complete answer. Do not coach. The sentence will come on its own day, or it will not.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

### Step 1: One sentence per child

The father writes or dictates one sentence for each child. Not a paragraph. Not a speech. One sentence.

Examples (not scripts — his words are his own):
- "I love you."
- "You are brave and kind."
- "I liked building blocks with you today."
- "Your laugh is my favorite sound."
- "I am so glad you are mine."

The sentence does not need to be profound. The ordinary ones are often the ones that matter most.

### Step 2: Into the envelope

Each sentence goes into its own envelope. On the outside, the father (or his caregiver) writes:
- The child's name.
- A future moment — not a date, but a kind of moment. Examples: "When you need it." "On a hard day." "When you graduate." "When you miss me." "Whenever you want."

The father chooses the moment. If he does not have one in mind, "For you" is enough.

### Step 3: Seal and give to the mother

The mother holds the envelope in trust. She does not open it. She does not read it. She stores it somewhere safe — a drawer, a box, a place where important things are kept. She decides, when the time comes, whether and when to give it to the child.

### Step 4: Repeat as capacity and desire permit

This is not a one-time practice. On another good day, weeks or months later, the father may write another sentence. Over time, a small collection may accumulate — not a book, not a testament, just a handful of sentences written on clear days. Or he may write one, once, and that is complete.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** tears that will not settle, frustration at not finding words, agitation, pulling away from the paper, flat affect, asking "what's the point?" in a tone of despair rather than curiosity.
- **What to do:** Put the pen down. Offer water, quiet company, or a change of activity. No explanation needed. If the distress is acute — thoughts of self-harm, inability to settle, withdrawal from all engagement — activate the crisis response path. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.

## Modifications

- **Dictated version:** The father speaks the sentence. A caregiver writes it down exactly as he said it, in his words. The caregiver reads it back to confirm. The father's voice is the author even if his hand is not the writer.
- **Drawn version:** If words are not coming but the father can draw, a small drawing — a heart, a stick figure, a sun — is a sentence in another language. It goes in the envelope the same way.
- **Shorter version:** One sentence for one child. Not both. Today's sentence is for this child. Another day's may be for the other.
- **Caregiver-assisted version:** The caregiver prepares the paper, envelope, and pen. Sits nearby. If the father asks "What should I say?" the caregiver may respond: "Whatever comes to you" or "What do you want them to know?" — but does not suggest specific words. If the father cannot begin, the caregiver may offer: "Some people start with 'I love you' — but it's your sentence." If the father still cannot begin, that is today's answer. The envelope stays empty and ready for another day.
- **"Hard day" version:** Do not attempt. This practice is for good days.

## After

No reflection required. The sentence exists. That is enough.

The father does not need to remember writing it. The sentence will remember for him.

## Traditions honored

- **Chochinov Dignity Therapy** (adapted) — the practice of creating a brief generativity document for loved ones. Adapted here to its smallest form: one sentence rather than a structured interview. (Chochinov et al., *Journal of Clinical Oncology*, 2005; Chochinov, *Dignity Therapy: Final Words for Final Days*, 2011.)
- **Jewish tzavaah (ethical will)** — the centuries-old tradition of leaving not material wealth but values, blessings, and love for the next generation. A single sentence of blessing is the elemental form of the tzavaah. The tradition holds that what a parent transmits to a child is not property but presence. (Abrahams, *Hebrew Ethical Wills*, 1926; Riemer & Stampfer, *So That Your Values Live On*, 1991.)
- **Japanese monogatari and ikigai** — the narrative tradition of transmitting meaning through story, and the concept of *ikigai* (a reason for being) — here expressed in the smallest possible form: one sentence that says "you are my reason." (Mogi, *The Little Book of Ikigai*, 2017; broader monogatari tradition from *The Tale of Genji*, c. 1000 CE.)
- **Continuing-bonds theory** — the research-based understanding that maintaining connection with a person who is changing or gone is healthy, not a failure to "move on." Each sentence is a continuing bond. (Klass, Silverman, & Nickman, *Continuing Bonds*, 1996.)
- **Mexican cartas y recuerdos** — the Latin American tradition of written remembrances and letters kept within families across generations. *Recuerdos* (keepsakes, remembrances) serve as physical anchors of relationship that persist across absence and loss. The tradition is especially strong in Mexican and Central American families where migration, illness, and separation have made written artifacts a primary vehicle for love across distance. (Gutierrez, in Klass et al., 1996; broader cultural practice.)
