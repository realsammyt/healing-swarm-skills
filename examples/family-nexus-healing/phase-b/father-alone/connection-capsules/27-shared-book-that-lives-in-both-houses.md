# Shared Book That Lives in Both Houses

**Configuration:** father-alone
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 2–10 minutes per entry (no schedule)
**Energy cost:** low

## Why this practice

The Phase A synthesis (§3.2) names "a shared object that lives in both houses" as a connection-capsule candidate — a physical artifact that accumulates the relationship over time without requiring any single act to be monumental. A notebook that travels between the father's home and the children's home via visits becomes a living record of the family's ongoing connection: a sentence, a drawing, a sticker, a pressed leaf, a scribble, a nothing. No one is required to add anything on any given day. The book accumulates.

Donald Winnicott's concept of the transitional object (1953, 1971) describes how a child's blanket or stuffed animal bridges the gap between self and other, presence and absence. This notebook is a transitional object for the whole family — a thing that belongs to no single house but to the relationship between houses. It carries the father's mark to the children and the children's marks to the father. It does not require narrative memory to participate in. A pressed leaf does not need to be remembered to be real.

The continuing-bonds framework (Klass, Silverman, & Nickman, 1996) holds that maintaining ongoing connection is healthy. This book is a continuing bond in physical form — not a memorial but a living document, added to by everyone, belonging to everyone.

## You'll need

- A physical notebook. Hardcover is better — it will travel. Unlined pages work best (they accommodate drawings, stickers, and pressed objects). A5 or slightly larger.
- A few pens, colored pencils, or markers kept with the book.
- Optionally: stickers, tape, a glue stick (for pressed leaves, ticket stubs, small flat objects).
- A bag or envelope the book travels in.

## Before you start

- This book belongs to the family, not to any one person. No one controls what goes in it.
- No one is required to add anything on any given day. The book is an invitation, never an obligation.
- There are no rules about what goes in. A sentence. A doodle. A sticker. A fingerprint. The date. Nothing. All are valid entries.
- The book travels with the children when they visit the father, and returns home with them. Or it can stay at the father's house and the children add to it during visits. The family decides the direction of travel.
- You can stop this practice at any time. The book will hold whatever is in it.

## When NOT to use this

- **DRIVING FLAG (Absolute):** This practice does not involve driving. However, per §5.1, driving status must be verified with the care team at each stage of the condition. No protocol in this kit assumes driving capacity.
- **SUICIDALITY FLAG (Caution):** The younger-onset dementia literature (Draper et al. 2010; Erlangsen et al. 2008) documents elevated suicide risk in the immediate post-diagnosis window and at major capability-loss transitions. If the father is experiencing thoughts of self-harm, or if a caregiver notices acute distress, withdrawal, or hopelessness, this practice yields to crisis response. Call 988 (US) or your local crisis line. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.
- **CONSENT AND CAPACITY-LOSS (Critical):** The father participates by choice. If he no longer wants the book, it stops visiting his house. **If the father's condition progresses to the point where he can no longer meaningfully consent to or understand the book's purpose,** the caregiver and family therapist together decide: does the book continue to travel (with the caregiver making or assisting entries on the father's behalf), or does it stay at the mother's house? Previous entries by the father remain — they are part of the book's honest record. If the father, while still able, expressed a wish about the book's future, that wish is honored. This should be discussed with the care team early.
- **Situational:** If the book itself becomes a source of distress — if the father is agitated by seeing entries he does not remember making, or if either child resists the book — pause the practice. The book will wait.
- **Relational:** If the supervisor during a visit has concerns about the book (its content, its role in the visit), those concerns take precedence. The book must remain readable by the supervisor at all times.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

### Step 1: Start the book

Someone — the mother, the father, a caregiver — opens the notebook and makes the first entry. It can be as simple as the date and "This book lives in both houses." Or a drawing. Or nothing but a handprint. The first entry sets the tone: this is not a performance. This is a place where small things land.

### Step 2: The book travels

The book moves between houses through visits. It comes with the children when they visit the father. During the visit — or before, or after — anyone may add something. The father may draw a circle. A child may put a sticker in. The mother may write the date and the weather. Or no one adds anything that visit. The book is patient.

### Step 3: Anyone adds, anytime

There are no prompts, no assignments, no "your turn." If the father is sitting with the book and a pen, and something comes, he adds it. If the 5-year-old wants to draw a dinosaur on a blank page, that belongs in the book. If the 8-year-old wants to write "I miss you" or "today was boring" or tape in a movie ticket, those belong too. The mother may add an entry at home — "It rained all day. We made soup." — and the father will see it next visit.

What may go in the book:
- A sentence ("I had cereal for breakfast." "I love you." "The dog chased a squirrel.")
- A drawing (any drawing, any skill level, any subject)
- A sticker
- A pressed leaf, flower petal, or blade of grass (taped or glued)
- A ticket stub, wrapper, or small flat object
- A handprint or fingerprint
- The date
- A weather report
- Nothing — a blank page is a valid entry

What does NOT go in the book:
- Nothing secret from the supervisor
- Nothing that asks a child to carry a message between parents
- Nothing that burdens a child with adult grief

### Step 4: Let it accumulate

Over weeks and months, the book fills. Some pages will be dense. Some will have a single mark. Some will be blank. The accumulation is the practice. No one needs to review it, evaluate it, or curate it. It is what it is — a record of a family's ongoing presence to each other.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** distress at seeing entries he does not remember, confusion about whose book it is, agitation when the book is present, refusing to look at it.
- **In either child:** resistance to the book ("I don't want to"), using the book to carry messages between parents, distress at the father's entries (or lack of entries).
- **What to do:** Remove the book from the visit gently. It does not need to be present every time. If the distress is acute, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.

## Modifications

- **One-direction version:** The book stays at the father's house. Children add to it during visits. It does not travel. This simplifies logistics and avoids the risk of the book being forgotten or lost in transit.
- **Digital companion:** Photos of book pages can be taken (with consent) so both houses have a copy even when the physical book is elsewhere. The physical book remains primary.
- **Simpler version:** Instead of a notebook, a single large piece of paper taped to a wall at the father's home, where everyone adds a mark during each visit. When it fills, it goes into a folder and a new sheet goes up.
- **Caregiver-assisted version:** The caregiver brings the book to the father, opens it to a blank page, and places a pen nearby. If the father picks it up, the caregiver steps back. If the father does not, the caregiver may say, "The kids added something last time — want to see?" and open to a recent page. If the father engages, the caregiver stays nearby. If not, the book goes away quietly.
- **"Hard day" version:** The book stays in its bag. Not every visit needs the book. Its absence is not a loss.

## After

No reflection required. The book speaks for itself.

If the family wants, they may occasionally flip through older pages together during a visit. This is not a review or an evaluation. It is the book showing the family to itself.

## Traditions honored

- **Winnicott's transitional object** — Donald Winnicott's observation that objects bridging presence and absence (a child's blanket, a parent's scarf) serve a vital psychological function. This book is a transitional object for the whole family. (Winnicott, "Transitional Objects and Transitional Phenomena," *International Journal of Psycho-Analysis*, 1953; *Playing and Reality*, 1971.)
- **Japanese zuihitsu** — the literary tradition of the "pillow book" or miscellaneous notebook, epitomized by Sei Shonagon's *Makura no Soshi* (c. 1000 CE). A zuihitsu contains whatever the writer notices: lists, observations, drawings, impressions, weather. It is not a diary and not a letter. It is a record of being alive and paying attention. This shared book is a family zuihitsu. (Sei Shonagon, *The Pillow Book*, trans. McKinney, 2006.)
- **Quaker journaling tradition** — the practice of keeping a plain record of inner and outer life, without ornament or performance, as an act of witness. George Fox and early Friends kept journals not for publication but for truth-telling. A family book where "I had cereal for breakfast" sits beside "I miss you" follows this tradition's commitment to the ordinary as sufficient. (Fox, *Journal*, 1694; Brinton, *Quaker Journals*, 1972.)
- **Continuing-bonds theory** — the research framework demonstrating that ongoing connection with a person who is changing or gone is healthy. The book is a continuing bond in physical form, growing as the relationship grows. (Klass, Silverman, & Nickman, *Continuing Bonds*, 1996.)
- **Mexican ofrendas and recuerdos** — the tradition of creating physical spaces and objects that maintain relationship across absence. An *ofrenda* holds tokens of the beloved; *recuerdos* (keepsakes) carry presence across distance and time. This book is a portable ofrenda, built together. (Brandes, *Skulls to the Living, Bread to the Dead*, 2006; broader Día de los Muertos tradition.)
