# Fifteen-Minute Pre-Visit Centering

**Configuration:** father-alone
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 15 minutes (before leaving for a supervised visit)
**Energy cost:** low

## Why this practice

The Phase A synthesis (§3.3, §3.5, §5.1) names the capacity to "arrive settled" as a core need for the father before supervised visits. Polyvagal theory (Porges, 2011; Dana, 2018) proposes that the ventral vagal complex supports social engagement and calm connection through face, voice, and breath, and that this system may still function when declarative memory does not. Settling the nervous system before a social engagement is not the same as meditation. It is closer to what athletes call a pre-game routine: the body entering a state it recognizes, so the person can be present rather than braced. This practice may help the father arrive at the visit with his nervous system closer to calm, which in turn may help his children's nervous systems settle in his presence.

## You'll need

- A quiet room in the father's home. The room should be familiar.
- A warm drink — tea, coffee, warm water, whatever he likes. Already prepared.
- Optionally: one piece of familiar music (a song he knows well, not something new) OR silence. His choice.
- A chair or place to sit comfortably.
- A caregiver or trusted person nearby (not in the room unless invited).

## Before you start

- You can stop at any step. Stopping is a win.
- This is not a meditation. You do not have to clear your mind, reach a state, or feel a certain way. You are just sitting with a warm drink in a quiet room for fifteen minutes before you go see your children. That is all.
- If today is a hard day and the visit is still happening, this practice may still help. A shorter version is below.

## When NOT to use this

- **DRIVING FLAG (Absolute):** Per §5.1, driving status must be verified with the care team. This practice is done at home before leaving for the visit. It does not assume the father drives himself.
- **SUICIDALITY FLAG (Caution):** Elevated suicide risk in the post-diagnosis window and at capability-loss transitions (Draper et al. 2010; Erlangsen et al. 2008). If the father is in acute distress before a visit — tearfulness that will not settle, expressed hopelessness, statements about not wanting to go on — the visit itself should be evaluated with the care team. The centering practice does not override crisis response. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.
- **Situational:** If the father is acutely agitated, confused about the day, or unable to settle in the room, do not force the practice. The caregiver may offer a simpler version (see Modifications) or simply sit with him quietly until it is time to leave.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

1. **Fifteen minutes before it is time to leave for the visit,** the caregiver (or the father himself) says: "It's almost time. Let's sit for a few minutes first."
2. **Go to the quiet room.** The father sits in his chair or his spot. The door can be open or closed — his preference.
3. **The warm drink is already there.** He picks it up. He holds it. He feels the warmth in his hands. He takes a sip. There is nothing else to do with the drink except hold it and sip it.
4. **If music is part of the routine, it is already playing.** One familiar song or a short playlist (no more than 15 minutes). Not new music. Not a podcast. Not the news. Something his body already knows.
5. **Sit.** That is the instruction. Sit and drink the warm drink and listen to the music or the silence. If thoughts come about the visit — worry, anticipation, sadness, hope — let them come. They do not need to be managed.
6. **Three slow breaths.** At some point in the fifteen minutes — it does not matter when — take three slow breaths. Inhale through the nose. Exhale through the mouth, longer than the inhale. The long exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system (Dana, 2018). That is the only "technique" in this practice. Three breaths. The rest is sitting.
7. **When it is time to leave, someone says so.** "It's time." The father finishes the drink or sets it down. He stands. He goes to the visit. The centering is done.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** increasing agitation instead of settling, pacing, getting up and sitting back down repeatedly, tearfulness he cannot ease, statements of dread or refusal, flat shutdown (staring, unresponsive), confusion about what the visit is or who the children are.
- **What to do:** The caregiver enters the room gently. Offers presence, not correction. "I'm here. We don't have to rush." If the father cannot settle within the fifteen minutes, the caregiver contacts the visit supervisor to discuss whether a brief delay is possible, or whether today's visit should be shorter. If the distress is acute, the crisis response path activates before the visit happens. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.

## Modifications

- **Shorter version (5 minutes):** Warm drink, three slow breaths, sit. That is all.
- **Lower-energy version:** The father does not leave the chair he is already in. The caregiver brings the warm drink to him. The music plays in the room he is already in.
- **"Hard day" version:** No music. No instruction. The caregiver sits near the father and drinks their own warm drink. Two people sitting in a room. The co-regulation happens through shared quiet.
- **Caregiver-assisted version:** The caregiver prepares the room in advance — warm drink ready, music queued, chair arranged. The caregiver guides the father to the room. If the father needs prompting for the three breaths, the caregiver can simply take three slow breaths themselves — the mirror neuron pathway may invite the father to follow without verbal instruction.

## After

Optional three-line reflection:

1. Did I settle, even a little, before we left?
2. What helped most — the drink, the music, the quiet, or the breathing?
3. Is there anything I want to change for next time?

## Traditions honored

- **Polyvagal theory (Stephen Porges, 2011; Deb Dana, 2018)** — the neurophysiological basis for long-exhale breathing as parasympathetic activation.
- **Japanese tea ceremony** — the principle that preparation before an encounter is itself part of the encounter. The host prepares the room, the water, the vessel before the guest arrives. (Okakura, *The Book of Tea*, 1906; Sen Soshitsu XV, *Tea Life, Tea Mind*, 1979.)
- **Quaker expectant waiting** — the practice of sitting in silence not as absence but as presence. (Pink Dandelion, *The Quakers: A Very Short Introduction*, 2008.)
