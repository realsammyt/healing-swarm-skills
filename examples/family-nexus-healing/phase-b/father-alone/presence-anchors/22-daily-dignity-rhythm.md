# Daily Dignity Rhythm

**Configuration:** father-alone
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 5 minutes
**Energy cost:** low

## Why this practice

The Phase A synthesis (§3.3) identifies sensory anchoring practices as the primary activity menu for a father whose narrative memory is thinning but whose body, breath, and senses remain vital. The synthesis names sound, water, movement, and breath as channels that "work WITH the still-vital body" and draws on research showing that procedural and sensory memory persist when declarative memory does not (Sacks, 2007; Porges, 2011). A single recurring anchor done the same way every day may help build a rhythm the body recognizes even when the mind does not narrate it. Consistency over novelty. The same practice, the same time, the same place. This is not a therapeutic exercise prescribed to a patient. It is a practice chosen by a person.

## You'll need

- A quiet spot in your home where you can stand, sit, or kneel undisturbed for five minutes.
- Optionally: a small bowl of water, a timer, or a piece of music (depending on which anchor you choose).
- Nothing else.

## Before you start

- You can stop at any step. Stopping is a win.
- This practice belongs to you. No one is grading it. If a day comes when you do not do it, that day is not a failure. The practice will be here tomorrow.
- If you have a caregiver or trusted person with you, they may set up the space and gently remind you it is time. They do not do the practice for you. They hold the space while you do it.

## When NOT to use this

- **DRIVING FLAG (Absolute):** This practice itself does not involve driving. However, per §5.1, driving status must be verified with the care team at each stage of the condition. No protocol in this kit assumes driving capacity.
- **SUICIDALITY FLAG (Caution):** The younger-onset dementia literature (Draper et al. 2010; Erlangsen et al. 2008) documents elevated suicide risk in the immediate post-diagnosis window and at major capability-loss transitions. If you are experiencing thoughts of self-harm, or if your caregiver notices acute distress, withdrawal, or hopelessness, this practice yields to crisis response. Call 988 (US) or your local crisis line. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.
- **Situational:** If today is a day of acute confusion, agitation, or distress, do not force the practice. Let it go. It will be here tomorrow.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

### Step 1: Choose your anchor (once, not every day)

Read through these four options. Choose the one that draws you. Once chosen, it is your anchor. You do it the same way every day. If after a week it does not fit, you may choose a different one. But give each one a week before switching.

**Option A: Breath counting (Zen tradition)**
Sit or stand. Breathe naturally. Count each exhale silently: one, two, three, four, five. Then start again at one. That is the whole practice. Five minutes of counting exhales to five. If you lose count, start at one. Losing count is not a mistake. It is part of the practice.

**Option B: Morning hand-washing (Jewish tradition, adapted)**
Stand at a sink. Fill a cup with warm water. Pour water slowly over your right hand, then your left hand, then right, then left, then right, then left — three times each hand. Feel the warmth. Feel your hands waking. Dry them slowly. That is the whole practice.

**Option C: Water and breath (wudu-inspired, adapted)**
Stand or sit near a basin of warm water. Dip your hands in the water. Bring wet hands to your face and hold them there for a breath. Then wet your hands again and run them slowly over your forearms. Then hold your wet hands still for one more breath. That is the whole practice.

**Option D: Standing body scan (secular somatic tradition)**
Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart. Close your eyes or soften your gaze. Starting at the top of your head, slowly notice each part of your body: head, face, neck, shoulders, arms, hands, chest, belly, hips, legs, feet. You are not trying to change anything. You are just noticing. When you reach your feet, take one slow breath. That is the whole practice.

### Step 2: Do the practice (every day, same time, same place)

- Pick a time. Morning is simplest — before anything else happens to you. But any consistent time works.
- Go to your spot.
- Do your anchor. Five minutes or less.
- When you are done, you are done. No journaling required. No scoring. No "how did that go?" Just done.

### Step 3: Let it accumulate

The practice is not about any single day. It is about the accumulation. Over weeks, your body may begin to recognize the rhythm before your mind narrates it. That recognition is the anchor working.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** increasing agitation during the practice, pacing instead of settling, tearfulness that will not ease, confusion about where he is or what he is doing, flat affect that feels unlike quiet settling.
- **What to do:** Stop the practice gently. If a caregiver is present, they may offer water, a warm drink, or quiet company. No explanation is needed. The practice can be tried again tomorrow. If the distress is acute — thoughts of self-harm, inability to settle across hours, withdrawal from all engagement — activate the crisis response path. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.

## Modifications

- **Shorter version (2 minutes):** Do only 5 breath cycles (Option A), or one round of hand-washing (Option B), or one face-touch with wet hands (Option C), or notice only three body parts — head, hands, feet (Option D).
- **Lower-energy version (seated or lying down):** All four options can be done sitting in a chair or lying in bed. The standing body scan becomes a lying body scan. The hand-washing can be done with a warm washcloth brought to the bedside.
- **"Hard day" version:** Simply sit in the spot where the practice usually happens. Breathe. Nothing more is required. Being in the spot counts.
- **Caregiver-assisted version:** The caregiver prepares the space (fills the water bowl, sets the timer, puts on quiet music if that is part of the routine). The caregiver may say, "It's time for your practice" or simply guide the father to his spot. The caregiver stays nearby but does not instruct. If the father cannot initiate, the caregiver may begin the first gesture — pour the first cup of water, place the father's hands on his own knees — and then step back.

## After

Optional three-line reflection. The father may say these silently, speak them aloud, or a caregiver may note them on his behalf:

1. Did I settle, even a little?
2. Was anything different from yesterday?
3. Is this still the right anchor for me?

## Traditions honored

- **Zen Buddhism** — breath counting (susokukan) as foundational awareness practice. (Shunryu Suzuki, *Zen Mind, Beginner's Mind*, 1970.)
- **Judaism** — netilat yadayim, morning hand-washing as daily renewal of agency. (Shulchan Aruch, Orach Chaim 4:1.)
- **Islam** — wudu ablution as sensory and spiritual preparation, adapted here at the level of water-and-presence rather than as religious obligation. (al-Ghazali, *Ihya Ulum al-Din*, Book 3.)
- **Secular somatic tradition via Buddhist vipassana** — body-scan practice adapted by Jon Kabat-Zinn's MBSR program. (Kabat-Zinn, *Full Catastrophe Living*, 1990.) Standing version draws on Chinese qigong zhan zhuang tradition.
