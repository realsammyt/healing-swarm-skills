# Morning Sensory Anchor

**Configuration:** father-alone
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 5–10 minutes
**Energy cost:** low to medium

## Why this practice

Kate Swaffer's concept of "prescribed disengagement" (Swaffer, 2016) names the social prescription that newly diagnosed people should retire, rest, and prepare to die rather than be offered meaningful engagement. The Phase A synthesis (§3.1, §3.3, §5.1) explicitly names this as a harm younger-onset families can resist: "the father is a person with a diagnosis, not a diagnosis with a person attached." This practice is the counter-move. It is something the father does FIRST in the morning, before anything else happens to him — before medication is managed, before a schedule is read to him, before he is a person who receives care. For a few minutes, he is a person who acts. He initiates.

## You'll need

- Whatever the chosen anchor requires (see options below). Materials should be accessible and safe.
- A home environment where the father can move freely in the morning.
- If a caregiver is present: the caregiver's awareness that this is the father's time to initiate, not a time to manage.

## Before you start

- You can stop at any step. Stopping is a win.
- This is yours. You chose it. If it stops working, you choose something else.
- Safety first: if the chosen anchor involves the kitchen (hot water, stove), a caregiver should be within earshot but not hovering.

## When NOT to use this

- **DRIVING FLAG (Absolute):** Per §5.1, if the chosen anchor involves any equipment, sharp objects, or heat sources, the care team should confirm the father's current capacity. Safety assessments are ongoing, not one-time.
- **SUICIDALITY FLAG (Caution):** Elevated suicide risk post-diagnosis and at capability-loss transitions (Draper et al. 2010; Erlangsen et al. 2008). Morning can be a vulnerable time. If the father wakes in acute distress, the morning anchor yields to care. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.
- **Situational:** On days when the father wakes confused, agitated, or significantly disoriented, the caregiver gently adjusts. The anchor may be simplified or set aside entirely.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

### Step 1: Choose your morning anchor (once, then keep it)

The anchor must meet three conditions: (a) the father can do it himself or mostly himself, (b) it is safe, and (c) it happens before anything else in his morning.

**Option A: Make tea or coffee.** Get up. Go to the kitchen. Make one cup the way you like it. Carry it to a place you like to sit. Drink it. That is the anchor. *(Safety note: if stove/kettle use is a concern, an electric kettle with auto-shutoff or a pre-set coffee maker preserves agency while addressing safety.)*

**Option B: Open a window and breathe.** Get up. Go to a window. Open it. Stand there and breathe the outside air for five breaths. Notice the temperature. Notice any sound. That is the anchor.

**Option C: One stretch, the same stretch.** Get up. Stand beside the bed. Arms overhead, on tiptoes, reaching toward the ceiling. Hold for three breaths. Release. That is the anchor.

**Option D: One piece of music.** Get up. Play one piece of music you love. The same piece every morning. Stand or sit and listen, start to finish. When it ends, your morning has begun. *(Setup note: a caregiver may pre-load the music on a simple device. The father presses play.)*

### Step 2: Do it first

Before medication. Before breakfast is served. Before anyone tells you the plan for the day. This is the first act of your morning. You wake, and then you do this.

### Step 3: Let it be ordinary

This is not a spiritual practice (unless you want it to be). It is not therapy. It is a cup of coffee, or a window, or a stretch, or a song. That ordinariness is the point. You are a person who starts his day.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In the father:** confusion about the steps of the anchor that was previously easy (a signal that the anchor may need simplification, not that the practice should end), frustration or anger at being unable to complete it, tearfulness on waking that does not settle, refusal to get out of bed that feels different from ordinary tiredness.
- **What to do:** The caregiver meets the father where he is. If the anchor is too complex today, simplify. If the father cannot get up, the anchor can come to him (see Modifications). If the distress is acute and sustained, the crisis response path activates. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.

## Modifications

- **Shorter version:** One sip of a warm drink someone brings. One breath at the window. One stretch while still in bed.
- **Lower-energy version (in bed):** The caregiver opens the window from across the room. The father breathes the air from bed. Or: the caregiver places a warm cup in the father's hands while he is still sitting up in bed.
- **"Hard day" version:** The father sits on the edge of the bed. He puts his feet on the floor. He feels the floor. That is the morning anchor for a hard day. Feet on the floor means: I am here. I am starting my day.
- **Caregiver-assisted version:** The caregiver prepares materials but does not do the anchor for the father. The caregiver's role is to make initiation possible, not to initiate on his behalf. "Your kettle is ready" or "The window is unlocked." Then step back. If the father needs physical assistance, the caregiver assists with the body mechanics while the father remains the one who is doing the thing.

## After

Optional three-line reflection:

1. Did I do my morning anchor today?
2. Was it easy, hard, or somewhere in between?
3. Do I still want this to be my anchor?

## Traditions honored

- **Disability-rights advocacy in younger-onset dementia** — Kate Swaffer's concept of prescribed disengagement (Swaffer, *What the Hell Happened to My Brain?*, 2016) and the DAI disability-rights framework. The counter-practice of preserved agency draws directly on this advocacy.
- **Zen Buddhism** — the monastic tradition of samu (work practice) as a form of practice that honors the body's activity. The morning anchor is closer to samu than to zazen: the practice is in the doing. (Dogen, *Tenzo Kyokun / Instructions to the Cook*, 13th century.)
- **Jewish tradition** — Modeh Ani, the first words spoken upon waking ("I give thanks..."), recited before any other act. The structural parallel: a person's first conscious gesture of the day is their own, preceding all obligations. (Shulchan Aruch, Orach Chaim 1:1.)
- **Qigong tradition** — morning standing practices (zhan zhuang) that work with the body's vitality upon waking. (Lam Kam Chuen, *The Way of Energy*, 1991.)
