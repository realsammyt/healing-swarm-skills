### Dignity framing

All 6 protocols avoid treatment language. The father is never "the patient," never "the subject," never described as receiving an intervention. He is a person who acts, who chooses, who initiates. Protocol 24 is the strongest example: "Before anything else happens to him... he is a person who acts." Protocol 25 states explicitly: "The father is the author of this artifact, not its subject." This framing is consistent and load-bearing across the set.

### Consent protocols in connection-capsules (25, 26, 27)

**Protocol 25 (Voice Recording):** Consent is described as renewable, withdrawable, and retroactively revocable. Capacity-loss fallback is explicitly named: "If the father can no longer give or revoke consent, decision authority passes to his designated Power of Attorney or to the family therapist, in that order." This is the strongest consent protocol in the set.

**Protocol 26 (One-Sentence Letter):** Consent is framed as "the father chooses to do this" and "no one prompts him." Mild reluctance is named as sufficient to stop. However, there is no explicit capacity-loss clause. See Safety-Critical Revision #1.

**Protocol 27 (Shared Book):** Consent is framed as family-level ("this book belongs to the family, not to any one person"). The supervisor readability clause provides external oversight. However, there is no explicit capacity-loss clause for the father's continued participation. See Safety-Critical Revision #2.

### Exit permission

All 6 protocols include "You can stop at any step. Stopping is a win." This language is consistent across the set. Protocol 27 adds: "You can stop this practice at any time. The book will hold whatever is in it." Protocol 25 adds: "His 'not today' is final for today." Exit permission is explicit and non-punitive throughout.

### DRIVING and SUICIDALITY flags

Both flags are present in all 6 protocols. The DRIVING FLAG is marked Absolute in all cases. The SUICIDALITY FLAG is marked Caution with crisis-line numbers (988) and explicit cross-reference to 00-crisis-response-card.md. Draper et al. 2010 and Erlangsen et al. 2008 are cited consistently. The post-diagnosis window and capability-loss transitions are both named.

### Connection-capsule consent rigor — capacity-loss scenarios

**The critical question: what happens when the father loses the capacity to consent to, or revoke consent for, artifacts that will outlive his current cognitive capacity?**

- Protocol 25 answers this explicitly: POA first, family therapist second. This is sound.
- Protocol 26 does NOT address this. Once a letter is sealed and given to the mother, the father may later lack capacity to revoke it. The protocol says consent is withdrawable ("he may ask for the recording to be deleted at any time") — but this language is from protocol 25, not 26. Protocol 26 has no revocation clause at all. See Safety-Critical Revision #1.
- Protocol 27 does NOT address this. The book accumulates entries the father may not remember making. The protocol acknowledges the distress risk ("distress at seeing entries he does not remember") but does not address whether the father retains the right to have his entries removed, or who decides if he loses capacity to make that decision. See Safety-Critical Revision #2.

### Safety of kitchen/stove use in morning anchor (Protocol 24)

Protocol 24, Option A (make tea or coffee) includes a parenthetical safety note: "if stove/kettle use is a concern, an electric kettle with auto-shutoff or a pre-set coffee maker preserves agency while addressing safety." This is a reasonable adaptation. However, the DRIVING FLAG reference in the contraindication block mentions "equipment, sharp objects, or heat sources" but does not specifically tie kitchen safety to the care team's ongoing assessment of Activities of Daily Living (ADL) capacity. See Non-Critical Revision #2.

### Closed-tradition warnings

**Islamic wudu (Protocol 22, Option C):** The protocol labels this "wudu-inspired, adapted" and draws on al-Ghazali. It explicitly states it is used "at the level of water-and-presence rather than as religious obligation." This is a reasonable adaptation. However, wudu is an obligatory act of worship (fard/wajib) in Islamic jurisprudence, and some Muslim scholars and practitioners may find its adaptation into a secular sensory practice uncomfortable or disrespectful, even when labeled "adapted." The protocol should include a brief note acknowledging that wudu is a religious obligation in Islam and that this adaptation is offered with respect, recognizing that Muslim practitioners may prefer to practice wudu in its original form rather than this adapted version. See Non-Critical Revision #3.

**Mexican ofrendas (Protocol 27):** The ofrenda tradition is described accurately and respectfully. Brandes is a solid scholarly source. The protocol draws the parallel at a structural level ("a portable ofrenda, built together") rather than recreating a specific ritual. However, ofrendas are connected to Day of the Dead observances that carry deep spiritual and communal meaning within Mexican and Mesoamerican Indigenous communities. The "portable ofrenda" metaphor could be read as flattening this specificity. A brief note acknowledging that the ofrenda tradition carries sacred communal dimensions beyond the structural parallel would be appropriate. See Non-Critical Revision #4.

### Ecumenical balance

The tradition set across the 6 protocols includes: Zen Buddhism, Judaism (multiple touchpoints: netilat yadayim, Modeh Ani, tzavaah), Islam (adapted), secular somatic/MBSR, polyvagal neuroscience, Japanese cultural traditions (tea ceremony, monogatari, zuihitsu, ikigai), Quaker Christianity, disability-rights advocacy, psychoanalytic theory (Winnicott), clinical frameworks (Chochinov, continuing bonds), Mexican cultural traditions (cartas, recuerdos, ofrendas), qigong/Chinese tradition, and Zen work practice. Christian contemplative traditions beyond Quakerism are not represented. Hindu and Indigenous traditions are absent. This is acceptable for a 6-protocol set; not every tradition needs to appear in every configuration. The balance is reasonable.

### Language sensitivity

- "His condition" appears occasionally — acceptable when contextually necessary (contraindication blocks).

It is offered in the "After" section, not the consent section, and functions as reassurance rather than prognosis. Acceptable.

## Safety-Critical Revisions

1. **Protocol 26 (One-Sentence Letter): Add capacity-loss consent clause.** The protocol lacks any provision for what happens when the father loses the capacity to consent to or revoke the letters he has written. Protocol 25 provides a model: "If the father can no longer give or revoke consent, decision authority passes to his designated Power of Attorney or to the family therapist, in that order." This exact clause (or its equivalent) must be added to Protocol 26's "Before you start" section. The letters are sealed artifacts that will outlive the father's current cognitive capacity; they require the same consent rigor as the voice recordings.

2. **Protocol 27 (Shared Book): Add capacity-loss consent clause and entry-removal provision.** The book accumulates entries the father may not remember making. The protocol must address: (a) who decides whether the father continues to participate when he can no longer meaningfully consent, and (b) whether the father (or his POA/therapist) retains the right to have specific entries removed. The book's supervisor-readability clause provides some oversight, but a capacity-loss fallback must be explicit. Recommended addition to the "Before you start" section, following Protocol 25's POA-then-therapist model.

3. **Protocols 25 and 26: Add explicit existential-content contraindication.** §5.1 names "contemplative practices that surface existential content without a held container" as a Caution-level contraindication. Connection-capsules inherently carry existential weight — the father is creating artifacts for a future in which he may not be cognitively present. Protocol 25 guards against this by banning farewell statements, but neither 25 nor 26 explicitly names the existential-content risk or specifies that a caregiver, therapist, or trusted person should be available (not just "nearby" but aware of the existential dimension) when the practice is attempted. Add a brief note in the "When NOT to use this" or "Before you start" section of both protocols: "This practice may surface feelings about the future, loss, or mortality. If these feelings become acute, the practice yields to care. A caregiver or trusted person should be aware that the practice carries this possibility."

## Non-Critical Revisions

1. **All protocols with breathwork: Add explicit "no breath retention" note.** §5.1 lists breathwork with retention as a Relative contraindication. While none of the 6 protocols include retention, none explicitly exclude it either. Protocol 22 (breath counting) and Protocol 23 (long-exhale breathing) should add a brief note: "Breathe naturally. Do not hold your breath or practice breath retention." This makes the implicit explicit and guards against drift if the father or caregiver modifies the practice.

2. **Protocol 24 (Morning Sensory Anchor), Option A: Strengthen kitchen-safety language.** The current parenthetical safety note about electric kettles is good. Add a brief tie to the care team's ongoing ADL (Activities of Daily Living) assessment: "The care team should confirm that independent kitchen use remains safe at the father's current stage. This assessment is ongoing, not one-time." This aligns with the DRIVING FLAG's model of ongoing verification.

3. **Protocol 22, Option C (wudu-adapted): Add closed-tradition acknowledgment.** Add a brief sentence in the traditions-honored section: "Wudu is an obligatory act of worship in Islam. This adaptation is offered with respect, recognizing that Muslim practitioners may prefer to practice wudu in its original liturgical form."

4. **Protocol 27, traditions-honored section (Mexican ofrendas): Acknowledge sacred communal dimensions.** Add a brief sentence: "The ofrenda tradition carries sacred communal dimensions within Mexican and Mesoamerican communities beyond the structural parallel drawn here."

5. **Protocol 26 (One-Sentence Letter): Add a withdrawability/revocability clause.** Protocol 25 explicitly states consent is "withdrawable" and "retroactively revocable." Protocol 26 should add equivalent language in its "Before you start" section: "The father may ask for any letter to be returned or destroyed at any time. If he later says he does not want the letter to exist, it is destroyed. If the father can no longer give or revoke consent, decision authority passes to his designated Power of Attorney or to the family therapist, in that order." (Note: the capacity-loss portion of this revision is Safety-Critical Revision #1; the withdrawability/revocability language is non-critical but important for consent parity across the connection-capsule set.)

## Summary

The 6 father-alone protocols form a coherent, dignified, and clinically careful set. Dignity framing is consistently strong — the father is never positioned as a patient, and agency is preserved even in the most simplified modifications. Safety flags (DRIVING, SUICIDALITY) are present in all protocols with appropriate severity levels and crisis-card cross-references. Tradition attribution is thorough, with minor closed-tradition sensitivities around wudu adaptation and ofrenda metaphor that need acknowledgment but not removal. The primary safety gap is in the connection-capsule consent architecture: Protocol 25 sets a strong standard (renewable, withdrawable, retroactively revocable, with POA/therapist fallback), but Protocols 26 and 27 do not match it. Since these practices create artifacts that will outlive the father's current cognitive capacity, consent rigor must be uniform across all three. With the 3 safety-critical revisions addressed, this configuration passes quality gate.
