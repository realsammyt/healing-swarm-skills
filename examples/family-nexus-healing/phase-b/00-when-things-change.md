# When Things Change

*How to re-triage when the father's condition progresses, when a child's baseline shifts, or when the mother's capacity changes. This is the progression and retirement appendix for the kit.*

---

## The principle

Things will change. The father's condition may progress. A child may develop new needs or show new signals. The mother's own capacity will shift — some weeks she will be steadier, some weeks she will be closer to the edge. None of this is failure. All of it is information.

The kit is designed to be revised, re-triaged, and in some cases retired. **Practices that have done their work should be honored and released, not clung to.** Practices that stop fitting should be set aside without apology. New practices can be added when the family is ready — but the default under any instability is to *simplify, not add*.

---

## Signs to deepen (add or move to middle-level practice)

The family may be ready to move from entry-level to middle-level practice when:

- The current practice has become something you do without thinking about — a rhythm, not a chore
- Sustained calm for two or more weeks at the current level
- A child begins to self-initiate an expressive practice (draws without being prompted, asks to journal, sings a bedtime song on their own)
- The father asks for his dignity rhythm unprompted, or actively participates in a connection-capsule practice on a good day
- The mother reports fewer edge-of-collapse moments; her daily anchor feels automatic
- The family finds themselves wanting more, not dreading the next practice

When these signs are present, you may consider adding one middle-level practice from the same configuration. Still one at a time. Still for at least a week before assessing.

---

## Signs to slow down or pause (NOT add new; return to entry level only)

**Pause signals are load-bearing.** When these appear, the family returns to entry-level anchors only — the simplest practices, done consistently, with no new additions — until stability returns:

- Any protocol triggering a stop-signal more than once in a week
- Regression in any child's baseline sustained beyond a few days
- Acceleration in the father's cognitive change (a new capability loss, a new day of disorientation, a new hard conversation with the care team)
- The mother reaching or approaching the "I'm at the edge" button (see `mother-alone/re-centering/34-im-at-the-edge-escalation.md`)
- Acute grief event — a transition, a hard visit, a diagnosis update, an unexpected clinical development
- Any crisis response moment (see `00-crisis-response-card.md`)

When pause signals appear, **do not try to add practices, do not try to "work through it with a new technique," do not interpret the difficulty as the kit's failure**. Return to the simplest daily anchors. Call in support. Rest the kit for as long as needed. The kit will be here when the family returns to it.

---

## Signs to retire a practice

Some practices will have done their work. Retiring them is a gift, not a loss. Signs:

- The practice no longer brings up what it used to
- The family outgrows it naturally (a child matures past it; the father's capacity has shifted)
- The practice has become rote rather than meaningful
- A different practice now serves the same function more naturally
- The family simply does not reach for it anymore and does not miss it

When a practice is ready for retirement, a gentle "honor and release" ritual is appropriate:

1. **Name it.** "This practice has served us. The bedtime story voice recording is not something Daddy can do now, but it was something he did, and we are keeping the recording."
2. **Gather the artifacts** if any — drawings, journals, recordings, photographs — in one place where they can be returned to as memory rather than active practice.
3. **Say thank you** to the practice and to the people it held. This is not metaphysical; it is the felt-sense acknowledgment that something ended.
4. **Move on** without guilt. A retired practice is not a forgotten practice. It is a practice whose work is done.

---

## The long-horizon trajectory (months to years)

As the father's condition progresses, his practices shift *toward simpler*, not more complex. Deepening for the father means:

- More silence
- More sensory anchoring (water, touch, song)
- Less narrative
- Less reading
- Less language
- More presence through body, voice, and breath alone

Deepening for the mother means:

- More integration with her life outside the acute container
- More capacity to hold this alongside other things (her work, her friendships, her own interests)
- More discernment about what she can and cannot do in any given season
- Continued weekly grief windows, possibly less frequent as time passes
- A slowly widening circle of peer connection

Deepening for the children means:

- Age-appropriate maturation of what the practices look like (the 5-year-old's drawings become the 7-year-old's stories, the 8-year-old's journals become the 10-year-old's letters to her future self)
- More of their own agency in what they practice and when
- A quiet understanding that the father they have now is the father they have, not a lesser version of a better one
- Growing ability to talk about what is happening, in their own words, when they want to

## When the kit is no longer serving

Some seasons, the kit is not what the family needs. That is fine. Signs that the kit is not currently serving:

- Reading it creates more anxiety than comfort
- The practices feel like another demand on top of already-too-much
- The family therapist or other care team members are providing what the kit was meant to provide, in a more integrated way
- The family has outgrown the kit entirely — they have absorbed the principles and no longer need the structure

**The kit is not an obligation.** If it does not fit for a season, set it down. If it never fits again, that is also fine. The principles are portable; the files are a scaffold, not a sacred text.

---

## What to do if a major clinical change happens

When a major clinical change happens — a new diagnosis, a new capability loss, a new level of supervised visitation, a new hospitalization, a new medication with cognitive effects, a sudden deterioration — **return first to the crisis response card**, then pause all non-anchor practices, then re-triage using the one-page triage.

Do not attempt to incorporate the change into existing practices immediately. Let the family settle first. Let the care team integrate the change into their care plan. Let the mother's nervous system regulate. Then, after a week or two, re-read the triage page and choose one new starting practice appropriate to the new situation.

---

## What to do if things actually get better

Some seasons may surprise you. A good week. A string of good visits. The father on a plateau that lasts longer than expected. The mother finding an unexpected peer. A child who seems to be thriving despite everything.

**Good seasons are real and should be received, not distrusted.** The kit's practices continue to hold in good seasons the same way they do in hard seasons — they give shape to ordinary life, not only to crisis. The family may find that during good weeks, the practices become less effortful and more joyful. This is not a trick. It is the container doing its work.

Celebrate good seasons. Take good photos. Keep good voice recordings. Note what helped. Carry the memory forward into harder seasons that may come later.
