# Contraindications Quick Reference

*Cross-configuration quick reference for when NOT to use specific practices. Full per-protocol flags are in each protocol file.*

---

## Severity tiers

- **Absolute** — do not attempt. The practice is not appropriate for this configuration or this person on this day.
- **Relative** — requires adaptation, professional consultation, or supervision. May be safe with modifications.
- **Caution** — proceed with awareness. Watch for specific signals. Be ready to pause.

---

## Universal rules (apply across all configurations)

**Absolute — never, for any practice in the kit:**
- Anything that puts a child in the role of emotional caregiver to an adult
- Anything asking a child to keep secrets from the other parent or from a trusted adult
- Anything secret from the visit supervisor during a supervised visit
- Anything that frames progressive cognitive change as the person's fault or something they should "try harder" to overcome
- Anything requiring the father to perform his own decline

**Relative — requires care-team awareness:**
- Breathwork with retention components for anyone on medications (consult Contraindications Database; some medications interact with breath practices)
- Practices that surface existential content for the father in the post-diagnosis window (care team should know)
- Writing practices for the mother that surface complicated feelings without a container (pair with trusted contact or therapist)

**Caution — monitor signals:**
- Any photo-based activity (can be beautiful or destabilizing for anyone)
- Cold water exposure (verify no cardiac contraindications)
- Silent practices without a familiar touchstone
- Grief practices on weeks when baseline is already collapsing

---

## Father alone (§5.1 of synthesis)

**Absolute:**
- Practices requiring intact executive sequencing on hard days
- Anything framing him as patient rather than person
- **Any practice or visit plan that assumes driving capacity** — driving status must be verified with the care team at each stage

**Relative:**
- Breathwork with retention (consult DB)
- Long solo silent practice without a familiar touchstone

**Caution:**
- Cold water exposure (verify no cardiac contraindications)
- Contemplative practices surfacing existential content without a held container
- **First-year-post-diagnosis suicidality risk** — the younger-onset literature documents elevated risk in the post-diagnosis window and at capability-loss transitions. Any practice involving existential content should be paired with care-team awareness. The crisis response card applies with full weight.

---

## Mother alone (§5.2 of synthesis)

**Absolute:**
- Any practice that pathologizes her grief, overwhelm, or anger

**Relative:**
- Breathwork without in-person teacher check if she has a trauma history
- Intensive retreats or silent weeks that remove her from the children

**Caution:**
- Writing practices that surface complicated feelings without a container (pair with trusted contact or therapist)
- Grief practices on collapsing-baseline weeks (return to anchor only)
- **Maternal suicidality / complicated-grief risk** — if she reports intrusive thoughts of self-harm, inability to care for the children, or persistent dissociation, activate the escalation path in the crisis-response card BEFORE continuing any practice
- **Substance use as coping** — the anticipatory-grief literature notes elevated alcohol and substance use in young caregivers; any increase in use patterns is a signal the container needs reinforcement

---

## Five-year-old boy alone (§5.3 of synthesis)

**Absolute:**
- Anything framed as a "therapy session" at age 5 (developmentally inappropriate for formal therapeutic frames)

**Relative:**
- Expressive writing practices (use drawing instead)
- Breathwork other than simple exhale practices (developmentally early)

**Caution:**
- Large group healing circles or community settings (overstimulating at this age without a held adult)

---

## Eight-year-old girl alone (§5.4 of synthesis)

**Absolute:**
- Anything that reinforces parentification — asking her to help with emotional labor, to watch her brother during stressful visits, to preempt adult needs
- **Anything asking her to keep grief, visit content, or feelings secret from the other parent or a trusted adult** — secret-keeping is elevated in this family's configuration (court involvement, supervised visits) and at 8 she is developmentally at the age where she can be asked and should never be

**Relative:**
- Intensive journaling without adult support (may surface content she cannot process alone)
- Group grief settings (may be too abstract; prefer family or individual)

---

## Mother with kids (§5.5 of synthesis)

**Absolute:**
- Any practice that puts children in the role of emotional caregivers to the mother

**Relative:**
- Family grief circles without a held adult facilitator

**Caution:**
- Overloading the bedtime ritual with additional content (keep it boring and reliable)
- Grief conversations during times when the mother herself is dysregulated (use fast-reset first, then conversation)

---

## Father with kids — SUPERVISED (§5.6 of synthesis)

This is the most critical configuration and the flags are thorough.

**Absolute:**
- Anything requiring the father to remember, plan, or execute complex sequences
- Anything secret from the supervisor
- Anything asking children to manage the father's affect or keep secrets
- Activities with fall risk or complex equipment

**Relative:**
- Competitive games (scorekeeping may spotlight cognitive limits)
- Crowded or noisy environments
- Long unstructured stretches
- Activities requiring reading small print or complex instructions
- Breathwork with retention (consult DB)
- **Disinhibition or significant condition progression** — some progressive cognitive conditions (frontotemporal variants especially) can produce disinhibition that may put the father at risk of behavior outside the agreed container. If this appears or intensifies, the container itself must be renegotiated with the care team and the court, NOT absorbed within the existing visit structure.

**Caution:**
- **Looking at old photos, including any photo-based activity or prepared photo book** — can be beautiful or destabilizing. The preparer (mother, with input from the care team) is responsible for selecting images the father tolerates on his current baseline rather than on the baseline of the relationship. The supervisor must be briefed in advance that photos may destabilize. Observable distress signals: sudden silence, pulling away from the image, repeated confused questions, tearfulness the father cannot settle. If any appear, redirect without fanfare to a sensory anchor (water, breath, touch, song). Either the mother or the supervisor can call a stop.
- **End-of-visit distress / separation difficulty** — visit endings are a known stress point when cognition is shifting. The beginning-and-ending rituals in the entry level exist specifically to make transitions readable and give everyone a graceful way out. The supervisor should be briefed to assist with closure.

---

## Family together (§5.7 of synthesis)

**Absolute:**
- Any configuration that violates the court-ordered arrangement or safety boundaries

**Relative:**
- Extended all-day family time in the acute phases of the father's condition
- High-stakes milestone events where performance pressure is inescapable

**Caution:**
- Anything that forces the father into a "be like before" frame

---

## If any flag crosses into crisis territory

See `00-crisis-response-card.md`. The crisis-response framework supersedes every other practice in the kit.
