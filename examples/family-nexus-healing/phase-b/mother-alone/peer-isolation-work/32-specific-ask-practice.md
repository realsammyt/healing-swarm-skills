# Specific Ask Practice

**Configuration:** mother-alone
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 15 minutes to prepare (once); 10 seconds to use (each time)
**Energy cost:** low (once prepared)

## Why this practice

People will say "Let me know how I can help." They mean it. But the mother, exhausted, overwhelmed, and carrying a situation most people cannot imagine, will almost always say "I'm fine" or "I don't know" or "Thanks, I will" — and then never ask. The offer evaporates. The isolation deepens.

The Phase A synthesis (§3.4) identifies a specific-ask practice as a concrete tool: a pre-written list of five actionable asks, kept on a card in her phone case, ready for the moment when "Let me know how I can help" arrives. The preparation happens once, in advance, when she has the bandwidth to think. The use happens in the moment, when she does not.

This practice draws on the Jewish concept of *bikkur cholim*, which reframes help not as charity from the strong to the weak but as an *obligation of the community*. When someone says "How can I help?", they are fulfilling an obligation. The mother is not burdening them. She is letting them do what they already want to do. The barrier is not their willingness. The barrier is her ability to name what she needs.

## You'll need

- A card — an index card, a piece of paper, a note in your phone. Something you can keep in your phone case or wallet.
- A pen.
- 15 minutes of clarity.

## Before you start

- You do this once, in advance. Not in the moment when someone offers help. In advance.
- You do not need to be in crisis to prepare this card. Prepare it on a good day so it is ready for a hard one.
- The asks on the card are not dramatic. They are ordinary. That is the point. The dramatic needs are handled by the care team. The ordinary needs — groceries, an hour off, a meal — are what friends and family can actually do.
- On a collapsing-baseline week, see Modifications below.

## When NOT to use this

- **MATERNAL SUICIDALITY FLAG (Caution):** If you are experiencing intrusive thoughts of self-harm, inability to care for the children, or persistent dissociation, your most important ask is not on this card. Call 988 (US) or your local crisis line. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`. This practice is for the ordinary asks — the crisis asks have their own protocol.
- **SUBSTANCE USE FLAG (Caution):** If alcohol or substances have become part of how you cope, be aware: asks related to "bring me a bottle of wine" or similar should not be on the card. If you notice this impulse, talk to your therapist. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

### Step 1: Write five asks

Sit down with your card. Write five things that another person could actually do for you this week. Keep them specific, actionable, and bounded. "Help me" is not an ask. "Pick up milk and bread from [store] on Tuesday" is an ask.

Here are five examples. Yours will be different:

1. **Groceries:** "Could you pick up [list of 5 items] from [store name]? I can Venmo you."
2. **Park time:** "Could you take the kids to [park name] for an hour on [day]? They know you and they'll be fine."
3. **A meal:** "Could you drop off a meal on [day]? Anything works. The kids eat everything except [allergy/preference]."
4. **Childcare hours:** "Could you watch the kids for 2 hours on [day] so I can [sleep / go to an appointment / sit in my car in silence]?"
5. **A phone call:** "Could you call me on [day] evening for 15 minutes? I just need to talk to an adult."

Each ask has: what, when, how long, and any relevant details. The person receiving the ask can say yes or no. Either answer is fine.

### Step 2: Keep the card accessible

Put the card in your phone case, your wallet, your kitchen drawer, or save the note on your phone's home screen. It must be reachable without thinking. When the moment comes — when someone says "What can I do?" — you will not have the bandwidth to think. The card thinks for you.

### Step 3: When the offer comes, name one

When someone says "Let me know how I can help" or "What do you need?" or "I wish I could do something," take out the card (or open the note) and say one of the five asks. Just one. Out loud.

"Actually, yes — could you pick up the kids from school on Thursday? That would help a lot."

That is the whole practice in the moment. The card removes the barrier between the offer and the ask. You do not have to generate the ask in real time. It is already written.

### Step 4: Update the card as needs change

Every few weeks, look at the card. Some asks may no longer be relevant. New needs may have emerged. Update the list. Keep it at five — enough to have options, few enough to remember.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In yourself:** guilt after asking (this is normal — it does not mean you did something wrong), shame that you need help (also normal, also not true), compulsive over-asking that strains relationships, inability to ask at all despite having the card.
- **What to do:** Guilt and shame after asking are common in caregivers. They are signals of the isolation, not of wrongdoing. If you cannot ask at all — if the card exists but the words will not come — that is information for your therapist. If the inability is accompanied by other signs of distress, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.

## Modifications

- **Collapsing-baseline version:** You do not write the card yourself. You ask someone — your therapist, your mother, a close friend — to write it with you or for you. "I need help writing down what I need help with" is itself a legitimate ask.
- **Text-message version:** If saying the ask out loud feels impossible, prepare it as a text message template. When someone offers help, send the text instead of speaking.
- **Smaller list (3 asks):** If five feels overwhelming, write three. If three feels overwhelming, write one. One ask, written down, ready to deploy, is better than a perfect list that does not exist.
- **Reverse version (for friends reading this):** If you are the friend, do not say "Let me know how I can help." Say: "I'm going to the grocery store — what do you need?" or "I'm free Thursday afternoon — I'd like to take the kids to the park." The specific offer is easier to accept than the open-ended one.

## After

No reflection required. You asked. Someone helped. That is enough.

Over time, you may notice that asking gets easier. Or it may not. Both are fine. The card is always there.

## Traditions honored

- **Bikkur cholim (Jewish visiting the sick)** — the reframe that matters most here: in Jewish law, the obligation to help is on the *community*, not on the sufferer to ask. When the mother names an ask, she is not burdening the asker. She is helping them fulfill an obligation they already hold. The Talmud (Nedarim 40a) teaches that visiting the sick removes one-sixtieth of their suffering — not through grand gestures but through presence and practical help. The specific-ask practice operationalizes bikkur cholim for a modern context. (Fred Rosner, "The Obligation of Visiting the Sick," in *Medicine and Jewish Law*, 1990; Joseph Telushkin, *A Code of Jewish Ethics*, Vol. 2, 2009.)
- **Ubuntu (Southern African philosophical tradition)** — *umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu* — a person is a person through other persons. Asking for help is not weakness. It is the activation of the relational web that makes personhood possible. The mother who asks is not diminished. She is enacting the fundamental structure of human interdependence. (Desmond Tutu, *No Future Without Forgiveness*, 1999.)
- **Christian mutual aid (Acts 2:44–45)** — the early church practice described in Acts: "All the believers were together and had everything in common. They sold property and possessions to give to anyone who had need." The tradition of concrete, material mutual support — not abstract goodwill but groceries, childcare, meals — has deep roots in Christian community practice. (Christine Pohl, *Making Room: Recovering Hospitality as a Christian Tradition*, 1999.)
