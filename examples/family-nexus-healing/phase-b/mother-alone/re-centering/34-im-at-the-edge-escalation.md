# I'm at the Edge — Escalation Protocol

**Configuration:** mother-alone
**Level:** entry (READ BEFORE NEEDED)
**Duration:** as long as it takes
**Energy cost:** variable

## Why this practice

This is the most important protocol in the mother-alone section of this kit. It exists for the moment when the container is breaking — when overwhelm, grief, rage, dissociation, or despair have crossed from "I'm struggling" to "I cannot do this." The Phase A synthesis (§3.4) names the warning signs directly: "prolonged dissociation, anger that frightens her, inability to cry when grief is clearly present, sleep severely disrupted for multiple nights, thoughts of harm, inability to be present with the children." These are signals that the container needs reinforcement, not that she is failing.

**Read this protocol BEFORE you need it.** Fill in the contacts NOW, today, while you can think clearly. When the edge arrives, you will not have the bandwidth to search for phone numbers. The numbers will already be here.

**The edge is not the end.** Reaching out is never weakness. The fact that you are reading this means the container is still holding. You are still here. That is enough to start.

## You'll need

- This document, filled in with your contacts (see Step 0).
- A phone.
- Nothing else.

## When NOT to use this

This protocol has no "when NOT to use" section. If you are at the edge, use it. There is no wrong time to reach out.

- **MATERNAL SUICIDALITY FLAG (ACTIVE):** If you are having thoughts of ending your life, call 988 NOW. Do not finish reading this protocol. Call first. Read later. You can also text 988 or text HOME to 741741. Someone is there, right now, who understands caregiver crisis and complicated grief. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.
- **SUBSTANCE USE FLAG (ACTIVE):** If you are intoxicated or under the influence and caring for children, call your "I can't care for the kids right now" contact immediately. The children's safety comes first. Your recovery comes next. No judgment. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.

## The practice

### Situation 1: Children are NOT present

You are alone. You are at the edge. The children are at school, with another adult, asleep in another room.

**Step 1: Name it.**
Say it out loud, to yourself: "I am at the edge." Naming it is not drama. Naming it is accuracy. It interrupts the spiral by one beat.

**Step 2: Pick your level.**

- **If you are having thoughts of harming yourself:** Call 988 NOW. Or text 988. Or text HOME to 741741. Do not wait. Do not talk yourself out of it. The person who answers understands caregiver crisis and complicated grief. You are not wasting their time.

- **If you are overwhelmed but not in danger:** Call your therapist. If your therapist is not available, call your trusted person. Say: "I'm having a really hard time. Can you talk for a few minutes?" You do not need to explain everything. You need to hear another voice.

- **If you cannot make a call:** Text. Text your therapist, your trusted person, or 741741 (Crisis Text Line). Type: "I'm struggling. I need help." That is enough.

**Step 3: Ground while you wait.**
If you have called or texted and are waiting for a response, do one of the fast resets from `33-fast-reset-sixty-seconds.md`:
- Cold water on your face.
- Three slow breaths: "Here." "Ground." "Enough."
- Hands flat on a surface, name three things you see.

The reset does not fix the edge. It holds you at the edge until help arrives.

**Step 4: Let help come.**
When the person answers — the crisis counselor, the therapist, the trusted friend — let them help. You do not need to perform composure. You do not need to explain the whole situation. You can say: "I'm a caregiver. I have two young kids. My husband has a progressive condition. I'm overwhelmed and I need to talk." That is enough context. They will take it from there.

---

### Situation 2: Children ARE present and you cannot regulate

This is the hardest scenario. The children are in the house. You are at the edge. You cannot calm down, cannot stop crying, cannot function, cannot be the parent they need right now.

**Step 1: Secure the children.**
If the children are safe where they are (playing, watching a show, in their rooms), they can stay. If they are not safe, move them to a safe space — their room, the living room with a familiar show on, a space with no hazards. Tell them: **"Mommy needs a few minutes. I'm going to be right here. You are safe."** That sentence is enough. You do not need to explain. Children understand "Mommy needs a minute" better than adults give them credit for.

**Step 2: Call your "I can't care for the kids right now" contact.**
Call the person whose name you wrote in Step 0. Say: "I need you to come be with the kids. I'm okay, but I need help right now." Or: "I'm not okay and I need someone here." Either version is true. Either version works.

If that person does not answer, call your backup. If your backup does not answer, call your therapist. If your therapist does not answer, call 988.

**Step 3: Ground while you wait.**
While waiting for someone to arrive or call back, do a fast reset (`33-fast-reset-sixty-seconds.md`). If you can, sit where the children can see you but you are not responsible for engaging with them. Your presence — even your imperfect, tearful, shaking presence — is enough for them in the short term. Children are more resilient to witnessing a parent's honest distress than to sensing a parent's hidden distress.

**Step 4: When help arrives, hand off.**
When the other adult arrives — in person or by phone — let them take over with the children. Go to another room. Close the door. Breathe. Cry. Call your therapist. Call 988 if you need to. The children are held. You can fall apart now, safely.

---

### Situation 3: Ongoing collapse (not a single moment — multiple days)

If the edge is not a single moment but a state that has lasted days — if you have not been sleeping, if you cannot eat, if you cannot be present with the children, if you are dissociating regularly, if your baseline has been collapsing for more than a few days:

**Step 1: This is not a fast-reset situation.** This is a "call your therapist and say 'I need more support than I'm getting'" situation. If you do not have a therapist, call 988 and ask for a referral.

**Step 2: Name what you need.** You may need: medication adjustment, increased therapy frequency, temporary in-home help with the children, respite care, a medical evaluation for yourself. You are allowed to need these things.

**Step 3: Let the care team help.** Your family therapist, the court-appointed social worker, your physician, your therapist — these people are part of the crisis response system. Telling them "I am not coping" is not a failure. It is the system working as designed.

## What the edge is NOT

- The edge is NOT a verdict on your parenting.
- The edge is NOT proof that you cannot handle this.
- The edge is NOT a sign that the children should be taken from you.
- The edge IS information that the container needs reinforcement.
- The edge IS a signal that you need more support, not more willpower.
- The edge IS something that other caregivers in your situation experience. You are not alone in this.

**Reaching out is never weakness. The edge is not the end.**

## Signals (for others who may be reading this)

If you are a friend, family member, therapist, or care-team member, and you are reading this because you are worried about the mother:

- **Trust her if she says she is at the edge.** Do not minimize.
- **Come when she calls.** If she asks you to be with the kids, come. If she asks you to talk, talk. If she asks you to just be in the house, be in the house.
- **Do not ask "What happened?"** Ask: "What do you need right now?"
- **If she cannot tell you what she needs,** offer specifics: "I can take the kids for two hours. I can sit here while you sleep. I can call your therapist for you."
- **If you believe she is in danger,** call 988 on her behalf and stay with her until help arrives.

## Modifications

- **Collapsing-baseline version:** If your baseline has been collapsing, this protocol IS the practice. Everything else in the kit pauses. Your only job is to get through today with the children safe and to reach out for help.
- **Non-verbal version:** If you cannot speak, text. Text 988, text 741741 (Crisis Text Line), text your therapist, text your trusted person. "I need help" in a text message is a valid escalation.
- **Non-English version:** 988 offers services in English and Spanish. The Crisis Text Line (741741) operates in English. If your primary language is neither, ask your therapist to help you identify a crisis line in your language and add it to the contacts above.

## After

After the edge passes — after the call, after the person comes, after the children are held, after you have slept or cried or sat in silence:

You are still here. The edge passed. The container held, even if it bent.

Talk to your therapist about what happened. Not to analyze it. To let someone know. To adjust the container if it needs adjusting. To add supports if supports are needed.

You do not owe anyone an explanation for reaching out. You activated the system that was built for exactly this moment. That is what the system is for.

## Traditions honored

- **Healing-swarm Crisis Response framework** — the RECOGNIZE-STOP-GROUND-ASSESS-RESPOND-FOLLOW UP structure that underlies all crisis protocols in this kit. The escalation protocol operationalizes this framework for the mother-alone configuration.
- **Bikkur cholim (Jewish visiting the sick)** — the obligation is on the community, not on the sufferer. When the mother calls for help, she is not burdening anyone. She is activating an obligation the community already holds. The person who comes is fulfilling a commandment, not doing a favor. (Talmud, Shabbat 127a; Nedarim 40a.)
- **Ubuntu (Southern African philosophical tradition)** — *umuntu ngumuntu ngabantu* — a person is a person through other persons. The mother at the edge is not diminished. She is a person whose web of connection needs to activate. Calling for help is an act of Ubuntu — it trusts that personhood is relational and that the community will hold what the individual cannot hold alone. (Desmond Tutu, *No Future Without Forgiveness*, 1999.)
