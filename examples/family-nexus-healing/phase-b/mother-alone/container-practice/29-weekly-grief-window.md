# Weekly Grief Window

**Configuration:** mother-alone
**Level:** entry
**Duration:** 1 hour (scheduled)
**Energy cost:** moderate

## Why this practice

The Phase A synthesis (§3.4) grounds this practice in Stroebe and Schut's (1999) dual-process model of coping with bereavement, which proposes that healthy grieving involves *oscillation* between loss-oriented coping (being with the grief) and restoration-oriented coping (attending to ongoing life). Both are needed. Being stuck in either is unhealthy. For anticipatory grief — grief for a person who is still here but changing — the oscillation is especially important: a mother in continuous grief mode cannot parent effectively, cannot work, cannot rest.

The practical application is *scheduled grief time* — one hour per week with a specific ritual container. This is not repression. The hour is real. What the structure does is honor the grief by giving it dedicated space while protecting the rest of the week from being entirely consumed. It also protects the children, who are deeply attuned to a parent's emotional state and who should not be asked to absorb a parent in continuous grief mode.

The Jewish mourning tradition offers the most articulated model of structured grief in world practice: *aninut* (the raw hours), *shiva* (seven days), *shloshim* (thirty days), *yahrzeit* (annual). Each stage has its container. The grief is not denied. It is given a shape. This weekly window draws on that structural wisdom.

## You'll need

- One hour, scheduled in advance. Same day each week if possible.
- A private space where you will not be interrupted. The children must NOT be present. Another adult — a family member, a friend, a paid sitter — holds the children during this hour.
- A candle and a way to light it.
- Optionally: music you choose (not music that ambushes you — music you choose), a journal, photos, a letter-writing kit, tissues.
- A timer set for 60 minutes (or use the candle as your timer if it burns approximately one hour).

## Before you start

- The children are with another adult. This is non-negotiable. Your grief hour is not a parenting hour.
- You have told no one what you will "do" during this hour because you do not owe anyone a report. This is your hour.
- You can stop early. Blowing the candle out at 30 minutes is not a failure. Some weeks the hour is too much. Some weeks it is not enough. Both are true.
- You do not need to cry. You do not need to feel anything in particular. Some weeks the hour will be full. Some weeks it will be numb. Some weeks you will spend it staring at the wall. All of these are the practice.
- On a collapsing-baseline week, see Modifications below.

## When NOT to use this

- **MATERNAL SUICIDALITY FLAG (Caution):** If you are experiencing intrusive thoughts of self-harm, inability to care for the children, or persistent dissociation, do not enter the grief window alone. Call 988 (US) or your local crisis line. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`. If you want to do the grief window during a difficult period, do it with your therapist present — by phone or in person.
- **SUBSTANCE USE FLAG (Caution):** If alcohol or substances have become part of how you cope, the grief window should not include them. If you notice that the grief window has become a drinking hour, that is a signal the container needs professional reinforcement. Talk to your therapist or a trusted contact. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.
- **Collapsing-baseline week:** If your baseline has been collapsing for several days, the grief window may be too much. See the collapsing-baseline modification below.
- **When children are present:** Never. This practice is structurally dependent on the children being elsewhere.
- **If any flag crosses into crisis territory, see `00-crisis-response-card.md`.**

## The practice

### Step 1: Open the window

Light the candle. This is the beginning. The candle marks the threshold: grief time is now. You do not need to say anything. The lighting is enough.

### Step 2: Be with what is here

For the next hour, you may do any of the following. You do not need to do all of them. You do not need to do any of them. You may simply sit.

- **Cry.** If tears come, let them. If they do not come, do not force them.
- **Write.** In a journal, on loose paper, on your phone. Write to the father. Write to yourself. Write to no one. Write about the anger, the love, the exhaustion, the fear, the tenderness, the fury. No one will read this unless you choose to share it.
- **Speak.** Say what you need to say out loud, to the room, to the candle, to God or the universe or no one. Speaking grief aloud is an ancient practice — the psalms of lament (especially 22, 88, 130) give voice to complaint without requiring resolution.
- **Look at photos.** Deliberately, on your terms. Photos you choose, not photos that ambush you in a phone scroll. Photos of the father as he was, as he is. Photos of the family. Photos of yourself.
- **Listen to music.** Music you associate with him, with your life together, with the grief, with something beyond the grief. Choose it in advance if possible. Surprises in the grief window can be destabilizing.
- **Sit.** Just sit. Let the hour hold you. Not every grief window needs to be active. Sitting with the candle is enough.

### Step 3: Close the window

When the timer sounds — or when you are ready, whichever comes first — blow out the candle. This is the end. The window closes. The grief does not disappear, but the dedicated space for it is done for this week.

Take a breath. Drink water. Wash your face if you need to. Take five minutes of transition before the children return or you re-enter the household.

The grief will seep into the rest of the week. That is normal. What the window does is give the grief a home so it does not have to live in every room.

## Signals to slow down or stop

- **In yourself:** dissociation during the hour (feeling detached from your body, watching yourself from above), grief that will not ease after the candle is blown out and bleeds into hours, inability to return to parenting after the window closes, intrusive images or thoughts that frighten you, a sense of falling rather than sitting with grief.
- **What to do:** Blow the candle out early. That is always allowed. Get a glass of water. If the distress will not settle, call your therapist or a trusted contact. If you are in acute distress — thoughts of self-harm, inability to function — call 988 or your local crisis line. See `00-crisis-response-card.md`.

## Modifications

- **Shorter version (30 minutes):** Light the candle. Set the timer for 30 minutes. Same practice, half the time. Some weeks 30 minutes is the right container.
- **Collapsing-baseline version:** Skip the grief window entirely this week. On a collapsing-baseline week, your only practice is `28-daily-five-minute-anchor.md` in its collapsing-baseline form ("I am still here. That is enough"). The grief window returns when your baseline stabilizes.
- **With a therapist present:** If the grief window feels too raw to do alone, schedule it during or immediately before a therapy session. The therapist holds the container. This is not weakness — it is wisdom.
- **Walking version:** If sitting in a room with a candle feels claustrophobic, the grief window can be a walk — alone, with no destination, no podcast, no phone call. The walk begins when you leave the door. The walk ends when you return. The container is the walk itself.
- **Letter-writing version:** Each week, write one letter to the father. You do not send it. You keep it or you destroy it — your choice. The writing is the practice, not the product.

## After

No reflection required. The candle is out. The window is closed. That is enough.

If you notice patterns over weeks — certain music that helps, certain topics that surface, certain weeks that are harder — you may note them. These patterns may be useful for your therapist. But tracking them is optional.

## Traditions honored

- **Stroebe and Schut dual-process model** — the bereavement research framework proposing that healthy grieving oscillates between loss-oriented and restoration-oriented coping. The grief window is a structured loss-orientation practice; the rest of the week is restoration-orientation. (Stroebe & Schut, "The Dual Process Model of Coping with Bereavement," *Death Studies*, 1999.)
- **Jewish mourning structure** — the most articulated system of structured grief in world practice. *Aninut* (raw grief before burial), *shiva* (seven days of community-held mourning), *shloshim* (thirty days of gradual return), *yahrzeit* (annual remembrance). Each stage has a container. The grief is given a shape, not denied. The candle in this practice draws on the *ner neshama* (soul candle) lit during shiva and on yahrzeit. (Maurice Lamm, *The Jewish Way in Death and Mourning*, 1969/2000; Anita Diamant, *Living a Jewish Life*, 1991.)
- **Psalms of lament** — the Hebrew Bible's tradition of speaking grief, anger, and complaint directly, without requiring resolution or piety. Psalms 22, 88, and 130 model a prayer that says "this is unbearable" without pretending otherwise. The grief window gives space for this voice. (Brueggemann, *The Message of the Psalms*, 1984.)
