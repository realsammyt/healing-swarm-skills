# Clinician Handoff Sheet

*A one-page document the family may choose to share with their family therapist, visit supervisor, court-appointed caseworker, or primary care physician. This is the ONLY document in this kit designed to leave the household.*

---

## What the family is using

The family is working with a supportive-material kit called **Family Nexus Healing**, developed in response to a specific family situation involving younger-onset progressive cognitive change in one parent, court-ordered supervised visitation between that parent and two young children (5 and 8), separate households in the same city, and anticipatory grief held by the other parent.

The kit contains ~36 small, concrete practices organized by family configuration (supervised visits, mother with children, each child individually, father's own daily rhythm, mother's own container work, and rare family-together moments). The practices are drawn from a research synthesis coalescing dementia-care research, attachment theory, polyvagal theory, narrative medicine, ritual studies, and traditional healing wisdom from many cultures.

The underlying research is available at the family's request. The kit is deliberately ecumenical and does not privilege any single tradition.

## What this is NOT

- **Not a treatment plan.** The kit is supportive material, not a clinical intervention.
- **Not a diagnosis** or a claim about what is happening clinically.
- **Not a cure.** "Healing" in the kit's usage means preserving connection, dignity, presence, and the capacity to metabolize grief — not restoring lost cognitive function.
- **Not a substitute** for your professional role, your clinical judgment, the family's care team, or the court's protective structures. The kit is explicitly designed to *complement* professional care and legal structures, not work around them.
- **Not a prescription.** Practices are offered; nothing is imposed. The family is free to set aside anything that does not fit.

## What the family may be doing

On any given week, the family is likely practicing one or two of the following, at their own pace and discretion:

- **Bracketing supervised visits** with a small consistent beginning ritual and a small consistent closing ritual, to give the visit a readable shape
- **Body-based co-regulation** with the children (humming, slow breath together, a known song) for hot moments or post-visit transitions
- **Answering hard questions** from the children ("why can't Daddy live with us") using age-appropriate scripts said once, calmly, rather than repeated anxiously
- **A bedtime ritual** as the primary daily secure base for the children — same shape every night, non-negotiable even when everything else is chaotic
- **The mother's five-minute daily anchor practice** — a brief morning practice drawn from a tradition that resonates for her (Jewish Mussar check-in, Christian Ignatian Examen, Buddhist metta for herself, or a secular anchor)
- **The mother's weekly grief window** — one scheduled hour per week for structured grief, bracketed, with the rest of the week left for other states (drawn from Stroebe & Schut's dual-process model of coping)
- **Connection-capsule practices** with the father on good days — voice recordings of bedtime stories, one-sentence letters, photographs of ordinary moments — designed to create carryforward memory without asking the father to perform his own decline
- **The father's daily dignity rhythm** — one recurring sensory anchor (sound, water, breath, movement) done the same way every day, preserving his agency and role

## How you can observe

The practices are visibly harmless. In a supervised visit, what you may see is:
- A quiet hello at the start
- A shared sensory activity (walking and looking for leaves; reading a picture book aloud; a sensory bin on a low table; singing a lullaby; sitting near water and listening)
- A quiet closing (three "glads" from each person, or just a hug and "see you next time")

In the mother's home, what you may hear about:
- A stable bedtime rhythm
- Age-appropriate conversations when the children ask hard questions
- Drawing and simple journals the children may or may not show you

None of this should feel foreign to your clinical training. Most of it is standard supportive practice, concretized and given to the family in directly-usable form.

## How to feed back what is working or not working

The family benefits from your observation. If you notice:

- A child showing sustained distress (regression, school refusal, withdrawal beyond a week, any statement of self-harm) — please loop in pediatric mental health or the family therapist, and let the family know.
- The father showing signs of disinhibition, acute agitation, or suicidality post-diagnosis or at a capability-loss transition — this is a known elevated risk in younger-onset populations and warrants immediate care-team attention.
- The mother appearing to approach collapse (inability to be present with the children, sustained dissociation, substance use as coping, thoughts of harm) — please activate the crisis escalation path with the family.
- A practice that seems to be destabilizing rather than settling — please say so. The family is empowered to set practices aside, and your observation helps them do so sooner.

The kit does not expect clinician feedback to flow through any special channel. Your usual supervision, therapy, or consultation channels are the right places. The family's therapist is the natural integration point for feedback from multiple observers (including you and the mother).

## What the kit is NOT asking of you

- Not asking you to learn the kit. You do not need to read the research, the protocols, or any other document in it.
- Not asking you to endorse, recommend, or prescribe the practices. The family is using them under their own agency.
- Not asking you to change your role. Your clinical judgment is primary.

## Honoring the court-involved context

The kit is privacy-first and local-first. None of the family's practice data leaves their household unless they explicitly choose to share. The only document designed to leave is this sheet. Nothing between the father and children is secret from you as supervisor — the practices are explicitly "readable by a supervisor," with no encoded language, no whispered asides, no secrets children are asked to keep.

The supervisor's role is understood in the kit as welcome witness, not adversarial authority. The family's practices are structured so that your presence protects everyone: the father from potential mischaracterization, the children from loyalty binds, and the mother's advocacy.

## If you have concerns

Contact the family's family therapist or the supervising court directly, through your normal channels. The kit is not a substitute for those channels. If you believe the family should stop using the kit, or should use it differently, please tell the family.

## The one thing the family most needs from you

Witness. The practices are designed to be held by your presence, not evaluated against a checklist. Your regulated adult attention — in the visit, in the therapy room, in the court interaction — is itself part of the container that makes these practices possible.
