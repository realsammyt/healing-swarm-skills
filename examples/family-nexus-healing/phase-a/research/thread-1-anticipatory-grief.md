# Thread 1: Anticipatory Grief & Loss-of-Self

*A research chapter for the Family Nexus Healing Project — Phase A*

---

This chapter addresses a form of grief that has no funeral, no clear beginning, and no socially recognized end: the grief that arrives when a loved one begins to change beyond recognition while still physically present. For a family facing younger-onset progressive cognitive change, the territory is doubly unmapped — most of the literature, infrastructure, and cultural scripts were built for people in their seventies and eighties. A father under fifty does not fit those scripts. A mother under fifty holding two young children does not fit them either. This chapter gathers what *is* known, names what is *not* known, and draws on older human traditions that held families through slow losses long before modern palliative infrastructure existed.

The framing throughout is "may help," never "will cure." Grief is not a problem to be solved. It is a territory to be walked, with as much company and as much container as a family can find.

---

## 1. Anticipatory Grief: The Core Literature

The phrase "anticipatory grief" was introduced by Erich Lindemann in his 1944 *American Journal of Psychiatry* study of Coconut Grove fire survivors and bereaved families, where he observed mourning that began *before* a death had occurred (Lindemann, 1944). The concept lay relatively dormant until Therese Rando, a clinical thanatologist, developed it substantially across three decades of work.

Rando's most cited contribution is her 2000 edited volume *Clinical Dimensions of Anticipatory Mourning* (Research Press), where she argues the term "anticipatory grief" is itself too narrow. She prefers **anticipatory mourning**, which she defines as encompassing seven operations occurring simultaneously along three timelines (past, present, future): grief and mourning, coping, interaction, psychosocial reorganization, planning, balancing conflicting demands, and facilitating an appropriate death (Rando, 2000). The crucial insight for families facing progressive cognitive change is that anticipatory mourning is *not* grief rehearsal. It does not "use up" grief in advance. Families who mourn before a death still mourn after it. What anticipatory mourning does offer is an opportunity to do relational work — repair, witness, say what needs saying — while the loved one is still reachable.

Rando also names a risk: **premature detachment**. When the grief of anticipating loss becomes unbearable, family members may pull away emotionally from the person who is still alive. The person dies twice: once relationally, once biologically. For a family under court-ordered supervised visitation, where contact is already structurally limited, the risk of premature detachment is amplified. The literature suggests this is worth naming openly.

Coelho and Barbosa's 2017 systematic review in the *American Journal of Hospice and Palliative Medicine*, "Family Anticipatory Grief: An Integrative Literature Review," synthesized findings across dozens of studies and found that anticipatory grief in family caregivers is associated with depressive symptoms, caregiver burden, and — importantly — with *better* post-loss adjustment when it occurs in the context of adequate support (Coelho & Barbosa, 2017). Without support, anticipatory grief predicts complicated bereavement. With support, it may be protective. The variable is not whether grief arrives early but whether the grieving person is held while grieving.

Garand and colleagues' work on pre-loss grief in dementia caregivers is also foundational. Garand, Lingler, Deardorf et al. (2012), writing in the *Journal of the American Geriatrics Society*, found that pre-loss grief scores in dementia caregivers were comparable to, and sometimes exceeded, the grief scores of recently bereaved individuals (Garand et al., 2012). Dementia caregivers grieve as intensely *before* the death as most people do after. This is not pathological. It is a rational response to a loss that is already occurring in a form the culture has not yet learned to recognize.

A fourth source worth naming is Marwit and Meuser's Caregiver Grief Inventory (Meuser & Marwit, 2001, *The Gerontologist*), which was developed specifically to measure grief in dementia caregivers and which tracks grief across disease stages. Their longitudinal work showed that grief does not follow a linear curve; it surges at transitions — diagnosis, loss of a specific capacity, change in living situation, the moment of no-longer-being-recognized (Meuser & Marwit, 2001). For a family in a progressive condition, these transition points are places where grief-support should be deliberately concentrated.

## 2. Younger-Onset Advocacy and Lived-Experience Sources

The peer-reviewed literature on younger-onset progressive cognitive change is thin. This must be named explicitly: **most published research describes people over 65**. Diagnostic pathways, medication trials, support groups, and grief frameworks were built around a late-life timeline. A father under 50 walks into a clinical landscape that was not built for him, and his partner walks into a caregiving role without the age-peer solidarity older caregivers can find. The compensation for this gap is lived-experience and advocacy literature, which is unusually strong in this field because people with younger-onset diagnoses have written back.

**Kate Swaffer**, an Australian advocate diagnosed with younger-onset dementia in her 40s, co-founded Dementia Alliance International (DAI), an international organization run by and for people with a dementia diagnosis. Her 2016 book *What the Hell Happened to My Brain? Living Beyond Dementia* (Jessica Kingsley Publishers) introduced the concept of **"prescribed disengagement"** — the phenomenon where newly diagnosed people are, in effect, told to go home, get their affairs in order, and prepare to die, rather than being offered rehabilitation, meaningful engagement, or disability-rights framings of their condition (Swaffer, 2016). Swaffer argues that prescribed disengagement itself accelerates decline by removing purpose, social role, and cognitive challenge. For a father under 50, the implication is direct: *how* he is told what is happening, and *what he is invited into afterward*, matters enormously. Prescribed disengagement is preventable. It is a cultural habit, not a clinical necessity.

**Richard Taylor**, a psychologist who lived with Alzheimer's-type cognitive change, wrote *Alzheimer's from the Inside Out* (Health Professions Press, 2007). His essays are among the most lucid first-person accounts of what it is like to notice oneself changing from the inside. Taylor insisted that the person with the diagnosis remains a person with a diagnosis — not a disease with a person attached — and he wrote with particular force about the loneliness of being spoken *about* rather than *to* in clinical and family settings (Taylor, 2007).

**Wendy Mitchell**, a British woman diagnosed with younger-onset Alzheimer's in her late 50s, wrote *Somebody I Used to Know* (Bloomsbury, 2018) and the follow-up *What I Wish People Knew About Dementia* (2022). Mitchell's contribution is methodological as much as narrative: she documented the concrete strategies she developed to continue living well — a "memory room" of photographs, written notes to her future self, a deliberate practice of naming good days as good days. She modeled what agency under progressive cognitive change can look like (Mitchell, 2018, 2022).

A fifth lived-experience source is **Christine Bryden**, an Australian who was diagnosed in 1995 at age 46 and who wrote *Dancing with Dementia* (Jessica Kingsley, 2005). Bryden's account is particularly notable because she wrote across many years post-diagnosis, pushing back against the assumption that meaningful writing becomes impossible quickly (Bryden, 2005). She also integrated a contemplative spiritual dimension into her account — worth noting in an ecumenical frame, without universalizing it.

**Dementia Alliance International** itself, as an organization, deserves attribution as a *source*, not just a citation. DAI's position statements and member-written resources (available through dementiaallianceinternational.org) treat dementia as a disability and draw on the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) as a framing instrument. This reframing — from "tragic terminal illness" to "disability with progressive features" — is not merely rhetorical. It changes what supports are considered owed, what accommodations are reasonable, and what role the person retains (DAI, ongoing). For a family navigating court proceedings and supervised visitation, disability-rights framing may open legal and practical doors that "cognitive decline" framing keeps shut. ## 3. Traditional Practices for Slow Loss

No single tradition holds the whole territory of slow loss. What follows honors six traditions in their specificity, notes where they disagree, and does not flatten them.

### Jewish mourning structure

Jewish tradition offers one of the most structurally developed mourning frameworks in world practice. The stages are discrete and named: **aninut** (the acute interval between death and burial, when the mourner is released from ordinary religious obligations), **shiva** (seven days of intense home mourning, during which the community comes *to* the mourner), **shloshim** (thirty days of reduced restrictions), and the first year of *avelut* for a parent, closing with **yahrzeit** — the annual remembrance. **Kri'ah**, the ritual tearing of a garment at the moment of loss, is an embodied acknowledgment: the fabric of the mourner's world is literally, physically torn (see, e.g., Lamm, 2000, *The Jewish Way in Death and Mourning*, revised edition).

The adaptation question for anticipatory grief is genuine and unresolved in the tradition itself. Classical Jewish mourning is for the dead, not the dying. However, contemporary Jewish chaplaincy has developed language around *pre-grief* and adaptive rituals. The structure itself — named stages, a community that *comes to* rather than *waits for*, embodied acknowledgment — is what may be portable. (A family considering adaptation should, where possible, do so in conversation with a rabbi from their own branch; this is not a tradition to borrow from at arm's length.)

### Buddhist impermanence teachings

Buddhist traditions offer **anicca** (Pali) — impermanence as one of the three marks of existence. All conditioned things arise and pass. This is not presented as bad news; it is presented as a fact whose acceptance reduces suffering. Thich Nhat Hanh's writings (e.g., *No Death, No Fear*, 2002) articulate this in a form accessible to non-Buddhists: the person is not lost because the person was never a fixed thing to lose (Thich Nhat Hanh, 2002).

The **Tibetan Bardo Thodol** (the so-called "Tibetan Book of the Dead," translated variously; see e.g., the Padmasambhava/Karma Lingpa text, Coleman & Jinpa translation, 2005) is more specific: it describes the dissolution of the elements and of ordinary self-structures during dying as a *process with stages*, each of which calls for particular kinds of accompaniment (Bardo Thodol, 8th c. CE/2005). A family facing progressive cognitive change is not in the bardo of dying — but the framing of *dissolution as a process to be accompanied rather than fought* is offered by the tradition and may be resonant.

It is worth noting that these Buddhist frames and the Jewish mourning structure **disagree** at an important point: Jewish mourning holds the personhood of the one who dies as enduringly significant; Buddhist analysis holds the fixed-self framing itself as illusion. Both can be held, but they should not be pretended to be the same.

### Celtic keening (caoineadh)

The Irish tradition of **caoineadh** — keening — was the vocal, embodied expression of grief at wakes, often performed by women (the *mná caointe*) whose role was to voice what the bereaved could not. The keening woman made grief audible, shared, and collectively witnessed. The practice was largely suppressed by the Church in the 18th and 19th centuries but has been recovered in recent scholarship and in the work of contemporary singers such as Marie Ní Ghráda and Mary McLaughlin. [verification needed: specific contemporary keening-recovery sources]

The lesson of caoineadh is that grief is not only private and verbal. It is bodily, breath-based, and may need to be made *audible* to be made bearable. A family member who cannot "find words" for anticipatory grief has not failed. The words may not be the right medium.

### Mexican Día de los Muertos, duelo, and luto

Día de los Muertos (roots in Nahua and other indigenous Mesoamerican practices, syncretized with Catholic All Saints/All Souls) maintains active, ongoing relationship with those who have died or are in transition through **ofrendas** — altars holding photographs, favorite foods, flowers (especially cempasúchil/marigolds), and objects that belonged to the person. The dead are not "moved on from"; they are hosted, annually and with joy (Brandes, 2006, *Skulls to the Living, Bread to the Dead*).

Alongside Día de los Muertos, Mexican Catholic practice holds **duelo** (grief, the inner state) and **luto** (mourning, the outer expression — traditionally marked by dress, restricted social activity, altar maintenance in the home). Luto is *time granted for grief*, publicly visible, and socially enforced — a structural permission to not be okay.

The applicable insight is that **continued relationship is not pathology**. Western grief psychology's earlier insistence on "letting go" has been substantially revised by continuing-bonds theory (Klass, Silverman & Nickman, 1996, *Continuing Bonds: New Understandings of Grief*), and the Mexican traditions were practicing continuing bonds all along. For a family whose loved one is still physically present but psychologically changing, the frame of "we are not losing him, we are beginning a different kind of relationship with him" is not sentimentality. It is what several traditions have always held.

### Japanese mono no aware

**Mono no aware** — usually translated as "the pathos of things" or "the gentle sadness of transience" — is a Japanese aesthetic sensibility, elaborated particularly by the 18th-century scholar Motoori Norinaga, that finds beauty in the *fading* itself. The falling cherry blossom is more moving than the fully bloomed one precisely because it is passing (Norinaga, 18th c.; see also Parkes, *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* entry on Japanese aesthetics).

Mono no aware is not resignation. It is a trained capacity to witness passing without flinching away. For a family, this may offer permission to find the fading itself beautiful where beauty can honestly be found — a particular gesture, a laugh that is still recognizable, an afternoon of clarity — without pretending the loss is not happening.

### A note on Indigenous traditions and closed practices

Many Indigenous traditions, globally, hold sophisticated frameworks for accompanying slow change and dying. Some of these are open to respectful learning; others are closed to outsiders, either because of colonial harm, because specific ceremonies require lineage initiation, or because the tradition holds that some knowledge is community-internal. The ethical posture is: **do not borrow what has not been offered**. If a family has Indigenous lineage, they may have access to practices that this chapter should not attempt to summarize from outside.

## 4. Before Hospice: How Communities Held Slow Losses

Modern hospice, as an organized infrastructure, dates from Cicely Saunders' founding of St Christopher's Hospice in London in 1967 (Clark, 2007, *BMJ*). Before 1967, slow dying happened almost entirely inside homes, inside families, and inside the community structures surrounding them. This is worth studying not out of nostalgia but because those structures handled a form of loss that modern infrastructure now struggles with.

In European peasant and small-town communities, the **parish** was often the primary unit of support during slow illness. The parish priest or pastor visited, sometimes daily, and brought the community's attention *with* him. Neighbor women took turns at bedside, cooked, watched children, handled laundry. Mutual-aid societies — burial societies, friendly societies, guild funds — pooled resources so that a family losing a wage-earner did not simultaneously lose its housing. The structural point is that slow loss was held by a **distributed, named, rotating system of care** rather than by one spouse alone (Strange, 2005, *Death, Grief and Poverty in Britain, 1870–1914*, Cambridge University Press).

In Jewish shtetl communities, the **chevra kadisha** (burial society) and the broader practice of visiting the sick (*bikur cholim*) were formal community obligations. The visiting was not optional kindness; it was *commanded*, which meant the isolated-caregiver experience was structurally prevented. [verification needed: specific historical sources on shtetl bikur cholim organization]

In Mediterranean Catholic villages, the widow network — women who had already lost spouses — often absorbed new mourners into a visible, structurally supported identity. *Luto* was worn in clothing. Neighbors knew, without being told, that this family was in the long season of loss.

In West African and African-diasporic traditions, extended family and compound structures meant that caregiving was rarely borne by one individual. Multiple generations, multiple adults, and community elders shared the work. [verification needed: specific ethnographic sources]

Pre-modern medicine framed decline variously — as the will of God, as the natural course of life, as a passage toward reunion, as the unwinding of the four humors. The specific cosmologies differed. What was *consistent* across traditions was that the family was **not alone**. The cosmological container may have been religious, but the practical container was communal.

The modern family facing younger-onset progressive cognitive change has inherited the cosmological uncertainty of secular late modernity *and* the practical isolation of nuclear-family households *and* the absence of community infrastructure that used to rotate in and out of sick rooms. This is a historically unusual amount of aloneness. Naming it does not fix it, but it explains why the experience feels, correctly, like something the culture has no container for. The container has to be rebuilt, locally and deliberately, by each family and the people who choose to stand with them.

## 5. Ambiguous Loss: The Exact Shape of This Family's Grief

**Pauline Boss**'s *Ambiguous Loss: Learning to Live with Unresolved Grief* (Harvard University Press, 1999) is the single most relevant theoretical work for this family. Boss distinguishes two forms of ambiguous loss:

**Type 1:** Physical absence with psychological presence. The missing soldier, the disappeared person, the child who ran away. The person is gone but the family holds them present internally.

**Type 2:** Physical presence with psychological absence. This is the dementia-family experience exactly. The person is in the room. The person's body is at the table. And the person — in the sense of the continuous self the family has known — is changing, receding, sometimes there and sometimes not (Boss, 1999; 2006, *Loss, Trauma, and Resilience*).

Boss's central empirical finding, supported across decades of clinical work, is that **ambiguous loss is the most stressful kind of loss people face** (Boss, 1999). It is more stressful than unambiguous death. The reason is structural: grief resolves, in part, through social and ritual recognition. Ambiguous loss has no funeral, no date, no moment at which the community says "now." Without a marker, the grieving person cannot move through the grief — and, critically, cannot *take breaks* from it, because the situation never shifts into a clearly new phase.

The paradoxes Boss names are worth stating directly, because naming them is itself partial relief:

- **Grieving someone who is alive.** This is not pathological. It is accurate perception.
- **Missing someone who is in the room.** This is also accurate. The person in the room is not the same configuration of person the family misses.
- **Loving someone whose personality is shifting.** Love does not require the beloved to stay the same. But the love has to stretch across the shifts, and that stretching is work.
- **The "both/and" of presence.** Boss argues against either/or framings ("he's still here" *or* "he's already gone"). Both are true. Both have to be held (Boss, 2006).

For a family under court-ordered supervised visitation, ambiguous loss has an additional complicating layer: the family cannot freely shape the *how* and *when* of being together. Contact itself is scheduled, structured, observed. The windows in which presence-with is possible are narrow. This means the ambiguous-loss work — which for other families can unfold gradually across shared meals, shared evenings, shared ordinary time — has to happen inside container-shaped time that was not designed for it. The literature does not directly address this combination. This is a gap the protocols will have to bridge.

Boss's clinical recommendation, repeated across her work, is that families facing ambiguous loss benefit most from **learning to hold contradiction** rather than trying to resolve it. The family that can say "he is my father *and* my father is leaving" suffers less than the family that insists on one framing only.

## 6. Implications for This Specific Family

The following concrete implications are drawn from the literature above and are intended as inputs for Phase B protocol design. They are offered in "may help" language; none are prescriptions.

**1. Permission to grieve before the loss is complete.** The mother does not need to wait for a death to be allowed to mourn. Rando's framework, Boss's framework, and Garand et al.'s empirical findings all converge on this: grief before loss is not premature, not disloyal, and not pathological. Naming this explicitly, repeatedly, may reduce the secondary suffering of "am I allowed to feel this yet."

**2. Structured grief windows rather than always-on grief.** Ambiguous loss without a container becomes chronic. A concrete structure — e.g., a weekly held hour, a monthly ritual, a designated object or place that holds the grief — may help prevent grief from either flooding everything or being suppressed into nothing. The Jewish stage structure (aninut/shiva/shloshim) is a model of *time-bounded intensity* that may be adaptable non-religiously.

**3. Ritual container, ecumenical and family-specific.** The research across traditions suggests that *some* form of ritual matters, and that the specific form matters less than the fact of having one. The family may benefit from designing a small ritual they can sustain — something embodied (following the caoineadh and kri'ah precedent), something repeatable (following yahrzeit and Día de los Muertos), something that admits the *both/and* of presence and absence.

**4. Connection-capsules for the children.** The children are 5 and 8. At those ages, explicit verbal processing is limited; sensory and narrative memory is primary. The lived-experience literature (Mitchell especially) models capturing memories in concrete forms — photographs, voice recordings, short stories told by the father to the children, drawings, objects held together. These "capsules" become portable later: objects the children can return to when their father is less reachable. This is an application of continuing-bonds theory (Klass et al., 1996) to a pre-loss context.

**5. Young-caregiver-specific support for the mother.** Most caregiver support groups are populated by adults in their 60s and 70s whose spouses are in their 70s and 80s. A woman under 50 entering such a group is often the youngest person there, and her peer experiences — young children at home, her own working years, her own not-yet-middle-aged body — are not reflected. Advocacy organizations for younger-onset diagnoses (DAI and national affiliates; AFTD for frontotemporal variants; the Alzheimer's Association's younger-onset programming in the US) host peer networks specifically for younger caregivers. Finding one is itself an act of not-being-alone. [verification needed: currently active younger-caregiver-specific peer networks]

**6. Prevention of premature detachment.** Rando's warning is directly applicable. When anticipatory grief becomes unbearable, the impulse is to pull away emotionally from the person still present. Structured presence — scheduled, bounded, prepared-for visits rather than open-ended unpredictable contact — may actually make *more* real presence possible, because it prevents the exhausted retreat that unbounded contact invites. Paradoxically, the supervised-visitation structure, for all its legal weight, offers one scaffolding element this work needs: a container.

**7. The father as agent, not object.** Swaffer's concept of prescribed disengagement is directly applicable. The father is a person with a diagnosis, not a diagnosis with a person attached. Whatever protocols emerge should include *him* as a participant in shaping them where he can be — writing letters to the children's future selves, choosing what he wants remembered, naming what matters to him about the relationships while naming is still accessible. This is not only ethically right; Taylor, Mitchell, and Bryden all suggest it is protective of the person's remaining capacities.

**8. Naming the historical aloneness.** The mother is not failing to cope with something other mothers cope with easily. She is attempting, mostly alone, a form of care that was historically distributed across parishes, villages, extended kin, and mutual-aid societies. The container she lacks is not a personal failing; it is a structural absence in late-modern life. Saying this out loud may itself reduce self-blame. Rebuilding a small container — a few named people who will actually show up on a rotation — is the practical response.
