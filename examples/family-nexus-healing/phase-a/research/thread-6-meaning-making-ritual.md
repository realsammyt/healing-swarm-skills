# Thread 6: Meaning-Making & Ritual Across Traditions

## Orientation and Positionality

This chapter grounds the ecumenical framing of the Family Nexus Healing project. Its claim is simple and load-bearing: families facing progressive cognitive change, forced separation, and anticipatory grief are not without precedent. Every known human culture has developed ritual containers for impossible situations, and the structural similarities across those containers — alongside their genuine differences — constitute a resource. A family does not have to invent meaning from scratch. Humanity has been here, in countless forms, for as long as there has been humanity.

Before proceeding, a positional note. I write as a researcher drawing on published academic and first-person literature. I am not a lineage-holder in any of the traditions discussed. I can describe what has been written openly about these traditions by scholars and by authorized teachers who have chosen to publish. I cannot transmit practices that require teacher-student lineage, and I will not attempt to. Where I am uncertain whether material is open or closed, I err on the side of closure and point toward published philosophical framing rather than specific ceremonial content. Readers with insider knowledge in any tradition discussed here will inevitably see places where my summary is thin; the appropriate response to that thinness is to consult lineage-holders, not to treat this chapter as sufficient.

A second note on language. Throughout this chapter I use "may help" rather than "will help." The ritual literature is strong on descriptive and theoretical grounds but varies in the quality of outcome research. Some of what follows is supported by empirical studies of meaning-making and grief; much of it is supported by millennia of lived practice in communities that found these forms worth keeping. Both forms of evidence matter. Neither is a prescription.

## Ritual Theory Foundation

The academic study of ritual in the twentieth century gave us vocabulary for what families already know intuitively: that there are moments when ordinary action cannot carry the weight of what is happening, and that human beings respond to those moments by doing something set apart.

**Émile Durkheim** (*The Elementary Forms of Religious Life*, 1912) argued that ritual binds communities together and that the "collective effervescence" of shared ceremony generates the felt sense of something larger than the individual. His framing is dated in some respects — it treats religion functionally rather than phenomenologically — but it introduces the central insight that ritual does social work that individual reflection cannot.

**Arnold van Gennep** (*Les Rites de Passage*, 1909) identified a three-stage structure underlying rites of passage across cultures: *separation* from a prior status, a *liminal* phase between statuses, and *incorporation* into a new status. For a family navigating a progressive condition, this three-stage structure maps suggestively onto many ordinary rhythms: arriving at a care facility (separation), sitting together in a room that is neither home nor hospital (liminal), leaving again (incorporation into the rest of the day). Van Gennep's schema is descriptive rather than prescriptive; it notices a pattern rather than commanding one.

**Victor Turner** (*The Ritual Process*, 1969; *Dramas, Fields, and Metaphors*, 1974) developed van Gennep's liminal stage into one of the most influential concepts in ritual studies. For Turner, liminality is the threshold state in which ordinary social categories dissolve and a different kind of relationship — *communitas* — becomes possible. Communitas is not structured role-to-role interaction but an undifferentiated solidarity: people meeting as people, beneath the categories that usually separate them. Turner observed this in initiation rituals across cultures; it may also describe what sometimes happens in hospital rooms, courtrooms, and supervised visitation spaces, where ordinary status markers lose their grip and something more naked shows up between people. Turner's work matters here because it names a quality of attention that families in transition sometimes report: an unexpected closeness that emerges precisely when ordinary roles can no longer be sustained.

**Mary Douglas** (*Purity and Danger*, 1966) showed how ritual creates order out of ambiguity. Her central insight is that cultures develop ritual responses to things that do not fit neatly into their classificatory systems — the in-between, the liminal, the undefined. Progressive cognitive change produces exactly this kind of ambiguity: a beloved person who is present and not-present, the same and not-the-same, requiring categories that ordinary language does not supply. Douglas helps explain why ritual forms may help where ordinary conversation fails: ritual does not require the ambiguity to resolve.

**Catherine Bell** (*Ritual Theory, Ritual Practice*, 1992) shifted the field from asking "what is ritual?" to asking "what does ritualizing do?" Her practice-theoretic approach treats ritual as a way of acting that distinguishes itself from ordinary action — through formality, repetition, performance, set-apartness — and thereby marks what is happening as significant. Bell's framing is useful for contemporary families who may not be religious but who find that lighting a candle, reading aloud, or keeping a weekly rhythm does something that "just talking" cannot.

**Ronald Grimes** (*Deeply Into the Bone: Re-Inventing Rites of Passage*, 2000; *The Craft of Ritual Studies*, 2014) has argued that late-modern life is ritually impoverished — that many contemporary passages (divorce, chronic illness, the slow losses of progressive disease) lack inherited forms and that people must often construct new ones. Grimes is careful to warn against eclectic pastiche and appropriation; his position is that new rites can be crafted, but that craftsmanship requires respect for what is being drawn from.

Taken together, these theorists give us a working frame: ritual names a set-apart form of action that carries meaning through ambiguity, holds community in liminal time, and can be constructed anew when inherited forms are insufficient. This framing underwrites the ecumenical survey that follows.

## Diverse Tradition Survey

What follows is a brief, respectful gesture toward seven traditions. Each receives a paragraph; each paragraph is insufficient. The purpose is not to teach any tradition but to demonstrate that precedent exists and to indicate where readers may go for further depth.

**Indigenous traditions.** This section requires particular care. Many Indigenous ceremonial practices are closed to non-members. They are not available for outsiders to adopt, teach, or transmit, and the harm done by casual appropriation is well-documented (Smith, 1999/2012). What is open is a substantial body of published Indigenous philosophical writing. Robin Wall Kimmerer (*Braiding Sweetgrass*, 2013), writing as a Citizen Potawatomi botanist, offers reflections on reciprocity, grief, and relationship with the more-than-human world that are explicitly shared. Vine Deloria Jr. (*God Is Red*, 1973) wrote philosophically on Indigenous relations to place and time in ways meant for general readership. The Haudenosaunee Great Law of Peace is widely discussed in published scholarship as a foundational political-ethical framework. The Lakota phrase *Mitákuye Oyás'iŋ* — "all my relations" — is openly taught by Lakota authors as a philosophical orientation toward kinship with all beings, and it may help families reframe relationship as a web rather than a dyad. None of this authorizes outsiders to perform ceremonial practices. The distinction between drawing on openly published philosophical writing and enacting closed ceremonies must be maintained throughout.

**Jewish tradition.** Judaism has developed one of the most structured systems for mourning in the world's religions. The stages — *aninut* (the acute period before burial), *shiva* (seven days of intense mourning), *shloshim* (thirty days), and the first year, marked thereafter by *yahrzeit* — give grief a shape and a calendar (Lamm, 1969/2000). The *Mi Sheberach* is a prayer for healing recited in community that names the one for whom prayer is offered. The psalms of lament — notably Psalms 22, 88, and 130 — give voice to complaint without requiring that complaint resolve into acceptance. The *Amidah*, recited three times daily, offers a daily container. *Shabbat*, the weekly set-apart day, offers a weekly container. The concept of *tikkun* (repair) holds that the world can be mended; its counterpart is the honest recognition that some things cannot be repaired and must instead be honored. *Kaddish*, the mourner's prayer, is a communal witness that does not mention death but sanctifies God's name — a refusal, at the site of loss, to abandon meaning.

**Christian tradition.** Christianity's liturgical year punctuates time with seasons of waiting (Advent), repentance and stripping-down (Lent), concentrated suffering and silence (the Triduum — Maundy Thursday, Good Friday, Holy Saturday), and ordinary rhythm (Ordinary Time). The Stations of the Cross walk the body through fourteen moments of Jesus' passion and are explicitly framed as accompaniment with suffering rather than escape from it. Pietà imagery — Mary holding the body of her son — offers a visual vocabulary of witnessed sorrow. *Lectio divina*, the slow, contemplative reading of sacred text, is taught openly across several Christian traditions. The Ignatian Examen, a structured fifteen-minute daily review developed by Ignatius of Loyola, asks where one noticed gratitude and where one noticed desolation that day (Fleming, 2008). *Compline*, the last prayer of the monastic day, offers an evening container of surrender. Much of this material is published and available; none of it requires conversion.

**Hindu tradition.** Hindu traditions span enormous diversity and resist single summary. The *samskaras* are sixteen life-cycle rituals that mark passages from conception through death, demonstrating a culture in which ritual and lifespan are co-extensive. The *Bhagavad Gita*, particularly the teaching at 2.47 on action without attachment to fruits, offers a philosophical orientation that may help caregivers who are doing work whose outcomes they cannot control. *Antim sanskar*, the last rites, orient the dying and the family toward the next phase rather than treating death as terminus. *Dharma* — situated duty, context-specific rightness — rejects universal rule-application in favor of the hard work of discerning what is right in this situation, with these relationships, under these constraints. Much Hindu philosophical material is available in published translation; specific practices, especially initiatory ones, often require authorized transmission.

**Buddhist tradition.** Buddhist traditions offer a highly developed vocabulary for impermanence (*anicca*), suffering (*dukkha*), and the end of suffering. *Metta* — loving-kindness practice — is openly taught across Theravada and Mahayana schools and has been adapted into secular clinical settings (Salzberg, 1995). *Maranasati*, mindfulness of death, is a contemplative practice within Theravada traditions. The Mahayana emphasis on the bodhisattva — one who commits to the welfare of all beings — offers an ethical horizon for caregivers. Pure Land traditions within East Asian Buddhism have developed extensive practices of accompanying the dying. The *Bardo Thödol* (often translated as the *Tibetan Book of the Dead*) addresses the transitioning mind specifically within Tibetan Vajrayana traditions; it is philosophically discussed in widely available translations (Thurman, 1994 [verification needed]) but its practice context is specifically Tibetan Buddhist and some related transmissions are lineage-restricted. The philosophical teachings on impermanence and compassion are open; certain practices are not.

**Islamic tradition.** The five daily prayers (*salah*) structure the day in a way that may help caregivers who are losing grip on time. *Sabr*, often translated as "patience" but meaning something closer to steadfast endurance, is a central virtue in the face of difficulty and is extensively discussed in the Qur'an and hadith. *Du'a*, personal supplication, is offered in difficulty. Sufi traditions have developed practices of *dhikr* (remembrance of God through repetition) and *tawakkul* (trust, reliance). *Qadar* — the doctrine of divine decree — creates space for acceptance of what cannot be changed without requiring resignation in matters that can be changed. Some Sufi practices are lineage-transmitted and should be referenced philosophically rather than appropriated; openly published Sufi poetry (Rumi, Hafiz, Attar, in scholarly translation) is widely available.

**Secular and humanist approaches.** Not everyone who needs ritual has a religious home, and secular traditions have developed their own resources. Viktor Frankl's *logotherapy* (*Man's Search for Meaning*, 1946/2006) grounds meaning-making without theism: the claim is that humans can find meaning even in suffering through the stance they take toward it. Sasha Sagan (*For Small Creatures Such as We*, 2019) writes explicitly about secular ritual design within family life. Tiffany Shlain (*24/6*, 2019) has proposed a "Technology Shabbat" — a weekly day off from screens — drawing on Jewish precedent while adapting it for secular households. Contemporary ritual design movements, including the Ritual Design Lab at Stanford [verification needed], explore how rituals can be built intentionally for situations inherited forms do not address. Humanist chaplaincy, now credentialed in several contexts, offers non-theistic accompaniment in hospitals and hospice.

## Scripts for the Unscriptable: A Structural Universal

Why do all human cultures develop ritual containers for impossible situations? The anthropology of religion offers a converging answer.

Clifford Geertz, in his essay "Religion as a Cultural System" (1966/1973), argued that religion addresses the problem of meaning when three specific kinds of limit are encountered: the limits of analytic capacity (bafflement), the limits of endurance (suffering), and the limits of moral insight (ethical paradox). Progressive cognitive change in a loved one confronts a family with all three. Ritual, Geertz argued, offers not answers but a frame within which the limits themselves become livable.

Talal Asad (*Genealogies of Religion*, 1993) complicated Geertz's account by insisting that ritual is not primarily about symbolic meaning-transmission but about the formation of dispositions — that ritual is something one *does* with the body and community, and that its effect is to shape how one stands in the world. Both framings matter here. Geertz helps us see why families reach for ritual in the face of the unlivable. Asad helps us see why doing something — lighting a candle, walking a path, repeating a phrase — may help when thinking about something does not.

The convergence across traditions is not at the level of content. Jewish *shiva* and Buddhist forty-nine-day intermediate state and secular memorial services share almost nothing at the level of belief. What they share is *structure*: set-apart time, communal presence, a form that holds the griever when grief itself cannot be held.

This has an important implication for the Family Nexus Healing project. A family does not have to adopt any tradition to draw on the universal structural insights that all traditions, in their own idioms, have arrived at: (1) grief needs a container; (2) the container is usually made of time, place, and community; (3) the container does not require the griever to feel any particular way; (4) the container can be entered and left; (5) repetition is a form of care. Ritual literacy across traditions is itself a resource.

## The Ecumenical Framing as Therapeutic

There is a specific therapeutic claim embedded in the ecumenical frame itself: *showing a family that their situation has precedent across time and cultures may reduce the isolation of "no one has been here before."*

Several literatures support this. Rita Charon's work on narrative medicine (*Narrative Medicine*, 2006) argues that finding one's story within a larger story is itself healing — that illness experience integrated into narrative is more bearable than illness experience held as incoherent fragment. Crystal Park's meaning-making model (Park, 2010) describes how distressing events generate a discrepancy between global meaning and situational meaning, and how healing involves either changing the appraisal of the event or changing one's global framework so that the event fits. Robert Neimeyer's meaning reconstruction work in grief (Neimeyer, 2001) positions grief itself as a process of reorganizing a disrupted self-narrative.

Irvin Yalom's classic account of therapeutic factors in group therapy (*The Theory and Practice of Group Psychotherapy*, 1970/2020) identifies eleven factors, one of which is *universality* — the patient's discovery that they are not alone in their suffering. Yalom consistently found universality among the most valued factors by patients themselves. The ecumenical framing of this project is, in effect, an exercise in universality: the family is invited to encounter the broadest possible sample of fellow-sufferers across time and tradition, and to recognize that their situation is human before it is particular.

What the ecumenical framing *is not*: a claim that all traditions say the same thing, that religious content is interchangeable, or that the family should feel obligated to find meaning. It is, more modestly, an offering: you are not the first, you are not alone, and if any of what others have found helpful speaks to you, it is available.

## Points of Divergence to Honor

Honoring the traditions requires honoring their disagreements. Flattening them into a generic "spirituality" erases what makes each actually useful.

Traditions disagree profoundly about the *afterlife and continuation of relationship*. Some hold that the person continues in another form (rebirth traditions within Hinduism and Buddhism), some that the person is resurrected (most Christian traditions, Islam), some that the person joins the ancestors and can be in ongoing relationship with the living (many African and East Asian traditions), some that the person is remembered but does not continue (many secular humanist frames, some interpretations within Judaism). These differences matter; they shape what kind of ongoing relationship the living may hope for.

Traditions disagree about *mourning timelines*. Jewish tradition prescribes eleven months of Kaddish; Buddhist traditions influenced by the forty-nine-day intermediate state work on that calendar; Christian mourning varies widely by denomination and culture; secular practice prescribes nothing. These different clocks are not mistakes in each other's direction; they are different answers to the question of how long grief should be structurally held.

Traditions disagree about *the expression of grief*. Some contain it tightly; some display it loudly; contemporary Western biomedical culture has tended to pathologize both extremes relative to a middle that itself is culturally specific (Rosenblatt, 2008).

Traditions disagree about *what dignity means in decline*. Some traditions emphasize the preservation of capability and autonomy as the core of dignity; others emphasize the acceptance of dependence as an opening to relationship; others reject the framing altogether and locate dignity in divine worth or kinship belonging independent of capacity.

These divergences are not problems to be resolved. They are an invitation. A family may find that one tradition's answer fits their situation and another tradition's answer does not, and this is not failure of ecumenism — it is the ecumenical project working as intended. The family is offered many mirrors; they take the one in which they can recognize themselves.

## Appropriation Warning

The ecumenical principle can shade into appropriation without vigilance. Some distinctions that may help.

*What this project may appropriately draw from:* published philosophical writing by authorized authors; practices that are openly taught without lineage requirement; structural insights that can be named without teaching specific content; prayers and texts that have been made public by their own traditions for general use; the testimony of insiders who have chosen to share with outsiders.

*What this project should not draw from:* closed Indigenous ceremonial practices, whose transmission is governed by community consent and whose appropriation harms the communities from whom they are taken; practices requiring teacher-student transmission (including many forms of Dzogchen, Vajrayana tantra, certain Sufi orders, and certain streams of Kabbalah); practices that are part of specific initiatory paths whose safety and efficacy depend on that initiatory context; cultural property whose use by outsiders has been explicitly objected to by the originating community.

Some practical tests. *Who developed this practice, and what do they say about outsider use?* If the answer is unclear, pause. *Is there a body of writing by authorized teachers published for general readership?* If yes, that writing is a legitimate source. *Can a respectful outsider read about it without offense?* If you cannot answer that question, assume the answer is no. *Is the practice offered as a practice or as philosophical framing?* The distinction between "drawing on the wisdom of a tradition" and "teaching a practice of a tradition" tracks closely with the distinction between appropriate and inappropriate use. The project should do the former; it should not do the latter without lineage authority, which it does not have.

When in doubt, close the question and move toward structural insight: the universal that "grief needs a container" can be stated without appropriating any particular container's contents.

## Implications for This Family

How should the protocols kit and app draw from many traditions without any one dominating?

A few principles follow from what has been said.

*Present traditions as offerings, not prescriptions.* Language like "In the Jewish tradition, mourners recite Kaddish as a communal witness; this may resonate for you or it may not" holds the offering without imposing it.

*Use explicit attribution.* Avoid generic "spirituality" language that flattens. Say which tradition each practice comes from and, where possible, point to a published source that can be consulted for depth.

*Rotate which traditions are foregrounded.* No single tradition should become the implicit default. Different protocols within the kit may draw from different traditions so that across the whole kit, the family encounters genuine diversity rather than token diversity.

*Honor the family's own context first.* If the family has a religious or cultural home, material from that tradition should be foregrounded with their consent. If they do not, or if their home is mixed, a genuinely diverse sample — including secular humanist resources — should be offered without any default.

*Let the family set aside what does not fit.* Nothing here is obligatory. A practice that does not resonate should be set down without apology. The ecumenical frame exists to widen options, not to require engagement with every option.

*Honor the ecumenical frame as itself a source of comfort.* The family may find that simply knowing their situation has precedent across all of human history is a form of company. This is not a substitute for specific practice; it is a background condition that specific practice, when found, can rest upon.

*Be honest about what is not being offered.* The project cannot and should not offer closed ceremonial material. Naming this limit is itself respectful — to the traditions whose practices are closed and to the family, who deserves to know what they are and are not being given.

## Closing Note

The theorists and traditions gathered here share almost nothing at the level of belief. What they share is the recognition that human beings, faced with situations too large for ordinary action, may do something set apart. A family doing something set apart — lighting a candle before a visit, reading a psalm in the parking lot afterward, keeping a weekly rhythm, sitting in silence when words will not come — joins a company that is older, larger, and more various than any single tradition can contain. That company does not solve anything. It witnesses.

*I am what I am. You are what you are. The walk is the point.*

## Annotated Bibliography

**Asad, T. (1993). *Genealogies of Religion: Discipline and Reasons of Power in Christianity and Islam*. Johns Hopkins University Press.** A foundational critique of Geertz's symbolic theory of religion; argues that ritual forms dispositions rather than transmitting meanings. Useful for understanding why doing something may help where thinking about something cannot.

**Bell, C. (1992). *Ritual Theory, Ritual Practice*. Oxford University Press.** Shifts ritual studies from asking "what is ritual?" to "what does ritualizing do?" Essential for the practice-theoretic framing used in this chapter.

**Charon, R. (2006). *Narrative Medicine: Honoring the Stories of Illness*. Oxford University Press.** Argues that integrating illness experience into narrative is itself therapeutic. Grounds the claim that finding one's story in a larger story may help.

**Deloria, V., Jr. (1973). *God Is Red: A Native View of Religion*. Grosset & Dunlap (and later editions).** An Indigenous philosophical critique of Western religious frames, published for general readership. Appropriate source for philosophical framing; not a source for ceremonial practice.

**Douglas, M. (1966). *Purity and Danger: An Analysis of Concepts of Pollution and Taboo*. Routledge & Kegan Paul.** Classic account of how ritual creates order out of ambiguity. Relevant to situations — like progressive cognitive change — that do not fit inherited categories.

**Durkheim, É. (1912/1995). *The Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (K. Fields, Trans.). Free Press.** Foundational for understanding ritual's social function. Dated in some respects; still useful for the insight that ritual binds communities.

**Fleming, D. L. (2008). *What Is Ignatian Spirituality?* Loyola Press.** Accessible introduction to Ignatian practices, including the Examen. Published for general readership.

**Frankl, V. (1946/2006). *Man's Search for Meaning*. Beacon Press.** Foundational secular account of meaning-making in suffering. The stance that meaning is found rather than given underwrites much secular ritual design.

**Geertz, C. (1973). Religion as a cultural system. In *The Interpretation of Cultures*. Basic Books.** Essay arguing that religion addresses limits of analytic capacity, endurance, and moral insight. Useful for understanding why ritual arises where ordinary frames fail.

**Grimes, R. L. (2000). *Deeply Into the Bone: Re-Inventing Rites of Passage*. University of California Press.** Argues that contemporary life is ritually impoverished and that new rites can be constructed with care. Includes warnings against pastiche.

**Kimmerer, R. W. (2013). *Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge, and the Teachings of Plants*. Milkweed Editions.** Openly published by a Citizen Potawatomi author for general readership. Appropriate source for Indigenous philosophical orientation toward reciprocity and grief.

**Lamm, M. (1969/2000). *The Jewish Way in Death and Mourning* (Rev. ed.). Jonathan David.** Standard reference on Jewish mourning structure, published for general readership.

**Neimeyer, R. A. (Ed.). (2001). *Meaning Reconstruction and the Experience of Loss*. American Psychological Association.** Edited volume on meaning reconstruction as a central process in grief.

**Park, C. L. (2010). Making sense of the meaning literature: An integrative review of meaning making and its effects on adjustment to stressful life events. *Psychological Bulletin, 136*(2), 257–301.** Widely cited integrative review of meaning-making research; grounds the Park meaning-making model referenced above.

**Rosenblatt, P. C. (2008). Grief across cultures: A review and research agenda. In M. S. Stroebe et al. (Eds.), *Handbook of Bereavement Research and Practice*. American Psychological Association.** Cross-cultural review of grief that resists pathologizing culturally specific patterns.

**Sagan, S. (2019). *For Small Creatures Such as We: Rituals for Finding Meaning in Our Unlikely World*. G. P. Putnam's Sons.** Accessible secular approach to ritual design within family life.

**Salzberg, S. (1995). *Lovingkindness: The Revolutionary Art of Happiness*. Shambhala.** Openly taught metta practice from a teacher authorized to transmit it in a lay-accessible form.

**Shlain, T. (2019). *24/6: The Power of Unplugging One Day a Week*. Gallery Books.** Secular adaptation of weekly set-apart time, drawing on Jewish precedent with explicit attribution.

**Smith, L. T. (1999/2012). *Decolonizing Methodologies: Research and Indigenous Peoples* (2nd ed.). Zed Books.** Essential reading on the ethics of research across cultural boundaries; foundational for the appropriation warnings in this chapter.

**Turner, V. (1969). *The Ritual Process: Structure and Anti-Structure*. Aldine.** Develops the concepts of liminality and communitas that ground much of this chapter's framing.

**Turner, V. (1974). *Dramas, Fields, and Metaphors: Symbolic Action in Human Society*. Cornell University Press.** Extends liminality beyond initiation rites into social process more broadly.

**van Gennep, A. (1909/1960). *The Rites of Passage* (M. Vizedom & G. Caffee, Trans.). University of Chicago Press.** The foundational three-stage schema (separation / liminal / incorporation) that underlies much subsequent ritual theory.

**Yalom, I. D., & Leszcz, M. (2020). *The Theory and Practice of Group Psychotherapy* (6th ed.). Basic Books.** Source for the eleven therapeutic factors, including universality — the discovery that one is not alone in one's suffering.
