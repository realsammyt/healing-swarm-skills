# Thread 3: Presence-Without-Memory — Connection When Cognition is Limited

*A research chapter for the Family Nexus Healing project, Phase B: father-with-kids-supervised protocol set.*

---

## Opening Frame

A father in his forties is losing narrative memory. His body still knows how to walk, how to lift a child, how to sit on the floor. His face still forms the micro-expressions his children learned to read before they learned to read words. His voice still carries the prosody they imprinted on as infants. What is thinning is the thread that ties moments into stories — the "and then, and then" of declarative memory, the executive scaffolding of complex planning, perhaps the reliable retrieval of names.

This chapter asks a single question: *how do humans connect across the gap where cognition is shifting but presence remains?* It gathers evidence from dementia-care research, contemplative traditions, and somatic science to suggest that the connection-thread may never have run primarily through narrative in the first place. What children receive from a father runs through face, voice, breath, touch, rhythm, and ritual — substrates that progressive cognitive change alters last, if at all.

A note on language. We use "progressive cognitive change" as baseline rather than diagnostic labels, following the preference of people living with such change who have organized against reductive terminology (Swaffer, 2015). We use "may help" rather than "will help," because individual variation is enormous and no practice in this chapter constitutes treatment. This is a research synthesis, not a clinical protocol.

## 1. Dementia-Care Research: Personhood as Relational Standing

### Kitwood and the Standing of Personhood

Tom Kitwood's *Dementia Reconsidered* (1997) reframed an entire field. Before Kitwood, the dominant "standard paradigm" approached cognitive change as a technical problem of deficit management: assess the losses, compensate where possible, manage behavior. Kitwood argued this paradigm itself produced much of what it diagnosed. He called the harms of reductive care "malignant social psychology" — the dozens of small acts (infantilization, outpacing, ignoring, objectification) that strip personhood from people whose cognition is changing.

Against this, Kitwood proposed that *personhood is a standing conferred in relationship*. It is not a property the person either has or lacks based on intact cognition. It is maintained by how others hold them. His VIPS framework, developed later by Dawn Brooker (2007), formalized this:

- **V**aluing people with cognitive change and those who care for them
- **I**ndividualized care rooted in specific biography and preference
- **P**erspective of the person — attempting to see the world from inside their experience
- **S**ocial environment that supports well-being

For a father with two young children, VIPS translates almost directly: his personhood as father is not erased by diminishing narrative memory, *provided his children and their mother continue to confer that standing through how they meet him*. The father is still the father. This is not sentiment; it is what Kitwood's evidence suggests actually sustains well-being across the course of cognitive change.

### Feil's Validation Therapy: Meeting Emotional Truth

Naomi Feil's validation method (Feil, 1993; Feil & de Klerk-Rubin, 2012) developed from decades of practice with older adults whose orientation was shifting. Its central move: rather than correcting a person who says something factually wrong ("No, Dad, Mom died ten years ago"), the caregiver attends to the *emotional* truth underneath ("You miss her"). Validation does not mean agreeing with false beliefs; it means recognizing the feeling as real and responding to that.

For a father whose memory of a specific morning may be unstable, but whose feeling of love or frustration or tenderness is intact, validation offers a way for his children to stay with him emotionally without either lying or forcing reality-correction that wounds. A five-year-old can usually do this intuitively. An eight-year-old may need coaching: "Daddy feels happy right now. You don't have to fix the mix-up. You can just be happy with him."

### Snoezelen and Multisensory Environments

Ad Verheul and Jan Hulsegge developed Snoezelen in the Netherlands in the 1970s, originally for people with profound intellectual disability (Hulsegge & Verheul, 1987). The approach was later adapted for dementia care. A Snoezelen environment offers curated, low-demand sensory inputs — soft light, textured objects, gentle sound, scent, tactile materials — with no requirement to *do* anything. The person can simply receive. Reviews of Snoezelen in cognitive change contexts are mixed on clinical endpoints but consistent on one finding: such environments tend to reduce agitation during the session and support connection that does not depend on conversation (Sánchez et al., 2013 [verification needed for specific citation]).

The principle — *low-demand, high-sensory, shared presence* — is exportable. A living room dimmed, a warm blanket, a bowl of smooth stones to touch, a slow piece of music — this is a home Snoezelen, and it requires nothing the father must "remember how to do."

### Sacks on Music and the Persistence of Procedural Memory

Oliver Sacks devoted much of *Musicophilia* (2007) and passages of *Awakenings* (1973) to the observation that musical memory is neurologically distinct from narrative memory and often persists remarkably long. The case of Clive Wearing — a musician with profound amnesia whose conducting and piano-playing remained intact — became iconic precisely because it demonstrated how deeply music is held in procedural and emotional systems that declarative memory loss does not reach. A man who could not recognize his own family from moment to moment could still complete a Bach cantata.

The implication for a young father: *songs he sang to his children as babies may still be available to him when their names sometimes are not*. Lullabies, silly songs, bedtime rituals with melodic shape — these are bridge objects across the gap. The children do not need to know the neuroscience. They need only to sing with him.

### Dementia Care Mapping and the Lancet Commission

Dementia Care Mapping, developed by the Bradford Dementia Group building on Kitwood's work, is an observational method that tracks well-being and ill-being indicators in real time, giving feedback on what actually supports the person rather than what staff *believe* supports them (Brooker, 2005). It operationalizes the simple principle: *watch what helps and do more of that*.

More recently, the Lancet Commission on dementia prevention, intervention, and care (Livingston et al., 2017; 2020 update) synthesized evidence across nonpharmacological interventions. The commission found strongest support for person-centered approaches, music-based interventions, and caregiver support; it found the "one-size" behavioral management paradigm inadequate. The commission's framing is useful here because it places nonpharmacological presence-based approaches not as soft adjuncts but as first-line evidence-based practice.

Most of this research was conducted with older-onset populations. The adaptation for a physically vital younger-onset father requires care: the *principles* transfer (personhood as relational, sensory anchors, music), but the *application* must honor his continued capacity for active participation, choice, and role.

## 2. Contemplative Traditions That Privilege Presence Over Narrative

The world's contemplative lineages have independently discovered that some of the deepest human experiences are not made of story. A chapter on presence-without-memory would be impoverished without them. We sketch six traditions, each in its own terms, without flattening them into a generic "mindfulness."

### Zen: Shikantaza, Just Sitting

In the Soto Zen tradition associated with Dogen, the core practice is *shikantaza* — "just sitting." Unlike koan practice, shikantaza has no object of concentration, no question to resolve, no narrative progression. The practitioner sits with what is already the case. Dogen's formulation in *Fukanzazengi* insists this is not a technique for *becoming* enlightened; it is the direct expression of already-present awakening. Being, not becoming.

For this chapter's purposes, shikantaza offers a vocabulary: presence that does not require the person to go anywhere, remember anything, or achieve a state. *Just being here, together.*

### Dzogchen: Rigpa and Natural Great Perfection

In the Nyingma tradition of Tibetan Buddhism, Dzogchen (the "Great Perfection") points to *rigpa* — pristine, naked awareness that is held to be the person's already-present nature, obscured only by grasping and rejecting. Longchenpa and later teachers emphasize "resting in what is already the case."

*An important caveat: Dzogchen has strong teacher-transmission requirements. It cannot be learned from a research chapter, and the point here is not to teach it but to recognize that sophisticated traditions exist around a practice whose content is "presence without effort."* The existence of such traditions is evidence that experienced practitioners across cultures have found something real in presence-prior-to-narrative.

### Quaker Silent Meeting: Expectant Waiting

The Religious Society of Friends, beginning with George Fox in the seventeenth century, developed a form of worship built around gathered silence. In an unprogrammed Quaker meeting, participants sit together without liturgy, waiting in what Friends call "expectant silence." Speech may arise, but it is not required; the silence itself is the worship. Contemporary Quaker writings (e.g., Pink Dandelion, 2008) describe this as "waiting on the Light" — a presence that is offered, not achieved.

For a family considering how to be together when speech may not flow: the Quaker meeting is evidence that *silence together is a form of meeting*, not a failure of meeting.

### Sufi Silent Dhikr

*Dhikr* — remembrance of the divine — is central to Sufi practice. While many forms of dhikr are vocal, several Sufi lineages (notably within the Naqshbandi order) cultivate *dhikr khafi*, the silent or hidden remembrance, held in the heart rather than spoken. The practice is presence as devotion: simply remembering, not as cognitive recall, but as continuous turning of the heart toward what is real. The Arabic word for heart here, *qalb*, is not merely the organ of feeling but an organ of knowing that does not depend on discursive thought.

### Christian Apophatic and Contemplative Traditions

The Christian contemplative tradition carries a long strand of *apophatic* theology — the way of negation, of meeting the divine through unknowing. *The Cloud of Unknowing* (anonymous, 14th century) instructs the practitioner to place all thoughts, even good thoughts, under a "cloud of forgetting" and to lift only a simple intention of love toward the divine.

Thomas Keating and others reformulated this tradition in the twentieth century as *Centering Prayer* (Keating, 1986/2006): a twenty-minute practice of silent intention, returning gently to a chosen sacred word whenever thought arises. Centering Prayer is explicitly presence-based and requires no narrative content.

For a Christian family, these resources offer language that may be more accessible than Buddhist framings: *presence through unknowing, love held beyond words*.

### Hindu Nirvikalpa Samadhi

In several Hindu contemplative traditions, *nirvikalpa samadhi* names a state of absorption "without construct" — without the conceptual scaffolding that ordinarily structures experience. Patanjali's *Yoga Sutras* (c. 2nd century BCE) treat this as the deepest meditative attainment. The technical specificity varies across schools, and as with Dzogchen, this is not a practice to be learned from a summary. It is cited here to show that the question "what is awareness when narrative falls away?" has been asked and explored for millennia.

### Brief Acknowledgments

*Taoist wu wei* (non-forcing, effortless action) and various Indigenous traditions of silent presence with land and ancestors also belong in this conversation. Regarding Indigenous practices specifically: many are held by their communities and should not be extracted for general use. The appropriate posture for a non-Indigenous family is acknowledgment that such wisdom exists and is not ours to instruct.

### What the Six Traditions Share

Across very different metaphysical frameworks, these traditions converge on a claim: *the most valuable form of being-together may not require narrative cognition from either party*. This is not a consolation prize for families facing cognitive change. It is, in the testimony of these traditions, among the most precious forms of contact available to humans at all.

## 3. Somatic Co-Regulation: Nervous Systems Meeting Nervous Systems

### Polyvagal Theory

Stephen Porges's polyvagal theory (Porges, 2011) proposes that the autonomic nervous system operates in a hierarchy: the ventral vagal complex supporting social engagement and calm connection, the sympathetic system supporting fight-or-flight, and the dorsal vagal complex supporting shutdown when other options are unavailable. The ventral vagal system is intimately linked, in Porges's account, to the muscles of the face, the middle ear, the larynx — the physiology of face-to-face connection. When two people are in ventral vagal states together, their faces soften, their voices acquire prosody, their breathing slows.

Critically: *this system does not require intact declarative memory to function*. It is older, more basic, and runs on signals that a young child reads automatically — eye contact, vocal prosody, facial warmth.

Some aspects of polyvagal theory remain contested within autonomic neuroscience (Grossman & Taylor, 2007 [verification needed]). But the clinical pattern the theory describes — two people settling each other's nervous systems through face, voice, and breath — is widely observed across traumatology, infant research, and contemplative practice.

### Deb Dana's Clinical Translations

Deb Dana (2018, 2020) translated polyvagal theory into clinical tools, including the "polyvagal ladder" (a visual map of nervous-system states) and practical co-regulation techniques: humming, long exhales, soft eye contact, slow movement, warm-toned voice. Her work is notable for making polyvagal ideas teachable to non-specialists, including family members.

For a mother supervising a visit: she has tools. Humming with the kids as Dad enters the room is not just a nice gesture; it is, plausibly, a ventral-vagal invitation for everyone's nervous system, including his.

### Mother-Infant Attunement as Template

Decades of research on mother-infant interaction — Tronick's still-face paradigm (Tronick et al., 1978), Beebe's microanalysis of mother-infant dyads (Beebe & Lachmann, 2002) — established that *rich, mutually regulating connection occurs before language* and persists as the substrate of connection even after language arrives. Infants and mothers coordinate through gaze, vocalization, touch, and rhythm in cycles measured in milliseconds. Nothing in this exchange depends on narrative memory.

The implication for this chapter is direct. A father whose narrative memory is shifting can still participate in the connection-system his children used with him when they were infants. The register may feel younger than the children's chronological age. That is not regression; it is using a channel that remains open.

### The Load-Bearing Sentence

*A father who cannot narrate can still co-regulate. His nervous system can still settle his children's nervous systems.* This is the central somatic finding of this chapter, and it may be the most important single claim for Phase B protocol design.

## 4. Younger-Onset Dignity: Vitality Preserved

### The Specific Tension

Most dementia-care research was developed with older-onset populations. A forty-something father is a different situation. He is physically strong. He may still drive, lift, walk for miles, hold his children on his shoulders. He carries an identity forged through work, partnership, and fatherhood that is recent and still unfinished. His grief and his children's grief run differently from late-life grief because this life was not supposed to be ending yet.

### Kate Swaffer and Prescribed Disengagement

Kate Swaffer, diagnosed in her forties and author of *What the Hell Happened to My Brain?* (2016), named a phenomenon she called *prescribed disengagement*. After diagnosis, people are often told — explicitly or implicitly — to retire, rest, stop driving, get affairs in order, prepare for decline. Swaffer argues this social prescription is itself a harm: it strips roles, purpose, and agency from people who still have substantial capacity, often accelerating the very decline it anticipates.

The counter-stance, which Swaffer and the Dementia Alliance International have advocated, is *active engagement*: continued work where possible, adapted roles, participation in decisions, self-advocacy, rehabilitation-oriented thinking rather than palliative-oriented thinking. This stance is contested in some clinical quarters but has reshaped younger-onset care.

### Implications for Father-with-Kids

Dignity preservation for this father is not primarily about protecting him from exertion. It is about *preserving his role as father* and *keeping him an active agent in his children's lives* for as long as that is possible, which may be substantially longer than the standard narrative suggests.

Concretely: he chooses what to do during visits, within whatever scaffolding helps him succeed. He is not managed; he is *supported*. The mother's framing to the children can name what is happening plainly — "Daddy's memory works differently now, and he's still Daddy" — rather than either hiding the change or dramatizing it.

A small but load-bearing point: the father should be protected from *performing* his own decline in front of his children. If a task is likely to go badly (complex directions, unfamiliar puzzles, anything requiring executive sequencing he has lost), it is a kindness — not a patronization — to not put him in that spot. Meanwhile, the things he is still good at (throwing a ball, carrying a child, singing, being present) become the center of visits, because they are where he shines.

## 5. Sensory Anchoring Practices

The following are gathered from multiple traditions and research literatures. They are tools, not prescriptions. Any of them *may help* individual cases; none will help all.

### Sound

Humming produces long exhales and mechanical vibration in the throat and chest, and has been associated in small studies with vagal tone and relaxation (Vickhoff et al., 2013 [verification needed for specific finding]). Singing familiar songs together draws on procedural music memory (Sacks, 2007). Gregorian chant, Tibetan singing bowls, drone instruments (tanpura, shruti box), and the Psalms chanted slowly all produce sustained, simple auditory environments that support presence without demanding cognition. Lullabies a mother once sang to her babies may function for a father the same way they once functioned for the children — as a direct nervous-system anchor.

### Water

Water has a long history in ritual and healing across traditions — baptism, *wudu*, *mikvah*, Kneipp water cures. The sound of flowing water (fountain, stream, faucet) is a nonverbal auditory anchor. Hand-washing rituals create a slow shared action requiring no narrative. Simple bowls of warm water with a soft cloth for hands and faces can become a grounding ritual before a visit.

### Touch

Safe, non-intrusive touch — held hands, a hand on a back, hand-over-hand in a shared activity — conveys presence directly. For young children with their father, touch is often the primary channel anyway. Animal companionship (a dog lying nearby, a cat to stroke) provides a shared object of attention and the well-documented calming effects of human-animal interaction (Beetz et al., 2012).

### Scent

Scent memory is routed through the olfactory bulb with unusual directness to limbic structures and may persist through cognitive change. Familiar household scents (a particular soap, a particular tea, a specific brand of shampoo the father has used for years) may anchor recognition. Aromatherapy literature in dementia care is suggestive but not definitive; reviews note small effects for some compounds and caution about quality and individual response (Forrester et al., 2014 [verification needed]). Some scent practices are liturgical and bounded (Catholic incense at Mass, Jewish *havdalah* spices, specific Buddhist incense in temple contexts); others are freely available.

### Movement

Slow qigong, kinhin (Zen walking meditation), simple stretching, and gentle repetitive gestures work with the still-vital body rather than against it. A father who has always been physical may find that movement, not stillness, is his doorway. Walking slowly with a child on each side, or doing slow arm-circles together, is practice.

### Breath

Breath practices exist in every tradition and range from profoundly complex (yogic pranayama) to elementally simple (noticing one breath in, one breath out). For the family context, simple is better: humming on the exhale, counting "one" on each exhale together, a long sigh. Heart-coherence breathing (approximately five-second in, five-second out) has a small research literature in stress reduction (McCraty & Shaffer, 2015 [verification needed for specific application]).

## 6. Implications for This Specific Family

### What Works When Narrative Fails

The activities that travel best across cognitive change in a parent-child dyad tend to share four features: *sensory-rich, repetitive, low-demand in terms of executive function, and built around shared attention rather than shared understanding*.

Examples that intersect "doable for the father" with "engaging for young children":
- Singing songs the father knew well (lullabies, hymns, children's songs)
- Slow walking outside, especially with something to look at — water, ducks, leaves
- Simple tactile activities: kneading dough, stirring, stacking smooth stones, threading beads
- Looking at old photos — *not as memory quiz* but as shared gazing, with the mother narrating where needed
- Being outside in warm weather, barefoot on grass
- A shared snack with no task attached
- A story read aloud (he does not have to read it; the children can read to him, and eight is old enough to love this)
- Throwing and catching a ball if his motor coordination remains intact
- Brushing a dog together
- Holding and being held

The activities to avoid are the ones that will spotlight what he has lost: board games with rule-tracking, complex craft projects, anything requiring him to recall specific biographical details on demand, and any task where the children might end up frustrated by his response time.

### How the Father Shows Presence

Through face. Through voice. Through the weight of him sitting close. Through what he sings. Through what he laughs at. Through his hand resting on his child's head. None of these require intact narrative memory. All of them are what the children will remember.

### A Pre-Visit Centering Practice (Phase B Anticipation)

Fifteen minutes before a visit, in a quiet room, the father *may find it useful* to sit or lie with something simple — a warm drink, a piece of familiar music, his own breath. The purpose is not to practice for the visit. The purpose is to arrive *settled* rather than to arrive *braced*. A settled nervous system is a gift he gives his children before he walks through the door. This practice will be detailed in the Phase B protocol document; its grounding is the whole of this chapter.

### Dignity Preservation When Children Are Watching

Children, especially young children, take their cues from the adult framing. The mother's steady, matter-of-fact naming of what is happening — neither hiding it nor catastrophizing it — is probably the single most protective factor for the children's experience. Humor helps when it is kind. Phrases like "Daddy's brain is working on something else right now" or "He gets the mix-ups sometimes and that's okay" give young children language without either lying or overwhelming them.

The father should never be in the position of performing his own decline. If something is too hard, the adult supervising shifts the activity without fanfare. This is not hiding anything from the children. It is protecting everyone, father included, from needless injury.

### Signals to Slow or Stop

The visit is becoming too much — for the father, and therefore for everyone — when the following appear: increasing agitation, pacing, pulling away, repetitive distressed questions, visible withdrawal, flat affect where there was warmth five minutes ago, confusion about who the children are or where he is, tearfulness he cannot settle, or a quality of strain in his face that the mother knows. The appropriate response is not to push through. It is to shift register — lower the demand, add a sensory anchor, offer water, offer quiet, shorten the visit, or end it gently. The children can be told: "Daddy needs a rest now. That's a good kind of stopping." This is not a failed visit. A visit that ended well five minutes early is a successful visit.

## Closing

Across dementia-care research, six contemplative traditions, somatic science, and younger-onset advocacy, a single pattern emerges: the deepest forms of human connection have never required intact narrative memory from both parties. A father whose cognition is changing still has — if he is supported in having it — face, voice, breath, touch, song, ritual, and nervous-system coherence. His young children still have a father, not the same father as before, but a father who can meet them through channels they have been using with him since they were born.

The task of Phase B is not to manufacture connection in the absence of memory. It is to clear the space for the connection that is already possible.
