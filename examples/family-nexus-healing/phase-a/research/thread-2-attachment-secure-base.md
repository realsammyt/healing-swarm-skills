# Thread 2: Attachment & Secure Base Under Uncertainty

*A research chapter for the Family Nexus Healing project, addressing the attachment needs of a 5-year-old boy and 8-year-old girl whose father is undergoing progressive cognitive change and whom they see in court-ordered supervised visits.*

---

## Opening Note on Scope and Limits

This chapter addresses a situation for which the peer-reviewed literature is thin: children maintaining attachment to a living parent whose cognition is progressively changing, in a context of separated households and structured supervised contact. Where direct research exists, it is cited. Where it does not, the chapter draws careful inferences from adjacent literatures (attachment under parental illness, children of divorce, pediatric grief, legacy work in palliative contexts) and names the inference as inference. No claim in this chapter should be read as a treatment recommendation. The language throughout is "may help" — because the practices described are supported by evidence of plausible benefit for related populations, not by trials specific to this family's circumstances.

Two children are at the center of this work. Their privacy matters more than any framework. The protocols downstream of this chapter should be lived loosely, watched carefully, and set aside the moment they stop serving the actual children in front of the actual adults.

## 1. Attachment Theory Foundation

John Bowlby's attachment theory (Bowlby, 1969/1982) reframed the caregiver-child bond as a biologically rooted behavioral system whose function is proximity maintenance in the service of felt security. A child under threat — illness, separation, fear, confusion — activates attachment behavior (seeking, clinging, calling, crying) aimed at a specific caregiver. When that caregiver is responsive and predictable, the child develops an "internal working model" of self-as-lovable and other-as-available. When the caregiver is inconsistent, frightening, or absent, the working model distorts accordingly.

Mary Ainsworth's Strange Situation procedure (Ainsworth, Blehar, Waters, & Wall, 1978) operationalized Bowlby's theory and yielded the classic typology: **secure** (distressed by separation, comforted by reunion), **anxious-resistant** (distressed, inconsolable, clingy and angry at reunion), and **avoidant** (minimizing distress, turning away at reunion). Main and Solomon (1986) later described a fourth category — **disorganized** — in which the child shows contradictory, frozen, or fearful behaviors, typically when the attachment figure is simultaneously the source of comfort and the source of fear or confusion.

Disorganized attachment is the category most directly relevant here. Solomon and George (1999) found that disorganization is more likely when a caregiver is frightened, frightening, or unpredictable — not necessarily abusive, but simply unable to offer coherent responses to the child's bids. A father whose cognition is shifting may, through no fault of his own, become episodically unrecognizable to the child: warm one moment, blank the next, occasionally mistaken in ways the child cannot reconcile. This is a textbook condition for disorganization risk — not a foregone conclusion, but a risk to be buffered.

Mary Main's Adult Attachment Interview research (Main, Kaplan, & Cassidy, 1985; Hesse, 2008) further demonstrated that attachment patterns propagate intergenerationally through the *coherence* of the caregiver's own narrative about their attachment history. The implication for this family is significant: the mother's capacity to hold a coherent, unflinching, non-avoidant narrative about what is happening to the father — one she can return to over years — is itself a protective factor for the children. The narrative does not need to be resolved. It needs to be coherent.

Two adjacent literatures inform the picture further. Research on children of high-conflict separation and divorce (Amato, 2000, 2010; Kelly & Lamb, 2000; Lamb, 2012) finds that children's outcomes are shaped less by the separation itself than by the conflict between caregivers, the stability of the child's primary attachment, and the predictability of contact with the non-residential parent. Structured, reliable, emotionally safe contact — even when limited — is protective. Unreliable or conflict-laden contact is not.

Research on children facing parental illness (Rauch & Muriel, 2006; Christ, 2000; Siegel et al., 1996) consistently finds that what damages children is not the illness itself but the *silence*, *evasion*, and *disorganization* around it. Children who are told age-appropriate truths, given predictable routines, and allowed to express mixed feelings do better across nearly every measured domain than children who are protected from knowing.

The literature on children whose living parent has progressive cognitive decline, specifically, is very thin. Most studies in the Alzheimer's and related dementias field concern adult children caring for elderly parents. The literature on younger-onset (early-onset) forms — which can affect adults under 50 and thus produce young children in the affected family — is emerging but small (Luscombe, Brodaty, & Freeth, 1998; Svanberg, Stott, & Spector, 2011; Johannessen, Engedal, & Thorsen, 2015). These studies consistently report that children in such families experience role confusion, anticipatory grief, social isolation, and a distinctive "ambiguous loss" (Boss, 1999) — a parent who is simultaneously present and absent. Boss's concept of ambiguous loss is, for this chapter, the most important theoretical anchor available. ## 2. Developmental Differentiation

The 5-year-old and the 8-year-old inhabit different cognitive and emotional worlds. A single protocol for "the children" will fit neither. What follows treats them as two distinct developmental cases.

### The 5-Year-Old Boy

At five, the child is squarely in what Piaget (1952) called the **preoperational stage**. His thinking is characterized by magical causality, egocentrism (not selfishness but a limited capacity to take another's perspective), animism, and a blurred boundary between wish and event. If Daddy is different, the five-year-old's native first hypothesis will be some version of *I did this* or *I can undo this*. This is not a pathology; it is how five-year-olds think.

His primary expression modes are felt-sense, body, play, and drawing. He does not yet have the narrative scaffolding to tell the story of what he feels; he will enact it. Under attachment stress, five-year-olds commonly show **regression** (earlier sleep patterns, toileting lapses, baby-voice), **clinging**, **sleep disruption and nightmares**, **somatic complaints** (tummy aches, headaches), and **play themes** involving loss, searching, repair, and rescue (Christ, 2000; Worden, 1996; Cait, 2005). These are normal responses to abnormal information. They are not symptoms to be suppressed; they are messages to be received.

What a five-year-old needs from a visit with a father whose cognition is shifting:

- **Concreteness over explanation.** "Daddy's brain works in a different way now" is enough. More is not better.
- **A predictable shape to the visit** — same place, same order of activities, same closing ritual. His nervous system regulates through repetition.
- **Body-based connection** that does not depend on the father's verbal coherence: a favorite song played together, a shared snack, a hand on his back, a picture book read aloud (even if the father reads the same page twice — the body of the reading matters more than the accuracy).
- **Permission to be a child** — to leave the room, to play on the floor, to not perform love.
- **An adult's regulated presence** — the supervisor or mother's calm body is the actual secure base. The father is the person being visited; he is not, at this stage, the one holding the regulation.

What protects him: reliable routine around the visit, a short honest answer to any question he asks, and the understanding that his drawings and play after the visit are how he is thinking — and should be witnessed, not interpreted back at him.

### The 8-Year-Old Girl

At eight, the child is moving into **concrete operational thinking** (Piaget, 1952). She can hold cause and effect more reliably, take another's perspective, tell a story about her feelings in words, and begin to conceptualize time, permanence, and — increasingly — mortality (Speece & Brent, 1984; Willis, 2002). She is not yet an adolescent. Abstract hypotheticals are still hard. But she can keep a simple journal, use metaphor, and answer a question like "what was the hardest part of today?"

Under attachment stress, 8-year-olds commonly show **somatic complaints**, **shifts in school performance and focus**, **hypervigilance** (scanning adult faces for information), and — importantly for this family — **parentified behavior**: watching out for the younger brother, worrying about the mother, preemptively suppressing her own needs to avoid burdening the adults (Chase, 1999; Earley & Cushway, 2002). Parentification in the short term can look like competence. In the long term it is a risk factor for later anxiety and for the adult-child's own difficulty receiving care. It deserves specific watchfulness.

Eight-year-old girls in this situation often carry a private, unspoken worry about the mother. *If Mommy breaks, we have nothing.* Her attachment vigilance may be pointed as much at the mother as at the father.

What an 8-year-old needs from a visit with a father whose cognition is shifting:

- **Honest, simple framing in advance.** "Daddy may not remember what we did last time. That's not because he doesn't love you." Said once. Not repeated anxiously.
- **Permission for mixed feelings** — to love him and be sad and be angry and be bored, sometimes in the same hour.
- **A role she can actually do**, not a role as co-caregiver. Bringing a drawing to show him is doable. Managing his mood is not. The distinction matters.
- **A debrief structure** after the visit — not an interrogation, but a predictable space (drawing, writing, a walk) in which anything she says is received without correction.
- **Explicit relief from watching the younger brother.** An adult must carry him. She can be his sister, not his second parent.

What protects her: the mother's visible okayness (not performed okayness — *actual* regulation, even if imperfect), a regular private channel for her own feelings that is not her brother's channel, and clear messages that her job is to be eight.

## 3. Expressive Writing and Narrative Therapy for Children

James Pennebaker's expressive writing paradigm (Pennebaker, 1997; Pennebaker & Chung, 2011) has generated over 200 studies showing that brief, structured writing about emotionally significant experiences is associated with improvements in physical health markers, mood, and cognitive integration in adults. The proposed mechanism is not catharsis but **narrative construction** — the act of moving an experience from felt-sense into language changes how it is held.

Adaptations for children exist, though the evidence base is smaller (Fivush, Marin, Crawford, Reynolds, & Brewin, 2007; Reynolds, Brewin, & Saxton, 2000). Findings suggest that structured expressive writing may help school-age children integrate difficult experiences, particularly when the writing is prompted, private or semi-private, and not graded or corrected.

Narrative therapy (White & Epston, 1990) offers a complementary frame. Its central move — **externalizing the problem** — reframes the difficulty as something separate from the person, something the person is in relationship with. For this family, the move is explicit: *the Forgetting* is not Daddy. The Forgetting is something that is happening to Daddy, that the family is living alongside. This grammatical shift gives the children a relationship-with rather than a fusion-with. It lets them love the father and dislike the Forgetting in the same sentence. The child-focused narrative therapy literature (Freeman, Epston, & Lobovits, 1997) provides many examples of children working with externalized "problems" through drawing, naming, and storytelling.

**For the 5-year-old:** drawing-as-writing. Simple prompts given by a trusted adult: *Draw where Daddy is in your picture today. Draw where you are. Draw the weather.* No interpretation offered back. The drawings are kept in a folder he controls.

**For the 8-year-old:** a simple prompted journal, offered but not required. Prompts might include: *One thing I want to remember about Daddy today. One thing I wish was different. One thing I want to tell him but can't.* A written journal can be private (she decides what, if anything, is shared), which matters developmentally — she is beginning to need an interior life.

## 4. Ritual as Container for What Cannot Be Explained

When explanation fails, structure holds. The research on family rituals as protective factors during transitions (Fiese et al., 2002; Fiese, 2006) finds that routine, predictable, meaningful rituals are associated with better child adjustment during family stressors, including parental illness and separation. Rituals do not explain; they contain.

Winnicott's (1953, 1971) concepts of the **holding environment** and the **transitional object** remain the most useful clinical vocabulary for what a ritual does for a child. The holding environment is not a place; it is the reliable, non-intrusive presence of an adult whose regulation allows the child's unregulated states to be survivable. A transitional object (a blanket, a stuffed animal, a particular song) is what the child uses to carry the holding environment through separation. Ritual is the holding environment made portable across time.

Three classes of ritual are likely relevant for this family:

- **Bedtime rituals** as the primary daily secure base — same order, same words, non-negotiable even when everything else is chaotic. This is the mother's domain and should not be asked to stretch to accommodate the father's situation. Its job is to be boring and reliable.
- **Special-day rituals** marking connection between visits: a "Tuesday tea," a Saturday drawing that will be shown to Daddy next time, a weekly lighting of a candle for "people we love who are far away in different ways." Ecumenical. Low-stakes. Adjustable.
- **Visit-bracketing rituals** — a specific thing done before every visit (e.g., choosing what to bring) and a specific thing done after (e.g., a walk, a snack, a drawing). The ritual bracket tells the nervous system where the visit begins and ends. Without a bracket, the visit bleeds into everything.

## 5. Connection-Capsules for Carryforward

This section names a class of practices designed to create durable artifacts of the father-child relationship that the children can carry into an adulthood in which he may not be present. The term "connection-capsule" is a working name.

The adult palliative-care literature offers the most developed model: **Dignity Therapy** (Chochinov et al., 2005, 2011), a brief structured intervention in which a person facing life-limiting illness is interviewed about what has mattered to them, what they want remembered, and what they want to say to the people they love. The transcript is edited into a legacy document returned to the family. Trials show improvements in dignity, sense of purpose, and family satisfaction. The model has inspired adaptations including Life Review (Butler, 1963) and various legacy-work protocols.

Dignity Therapy cannot be imported wholesale here, for several reasons. It was designed for adults with clear cognitive capacity, addressing a more predictable end. A father with progressive cognitive change may have fluctuating capacity, may have days of clarity and days not, and — critically — should not be asked to perform his own decline for the sake of his children's future memories. This is the central ethical tension of connection-capsule work: **the artifact must serve the children without conscripting the father into a performance that diminishes him**.

Practices that may help, held loosely:

- **Letters-to-be-opened-later.** If and when the father wants to and is able, he writes (or dictates) letters to each child to be opened at future moments — a birthday, a graduation, a hard day. The letters can be simple. "I love you. I am proud of you. I remember the time we —." He is not asked to compose a legacy; he is asked to say what is true today.
- **Voice recordings of ordinary things.** The father reading a bedtime story. Telling the story of how he met their mother. Singing a song he loves. Everyday voice, not monumental statements. Everyday voice is what the children will most want to hear in twenty years.
- **Photograph and video of ordinary moments**, not staged. A walk. Breakfast. Laughing at something. The supervisor or mother can be the quiet camera-holder.
- **Monogatari — story-telling as legacy.** In Japanese tradition, *monogatari* names an older form of narrative-as-inheritance, in which stories of a life are offered forward as gifts to those who come after (noted here with care for lineage — this is a cultural frame, not a technique to be extracted). The spirit of monogatari is that a story told to a child can cross time in ways a fact cannot.
- **A shared object that lives in both houses.** A small book, passed back and forth at visits, in which the father adds a sentence or a mark and the children add theirs. The book, not the father, carries the accumulation.

The rule of ethical practice is: *if the father cannot, today, do this thing with ease and consent, it is not done today.* The artifact exists to serve the relationship, not the other way around.

## 6. Traditional Practices for Children Facing Parental Loss

Many traditions have thought carefully about children and loss. What follows is offered ecumenically — no tradition is privileged, and none is appropriated. Each is a frame from which this family may or may not draw, depending on their own roots. Lineage is named; depth of practice belongs to those within the traditions.

**Jewish tradition.** In Jewish mourning practice, the Kaddish — a prayer of praise containing no mention of death — is recited by mourners in the presence of a community (a *minyan*). It is significant that the Kaddish is witnessed; grief is not carried alone. Halakhic tradition distinguishes what is asked of children by age; the young child is exempt from formal obligations but is included in presence, meals, and stories. The principle relevant here is **communal witness**: the child does not have to know what to say. The community holds the words around the child.

**Buddhist traditions.** Teachings on impermanence (*anicca* in Pali, *anitya* in Sanskrit) are often introduced to children through simple, concrete practices — attending to a falling leaf, watching a candle, noticing a changing cloud. The teaching is not *nothing lasts, be sad*; it is *things change, and love is still here while they are changing, and love is still here after*. Thich Nhat Hanh's writings for and with children (e.g., Nhat Hanh, 2002) offer developmentally gentle frames for impermanence that do not frighten. [verification needed: exact title and year of the Nhat Hanh child-oriented text being cited.]

**Latin American traditions — Día de los Muertos.** In Mexican tradition and its diaspora, Día de los Muertos explicitly includes children in an ongoing relationship with those who have died or are otherwise beyond everyday reach. Altars (*ofrendas*), photographs, favored foods, marigolds, and stories keep the relationship in active circulation. The tradition offers a frame — relevant even when the parent is still living — in which presence is not binary. A person can be present in a photograph, a food, a story, a song. [Note: this practice belongs to specific communities; any adaptation by this family should be grounded in their own cultural relationship to the tradition.]

**Celtic and Irish traditions.** In traditional Irish wake practice, children were historically present — not shielded from the body of the deceased, not excluded from the ritual meal, not sent away while the adults wept. The wake's long night of stories, food, music, and tears was a communal holding in which children participated as children. The relevant principle is **inclusion without pretense**: children are not asked to grieve like adults, but they are not asked to be absent either. [verification needed for specific ethnographic source — Ó Crualaoich or similar Irish folklore scholar.]

**Additional traditions held open.** African philosophies of *Ubuntu* ("I am because we are") frame the child as held by a community, not a nuclear unit alone — a reminder that the mother should not be the only adult the children have. Many Indigenous traditions across the world carry storytelling-as-lineage practices, often tightly woven with land and specific kinship; these are named here with respect and without extraction. Where this family has ancestral roots in any of these traditions, the roots are a resource; where they do not, the principles can still inform without being taken.

The common thread across all these traditions is this: **children are not protected by being excluded from loss. They are protected by being included in a structure larger than the grief, held by adults who are themselves held.**

## 7. Implications for This Specific Family

The following are concrete implications drawn from the foregoing. They are offered as starting points for protocols, not as prescriptions.

1. **Differentiate by child.** The 5-year-old needs body, ritual, and concrete presence. The 8-year-old needs honest words, a private interior channel, and explicit permission not to be a second parent. One protocol cannot serve both children.

2. **Answer "why" questions briefly and truthfully.** To the 5-year-old: *Daddy's brain works in a different way now. It's not because of you. It's not something you can catch. It's not something you can fix. He still loves you.* To the 8-year-old, the same truths in slightly fuller form, and: *Nobody knows exactly what will happen. We're going to take care of each other.* Once. Calmly. Repeated on request, not volunteered anxiously.

3. **Bracket every supervised visit with ritual.** A before-visit ritual (choose what to bring, name one thing you hope to do) and an after-visit ritual (a walk, a snack, a drawing, a debrief). This gives the nervous system a shape for the visit and a way out of it.

4. **Prepare without promising.** Before a visit: *Daddy may be different today than last time. We'll see how he is when we get there. If something feels hard, you can tell the supervisor or me and we can take a break.* No promises about what the father will do or remember.

5. **Debrief without interrogating.** After a visit: *How was your heart today? What do you want to draw? Do you want to be quiet?* Receive whatever comes. Do not correct the child's perceptions. A child who says "Daddy was weird today" is reporting accurately from inside a five- or eight-year-old body and should not be talked out of the report.

6. **Build connection-capsules without conscripting the father.** Start small and low-stakes. A voice recording of a bedtime story, made on a good day. A sentence the father wants to say, written down by someone else. Photographs of ordinary moments. If a given day does not have the capacity for the capsule, the capsule waits. The father is never asked to perform his own condition.

7. **Honor distinct emotional styles.** The 5-year-old's grief will mostly be body and play. The 8-year-old's grief will be more verbal and more private. Neither is more legitimate. The mother should expect to see both — sometimes in the same child in the same hour — and should not try to make them look alike.

8. **Watch, do not pathologize.** Regression, sleep disruption, somatic complaints, clinginess, and hypervigilance are within the normal range of responses to this situation. They are messages, not symptoms. What warrants additional support is: *persistence* of distress over weeks without any good moments, *withdrawal* from previously loved activities, *expressions of self-blame* that do not ease with reassurance, *school functioning* that significantly changes, or the *parentification* of the 8-year-old hardening into chronic caretaking. These are signs to loop in more help — a pediatrician, a child therapist — not emergencies, but signs.

9. **The mother's role is to be held, too.** The most evidence-supported protection for the children is a mother whose own nervous system is regulated and whose own story about what is happening is coherent (Main et al., 1985). This is not a demand on her to be fine. It is a recognition that her care is a load-bearing wall, and load-bearing walls require reinforcement. Her grief, her rest, her support network, and her permission to feel her own mixed feelings in front of the children (*within reason, without conscripting them as comforters*) are part of the children's protection.

10. **The supervisor is not a guard; she is a witness.** The role, if it can be held that way, is to be a regulated adult presence whose job is to make the room safe enough for whatever form the father-child relationship takes that day. A witness who can notice and gently redirect, who is not competing with the parents for meaning-making, and who can hold the children's affect without absorbing or amplifying it.

---

## Closing Note

Two children will carry this into their adulthoods. What this chapter has tried to offer — through attachment theory, developmental differentiation, expressive and narrative practice, ritual, connection-capsules, and traditional wisdoms — is a set of resources from which the mother, the supervisor, and anyone else in the holding circle may draw. None of it cures anything. Some of it may help. The measure, always, is the two actual children in the actual rooms, on actual Tuesdays, after actual visits.

The walk is the point. The children will be held by the walk, not by any single practice within it.
